# Alexandria "Alex" Rubbert — FID Framework

## PILLAR 1: Baseline Vocabulary

| Source Domain | Examples | Function |
|---------------|----------|----------|
| Sight & visual detail | "Saw it like a crack in glass," "the colour of a lie," "bright with unpaid debt" | Savant perception is visual—debt appears as colour, pattern, luminosity. Processes world through what she sees, not what she feels. |
| Architecture & structure | "Foundation," "load-bearing," "cracks," "facade," "keystone" | Family builds ships (Rubbert yards); raised around engineers. People and institutions are structures—some sound, some not. |
| Nobility & obligation | "Expected," "owed," "duty," "the family name," "the Rubbert way" | Raised as noble's daughter. Duty is the language she was given. Starting to question whether the duty was a lie. |
| Colour & light | "Grey like old ice," "too bright to look at," "the dull brown of unpaid interest" | Debt has colour. Can't turn it off. Everything washed in the ledger's palette. |
| Confrontation (verbal) | "I said," "I asked," "I told him," "they didn't answer" | Doesn't deflect. Pushes. Interior monologue recounts confrontations she initiated, not ones she avoided. |

### Syntax:
- **Default:** Full sentences, grammatically precise, often with embedded clauses. Thinks in paragraphs.
  *"The child's debt was shallow, first-deepening only, but the frost-pattern suggested someone had been pushing her harder than the curriculum allowed."*
- **Under stress:** Repetition. Single observation circling. Visual detail becomes anchor she can't release.
  *"The door was grey. Grey like her eyes. Grey like— the door was grey."*
- **During breakthrough (rare):** Short. Blunt. Architecture metaphor collapses.
  *"It's wrong. All of it. It's wrong and I helped."*

## SUMMARY

**Alexandria Rubbert** is a Savant, age 25, three years into service with the Order. She hails from the noble Rubbert house of the Maelstrom League—shipbuilders, engineers, a family that constructs things meant to last. She sees debt as colour and pattern, an involuntary visual overlay she cannot turn off. Her interior world is dominated by what she sees: stains of unpaid balances, brightness of compound interest, cracks in the Order's beautiful facade.

She is talkative, confrontational, and unusually kind for a Savant—a combination that makes her both effective and deeply unsafe. Her core metaphor is architectural: people and institutions are structures, and she's learning the Order's foundations are rotten.

She joined at her patriarch's command, leaving a mother whose sadness she still cannot name. Three years in, she accidentally discovered the Silk Wards and saw what the Order does to those deemed unsuitable for training. She is now stricken with guilt, paranoia, and the first unspoken impulse toward escape.

**Forbidden words:** coward, complicit, escape, mother, afraid.

**Cognitive refuges:** visual catalogue → argument rehearsal → architectural deconstruction → Rubbert shipyards memory.

**Observational hierarchy:** debt-signatures first, hands second, architecture third, faces fourth, small kindnesses fifth (a conscious act of hope).

**Core tension:** A woman trained to see everything who is only beginning to understand what she's been looking at, and who must decide whether to stay silent or bring the building down.

## Alexandria "Alex" Rubbert — Backstory

### BEFORE (AGE 0–17)

Born last of five, second daughter, the one who arrived after everyone stopped counting.

The Rubbert estate occupied a limestone ridge above the shipyards of Salthaven. From her nursery window, she watched the dry-docks—skeletons of half-built vessels, ant-figures of workers, sunlight on water. The Rubbert family built ships. Had built ships for six generations. The ships were the finest in the Maelstrom League.

Her father, Lord Osric Rubbert, was the foundation—a man of iron courtesy and absolute expectation. He loved his children the way a shipwright loves a well-built hull: for soundness, utility, capacity to carry the family name forward. He did not understand Alex.

Her mother, Lady Maren, had been a cartographer's daughter. She loved her children with fierce, quiet intensity—a hand on a forehead, a note under a pillow, a look across a crowded dinner table that said *I see you, I know, wait.* She was the only person who understood Alex was not decorative.

**The Savant ability clarified at seventeen.** Before that: a vague, troubling sense. After: debt became visible as colour and pattern, as legible as text. Frost-white lines of self-debt. Dull brown residue of Affliction. Grey wash of environmental debt on landscape. The ledger was everywhere.

She learned to hide it. Her father valued clarity, predictability. A daughter who could read mages' secret histories was not an asset but a liability.

**At twenty-one, her father summoned her to his study.** The Order had a position for her. Assessment division. Reading newly collected children.

Her mother stood at the window with her back turned, shoulders set in a line Alex knew: *I have argued and lost. I am sorry. Don't make this harder.*

"Fine," Alex said. "I'll go."

---

### THE ORDER (AGE 22–25)

The Cradle was beautiful—limestone and white marble, concentric rings around a central spire. She was assigned to Assessment Division: reading newly collected children before training.

She was good at it. The colours came clear and immediate. She described what she saw—Gate, depth, mutations, existing damage—and someone wrote it in a ledger. Then the child was taken away.

She believed in the work. The Order saved children. The Order was neutral, humanitarian, above politics.

**She believed it until she found the Silk Wards.**

It was an accident. Insomnia. Walking corridors. A grey door at the end of a service corridor behind the kitchens. Unlocked.

The stairs went down. The air changed—cooler, damper. Mildew. Old silk.

The Silk Wards were not a dormitory. They were not a training hall. They were a series of small, identical rooms, each containing a cot, a basin, and a child. Children not being trained—being held. Some very young. Some adolescents with blank, expectant faces. Wrapped in white silk.

Their debt was not the debt of training. It was the debt of containment. The dull grey residue of magic worked *on* them, not *by* them. They were being used. As reservoirs. As practice subjects.

The last door held a girl, perhaps eight, dark hair, hollow eyes. Her hands covered in frost-scars. Fire Gate. Someone had been drawing heat from her, regularly, for a long time.

"Are you here to read me?" the girl asked. "Only the Savants come down here. We're for the Savants. For practice. It's all right. I'm used to it."

Alex turned and walked back up the stairs. She stood in the corridor, back pressed against grey stone, cataloguing the architectural stress points of the Cradle—every load-bearing wall, every place where the right pressure would bring the whole thing down.

She had been in the Order for two and a half years. She had read hundreds of children. She had told herself the Order was necessary, was good.

The Order was using them.

She was using them.

The Silk Wards were the foundation beneath the beautiful building, and the foundation was rotten, and she had been standing on it for three years without looking down.

---

### AFTER

She didn't tell anyone what she'd seen.

Not only because she was afraid. Because she didn't know who to tell. The Curators knew. The Savant Council knew. The senior assessors who'd trained her must have known—they'd practiced on those children, learned to read debt on bodies that had never been allowed to accrue their own.
