# Chapter 13: The Lesson

Morning. Mist rising from the grass like breath from a cold mouth. The clearing behind the cottage was flat and open, the ground worn bare by feet, the scrub pushed back in a rough circle. A training ground. Not the Order's kind—no grey stone, no scorched walls, no instructor with clean hands and a flat voice saying *again*. Just grass and mist and a woman with grey eyes teaching a girl how to shape water.

Hark stood at the edge of the clearing. He hadn't planned to stay. He'd planned to leave before dawn, before anyone woke, before the grey light crept through the window and Marta put another bowl in front of him. But his feet had carried him here instead of to the path, and now the lesson was underway, and he was watching.

The girl was young. Seventeen, maybe. Dark skin, short hair, hands that shook when she reached for the rain barrel. She'd been collected late—Hark could tell by the way she moved, the flinch still in her shoulders, the way her eyes tracked Renne's hands like she was expecting a blow. She'd been with the Order long enough to learn fear. Not long enough to learn guarding. Her reservoir was open, unsteady, the water in the barrel sloshing without being called.

"You're forcing it," Renne said. Her voice was low, patient, the voice of someone who'd said the same words a hundred times and would say them a hundred more. "Water doesn't respond to force. It responds to invitation."

The girl tried again. The water rose from the barrel—a thin thread, wobbling—and collapsed before it reached her palm. She made a sound of frustration. Her hands dropped to her sides. "I can't."

"You can. You're trying to command it. Don't command. Ask."

"That doesn't make sense."

"It will." Renne stepped closer. Her hands were moving—not shaping, just moving, the same restless motion Hark had been watching for two days. "The Order teaches you to grab. To pull. To treat your element like a tool you're forcing into shape. That works. For a while. But it costs more than it needs to. Every time you force water, you pay a debt you don't have to pay. If you invite it instead—" She opened her palm. Water rose from the barrel, smooth and effortless, and pooled in her hand. No wobble. No collapse. The vortex formed a moment later, silver and silent, spinning in the morning light.

"Invite it," the girl repeated. "Like it's a guest."

"Like it's a partner. Water wants to move. It always wants to move. You're not making it do anything. You're giving it a place to go." Renne let the water fall. It splashed into the grass, ordinary again. "Try. And this time, don't pull. Just open your hand and wait."

The girl tried. The water wobbled. Held. Collapsed. But it lasted longer this time. A second. Maybe two. She looked at her palm like it had betrayed her. "It's not working."

"It's working. You're just not patient."

"I've been patient for three weeks."

"Three weeks is nothing. I've been learning for ten years." Renne's voice was still patient, still low. "You'll get it. Or you won't. Either way, forcing it won't help."

The girl tried again. The water rose. The vortex spun—ragged, unsteady, but spinning. She laughed. The sound was bright and surprised and quickly stifled, the way Lea's laugh had been, in the dormitory dark. Hark's chest tightened. He didn't name the feeling. He never named it.

The instructor had said: "Fire responds to anger."

He'd been ten, standing in the training chamber, his hands wrapped in silk. The silk was white and clean and didn't chafe, and he'd hated it with a ferocity that surprised him. The instructor was behind him—he couldn't see the man's hands, but he knew they were clean, knew they were steady, knew they wouldn't move no matter what he did.

"You're angry," the instructor said. "Good. Use it."

Hark had tried. He'd reached for his internal reservoir—the furnace with the door still missing, heat bleeding out of him constantly—and pulled. The flame in his palm had sputtered. Guttered. Died. The cold had crept into his fingers, the frost writing new lines, the debt tallying itself.

"Not angry enough. Think of something that makes you furious."

He'd thought of the carriage. The handler's clean hands, folded on grey sleeves, motionless. The silk around his wrists. The window set too high to see out of. His mother's face as the door closed—her hand reaching, shaking, falling away.

The flame had roared. It had filled his palm, orange and white, the heat so intense he couldn't feel the cold anymore. The frost on his knuckles had melted and refrozen instantly, the debt compounding, his body paying interest on a loan he'd never agreed to.

"Good," the instructor said. "Again."

He'd learned that anger was fuel. Rage raised heat. Heat raised power. A Fire mage who could harness his emotions cast hotter and longer than one who couldn't. He'd learned to reach for the anger whenever he needed to cast—the carriage, the handler, the silk, his mother's face, the way the neighbours had watched and done nothing. The rage was always there. Banked. Waiting. He'd fed it to the flame for three years, and the flame had never failed him.

He had not learned any other way. Not yet.

The lesson ended. The girl walked back toward the cottage, her hands still shaking but her shoulders looser, the vortex still spinning in her memory. Renne stood in the clearing for a moment, watching the mist lift, her hands still moving at her sides. Then she walked over to where Hark stood at the edge of the trees.

"You're staring again."

"You're interesting when you're not trying to be."

She raised an eyebrow. "Was that a compliment."

"It was an observation."

"From you, that's the same thing."

He didn't answer. She didn't push. They stood at the edge of the clearing, the mist burning off, the morning warming, and the silence between them was easier than it should have been. He'd known her for two days. He'd spoken more words to her than he'd spoken to anyone since Joel. The words kept coming, and he kept letting them, and he didn't know why.

"Invitation," he said. "Water responds to invitation. Fire doesn't do that."

"Doesn't it?"

"Fire burns. Fire consumes. You don't invite fire. You light it and get out of the way."

"That's what the Order taught you."

"That's what fire does."

She looked at him for a long moment. Her grey eyes were steady, unblinking. Then she held out her hand. Water pooled in her palm—from the air, from the mist still clinging to the grass, from somewhere he couldn't see. The vortex formed. Small. Silver. Spinning.

"Try."

He shook his head. "I'm Fire."

"I know. Try anyway."

He reached out. His gloved fingers hovered over the water. He didn't touch. The water shivered—a faint ripple, a tremor that ran from the edge of the vortex to the centre and back. The spinning slowed. Then steadied.

"You're warm," she said. "Even through the gloves."

He pulled his hand back. "That's not magic. That's just my body."

"Same thing." She let the water fall. "Invitation."

He stood in the clearing after she walked away. The mist was gone. The sun was up. The girl's laugh was still echoing somewhere in the back of his mind, and the vortex was still spinning behind his eyes, and his hand was still warm where the water had shivered. He flexed his fingers. The leather creaked. The cold was still there, but it felt different. Or he felt different. Or nothing was different and he was just tired.

He didn't sleep in the cottage that night.

He walked back to the village. Down the path through the wind-twisted trees, past the fork where he'd turned left instead of right, past the rock where the child had been fishing. The child wasn't there. The rock was empty. The village was quiet in the late afternoon, the boats pulled up on the shingle, the nets drying in the sun.

The ferry was at the dock. The ferryman with one eye was loading crates onto the deck, his dog watching from the bow. He looked up when Hark approached. "Heading back?"

"Yes."

"Thought you might stay longer. Most people do, once they find what they're looking for."

Hark didn't answer. He boarded the ferry. Paid his coin. Stood at the rail and watched the island shrink behind him—the green hills, the wind-twisted trees, the thin line of smoke rising from the cottage chimney. The dog came and sat beside him. Didn't ask questions. Dogs were good at that.

The crossing was calm. The sea was flat, grey, indifferent. His stomach didn't protest. His hands stayed in his pockets. The splinter was still there, but it had changed shape. He didn't know how. He didn't want to know.

Salthaven was the same. The harbour smell. The screaming gulls. The ropewalk stairs, dark and narrow, the wood groaning under his boots. The door at the top was locked. He unlocked it. Stepped inside. Locked it behind him. Checked the lock twice.

The room was the same. Bed. Chair. Washbasin. The salt-crusted window catching the last of the evening light. He lay on the cot and stared at the ceiling—crack east to west, water stain shaped like a hand—and didn't sleep.

He was trying his best not to feel anything. He was failing. The feeling was there, a knot in his chest that had nothing to do with his gate. He didn't name it. He never named it. But it was there, and it was growing, and the splinter was a door, and the door was open, and somewhere in the hills above the sea a woman with grey eyes was teaching a girl to invite water instead of forcing it.

*Invitation.*

He flexed his fingers. The leather creaked. The cold was still there. But the warmth was too—his own body heat, bleeding through the gloves, ordinary and steady and asking nothing in return.

He stared at the ceiling until the grey light came. The day arrived. The feeling didn't leave.
