# Chapter 4: Ogre

Three. Firelight deeper in, orange smear on wet stone. The cave throat opened into a chamber, low-ceilinged, the walls sweating. Stalactites dripped slow water into pools that reflected nothing. The smell was a physical thing—offal and old blood and the particular musk of things that slept in their own waste.

Hark counted. One ogre near the fire, squatting, its back to him. A second to the left, sprawled on a pile of something that might have been bedding or might have been bones. The third was further back, half in shadow, its breathing slow and wet. Sleeping. Or close enough.

Element of surprise. He did not have numbers. Numbers would have been nice. Numbers would have been a contract with better pay. He had a crossbow, a sword, three smoke pellets, and a coil of wire still in his pack. The wire wouldn't help here. The crossbow would.

He raised it.

The first ogre died before it saw him. The bolt took it through the throat—soft tissue under the jaw, the same shot he'd used a dozen times before. It clutched at the wound, thick fingers clawing at the shaft, and a gurgle came out of its mouth that might have been a roar. Not loud enough. Not fast enough. Hark was already moving, sword drawn, closing the distance before the body hit the ground.

The second ogre turned. Too slow. They were always too slow. He was inside its reach—past the long arms, past the swinging fists—before it could raise the club propped against the wall. The blade found the soft tissue beneath the arm. In. Out. A handspan of steel through the axillary artery, the place where the blood ran close to the surface. Joel had taught him that. *"Armpits and groins and throats. The rest is just bone and attitude."*

Step back. The ogre clutched at its side, fingers coming away black in the firelight. It took one step, two, then its knee buckled and it went down face-first. The ground shook. Water rippled in the pools. Stalactites trembled.

The third ogre roared.

Hark's inventory flashed. Smoke pellet. He pulled it from the belt pouch, thumbed the clay sphere, threw. It shattered against the cave floor and the chamber filled with grey—saltpetre and kelp, thick and bitter and blinding. The ogre swung its club through the smoke. Blind. Wild. A blow that would have caved in his ribs if he'd been where it thought he was.

He wasn't. He'd moved left, shallow breathing, circling. The smoke was his element. He'd trained in it. Joel had made him train in it, blindfolded, until he could navigate by sound and slope and the memory of where the walls were. The ogre swung again. He stepped inside the arc, behind it, and put the sword through its kidney.

Twist. The ogre screamed. The sound was huge in the confined space, bouncing off the walls, echoing down into the deeper dark. Hark pulled the blade free and stepped back. The ogre fell. The screaming stopped. The smoke began to clear.

Three. Job done. Contract complete. He'd take the ears—left ear from each, the guild's proof of kill—and walk back to Salthaven with a sack of salted cartilage and a pay chit that wasn't worth the work. Standard. Boring. The kind of job that left him cold but not colder.

The handler's crossbow had been loaded when Lea fell.

Hark remembered the sound. A sharp crack, then nothing. Lea had made no sound at all. Just her grey eyes going wide, the bolt through her chest, the air pressure collapsing around him like a held breath released. The handler stood behind the instructor, crossbow raised, his hands steady the way they'd always been steady. Clean hands. Steady hands. The bolt had taken her through the heart, and she'd gone down without a word, and the only sound was the crack of the crossbow and the thud of her body hitting the forest floor.

He had not cried. He had screamed. He had opened his gate and screamed light—pure, white, searing—and the handler's eyes had gone wide in the instant before the beam took him. He didn't remember pulling the trigger on his own crossbow later. He remembered the crack. He would always remember the crack.

He blinked. The smoke was thinning. The cave was quiet.

Four ogres. Not three.

The fourth emerged from the shadows at the back of the chamber—larger than the others, older, its hide crosshatched with scars. Old wounds. Old fights. This one had survived things that should have killed it. Its eyes were small and clever in the firelight, and they found Hark without hesitation.

Inventory. Sword—dulled, the edge rolled from the last kidney strike. Crossbow—empty, the bolt still lodged in the first ogre's throat. Smoke—expended, the last pellet shattered on the floor. Wire—in the pack, coiled tight, not deployed. Magic—reserved. Always reserved. The gate in his chest was a door he kept locked, and the key was fifteen years of running, and he didn't turn it for anything less than dying.

The ogre took a step forward. The ground shook. Water splashed. Hark calculated. He had thirty seconds before it closed the distance. He used fifteen of them standing still.

Heat-theft. Not fire. Not light. The base state, stripped to its meanest form—drawing warmth directly from a living body and giving nothing back. It was Affliction in all but name, the same working that Dominion inquisitors used to extract confessions, and it left the same mark. The mage took heat. The target died cold.

He didn't need much. The ogre was warm—blood-warm, fire-warm, the heat of a body that had been sleeping next to a flame. Hark closed the distance. The ogre swung. He ducked under the arm, put his bare hand against its chest. The glove was off. He didn't remember pulling it off.

The heat came.

It flowed into him like water into dry ground, a rush of warmth that lit up his internal reservoir and pushed back the permanent cold in his fingers. For one second, two, he could feel temperature again. The heat of the ogre's body. The heat of the cave. The heat of his own blood, moving, alive.

Then it stopped. The ogre's eyes went wide—confused, stupid, not understanding why it was suddenly cold in a cave with a fire burning. Its skin greyed. Frost spread across its chest in a thin white film, the same frost that lived in Hark's own hands, the same debt written in a different ledger. It opened its mouth. No sound came out. Its heart stopped. It fell.

Hark stood over it. His fingers were cold again. Colder than before. The debt had settled, and the settling was a weight in his bones, a new white line threading across his knuckles, a numbness that reached further up his wrist than it had an hour ago. He pulled the glove back on. The leather was stiff. His hands were shaking. He made them stop.

Four ogres. Not three. The contract was wrong. The pay was still light. Graen would argue about the extra ear, claim the clause didn't cover it, try to short him again. The argument was tomorrow's problem. The ears were today's.

He took them. Left ear from each, a sharp knife and a steady hand. The salt was in his pack, coarse grey crystals that stung the cracks in his gloves. He packed the ears in salt, wrapped the sack tight, tied it to his belt. The smell followed him. It always followed him.

The cave was quiet. The fire was dying. The bodies were already cooling, the frost on the fourth ogre's chest melting into ordinary water that dripped onto the stone. Hark cleaned his sword on the bedding—bone pile, whatever—and sheathed it. He did not look back. He never looked back.

The coastal trail was grey with afternoon light. The sea was still hungry. The gulls were still screaming. He walked toward Salthaven with a sack of salted ears and a cold that had settled deeper into his fingers, and the ledger in his flesh had a new entry, and the contract was complete.

Four ogres. The pay was for three. The extra ear wouldn't buy new gloves. Tomorrow's problem.

The cold was already there, waiting.
