# Chapter 15 – The Grain-Lords' Confederacy

The farm looked smaller than he remembered. That was the first thing wrong.

He walked the last mile through the Confederacy's black soil, the sky enormous above him, the horizon a flat line of grey-green that he didn't look at. His body was a catalogue of damage. Left arm bound in a torn sleeve, the new scars hidden but throbbing with every step. Ribs wrapped tight under his coat, the cracked bones pulling on each inhale. Jaw still clicking from the Iron Rib woman's fist. He had been walking for five weeks and it showed in the way he carried his weight, favouring his right side, the old shoulder injury from the alley a low constant beneath the newer hurts.

Joel had a rule about coming back. If you walk onto a farm twice, you owe the farmer something. First time's business. Second time's a relationship.

Hark had said he didn't have relationships.

Joel had said, Then pay your debts and get out.

He'd been right about most things.

The farm appeared at the end of the rutted track. The fields were still grey, leached of colour—the Soil Savant's debt still bleeding into the land. The barn still slumped at one corner. The axe was still embedded in the stump, rusted along the blade. Nothing had changed. That was the second thing wrong.

Elias stepped out of the barn. He looked older than five weeks should account for. His knuckles were more swollen, the joints distended, the bone-brittleness advancing. He had been working the land harder, or the stress of the network's collapse was accelerating his debt, or both. His eyes moved over Hark—the torn sleeve, the favouring of the right side, the way he held his left arm slightly away from his body.

"You're still alive."

"Mostly."

Elias gestured inside. They sat at the same kitchen table, Hark with his back to the wall, one door and two windows counted. The wooden box was still on the shelf—the one with the clay bird and the folded ledger page. Neither of them looked at it.

Elias reached for Hark's left arm. Hark let him. A Savant's reading was information, and he needed to know what Vyktor might have seen. Elias pushed back the torn sleeve. The new scars were livid—branching frost-lines, wider than the old internal-use marks, threading from knuckles to mid-forearm. Environmental-draw scars. The pattern was unmistakable.

"You used the Gate."

"Twice."

Elias traced a line without touching it. His swollen finger hovered over a jagged branch that ran toward Hark's wrist. "Environmental draws. Desert stone, by the pattern. The debt is fresh. Weeks, not months." He moved his finger to a second line—wider, more diffuse, the edges blurred. "This one's different. Unfocused. A burst, not a draw. You threw heat at something."

"Someone."

"Did they die?"

"One did. The other walked away."

Elias was quiet for a moment. He turned Hark's arm slightly, examining the scars in the grey light from the window. "The one who walked away—Storm Gate? The scars have a charge-residue. Faint, but it's there. He clipped you with something."

Hark didn't answer. The cracked ribs were answer enough.

"You're not clean. If the Storm-mage survived, he can read these scars the same way I can. He knows what you are now."

"He already knew. He tracked me across the desert. He won't report."

"Why?"

"Because I burned him and he walked away. That's not something you put in a file."

Elias nodded slowly. He was old enough to understand pride, and shame, and the kind of silence that became a grudge. He released Hark's arm and sat back.

Hark gave him the countersurveillance report—stripped of detail, transactional. He crossed the Hold-Kingdoms. A clean-knuckled guard searched his pack at the mountain shelter. A lord on the western slope was feeding information to a Dominion contact. Two bounty hunters tried to take him at the lord's hall. Dead. He surfaced in the Dominion, moved visibly, drew attention. Team Four found him in the desert. Rask dead. Vyktor burned and not reporting. He came back on a different road through a different caravan network. No tail.

Elias listened without interrupting. When Hark finished, he said: "Five weeks."

"Longer than it needed to be."

"Was it clean?"

"Clean enough. If anyone was watching, they're gone or they lost me in the desert."

"And the Storm-mage?"

"Personal. Not professional. He'll come for me, not for her."

Elias accepted this. He didn't ask if Hark was sure. He was old enough to know the difference between a mission and a vendetta.

"The network's been hit," Elias said. "Team Five destroyed a waystation three days ago. Marta's cousin is dead. Children dead or taken. Team Two is closing from the east—rumour says the Blood-mage went rogue, killed his Silencer. Unconfirmed. Team Three is somewhere south, asking questions. Team Four—you already know. Team One—" He paused.

"What about Team One?"

"No one's seen them. No ash, no blood, no storm, no ledger inquiries. They're either not here yet, or they're better than the others."

Bad odds. Catalogued.

"Renne is still at the farmhouse," Elias said. "Two days east, near the old-growth forest. She moved once since you left, but not far. The eastern path is clear. I've altered the records."

Hark reached into his inner coat pocket. The paper was still there—Elias's original message, folded, unread for five weeks. He placed it on the table between them.

Elias looked at the paper. Then at Hark. "You never opened it."

"Didn't need to. Knew where it said to go."

"But you didn't go."

Hark didn't answer. The tactical reason was real—countersurveillance, the Pragmatic Retreat, the long way wrong to shake the tail. The other reason was still locked in the drawer.

Elias unfolded the paper. Read his own handwriting. Read the updated location he had just given verbally. Nodded. Slid the paper back.

"You're going now."

"Yes."

"What changed?"

"The path is clean."

Elias didn't ask the other question. He was old enough to know that some questions didn't get answers.


Sixteen years ago. A ravine outside a town called Ketterhollow, where the Crown of Winter's foothills gave way to the Grain-Lords' plains. A narrow cut in the earth, steam rising from a hot spring trickle that threaded through the rocks. The smell of wet stone and minerals. A boy half-frozen on the bank, feverish, his clothes torn, his skin steaming in the cold air. He was unconscious when she found him—his hands already scarred, frost-lines on his knuckles, new then, still forming, white on reddened skin. His internal reservoir was bleeding heat through a hairline fracture in his guarding. He hadn't noticed. Or he was past caring.

Renne was sixteen. She had been free of the Order for almost two years, living rough, healing where she could, running a fledgling network for Sensitive children. Her hands were already scarred from the early healings—thin white lines on her fingers, the first entries in a ledger that would span decades. She pulled the boy out of the ravine, dragged him to a cottage a quarter-mile away, and waited for him to wake.

When he opened his eyes, he looked at her like she was a trap and he was too tired to care.

She didn't ask his name. She didn't ask where he came from. She set water to boil and began tending his wounds—the healing sword-cut on his forearm, the old knife nicks, the frost-scars. Her hands were gentle, clinical. She turned his palms over, examined the damage.

"This is old," she said, tracing the frost-lines. "Years old. Magic debt?"

He nodded.

"Fire Gate?"

Another nod.

"You don't talk."

He shook his head.

"That's fine. I talk enough for both of us."

She fed him. She gave him the bed and took the root cellar for herself. She asked nothing in return.

Days became weeks. He told himself he was resting. Healing. His body needed it—he had been running for five years, since the night Lea died. But his body had needed rest before, and he had never stayed.

He stayed because she didn't push.

She talked. He listened. She told him about the Order—how she escaped at fifteen, how she had been running her network for almost two years. How she took in Sensitive children, taught them to hide. "I don't heal with magic unless I have to. Every working leaves a scar." She held up her hands—the bandages were off now. Fine white lines crisscrossed her palms and knuckles. "Seventy-three workings since I escaped. I remember every one."

"How many?"

She looked up. Startled—not by the question, but by the sound of his voice. It was the first word he had spoken since the ravine, weeks into their stay, rough from disuse.

"Seventy-three," she said. Then she smiled—a small thing, barely a curve of her lips. "Seventy-four. I'm counting this one."

His chest ached. He looked away. Counted the exits. One door. Two windows. The hearth was not an exit, but the flue might be.

She didn't push.

He picked the flower during the third month—a star-thistle from the ravine's edge, grey-blue, tough, drought-resistant. The kind of flower that grew where nothing else would. He pressed it flat between the pages of her herbalism book, the one with the yellowed paper and the cracked spine. He hid the book under his side of the bed. He checked it every few days to make sure the flower was still there.

She taught him to skip stones in the hot spring pools. He was terrible at it—all wrist and no patience, the rocks sinking every time. She laughed, and the sound was something he didn't have a word for.

He taught her to guard her reservoir, the Cradle technique, the one that had kept him alive. She asked about the scars on his hands. He told her the ledger. She asked if it hurt. He said always.

They shared meals and silences and a bed that was too small for two people who didn't know how to sleep next to someone else.

The last night. A courier had arrived earlier—a woman in a grey cloak, face hidden, voice low. An Order patrol was three days north. Not hunting Renne specifically—not yet—but sweeping through the Confederacy, scanning for unregistered debt. They would find the cottage. They would find her.

After the courier left, Renne sat at the table. Her hands were trembling. "I have to move. The safehouse isn't safe anymore."

Hark nodded.

"You should go. Your face is known in the Confederacy. If someone sees you with me—"

"I know."

She looked at him. Her eyes were wet. "This isn't goodbye. It can't be. I won't let it be."

He wanted to say something. The words were there. I want to stay. I want to come with you. I want—

His chest tightened. His jaw clenched. The word never reached his mouth. It died in his throat, a shape without sound. His hands curled into fists at his sides. Renne watched his face. She saw the struggle, the way the silence was not emptiness but pressure.

He reached into his coat. He pulled out the flower—dried now, pressed flat, grey-blue against his scarred palm. He held it out.

She took it. Her fingers brushed his. Her hands were warm.

"You kept this."

He nodded.

"You kept it for months."

Another nod.

"You're not allowed to want. But you kept it anyway."

He didn't answer.

She put the flower in her pocket. She stood. She kissed his cheek—a brief, soft press of her lips against his jaw. "The river remembers," she said.

Then she was gone. Into the night, into the network, into the work that would define the rest of her life.

He stood in the empty cottage for a long time. Then he walked to the ravine. He knelt beside the hot spring trickle—the same spot where she had found him. He didn't cry. He hadn't cried since Lea died.

He stayed until dawn.

Then he walked south.

He never looked back.


Back in the present, Elias hadn't moved. The wooden box was still on the shelf. He didn't take it down this time. He didn't need to.

Hark looked at the box. Then at Elias. "I told you I'd kill them for you. I've killed two. Rask. Calen. Ellera at the farm before I left. Vyktor will come for me, and I'll finish it."

"That's not why you're doing this."

"No."

"But you're going to do it anyway."

"Yes."

Elias nodded. He didn't say thank you. He didn't say good luck.

Hark stood. His body protested—ribs, jaw, left arm. He ignored it. At the door, he paused. The light on the barn's weathered wood caught him, a clean line of pale sun through cloud. He registered it for half a second. Then he looked away.

He touched his coat pocket. The new paper—the updated location—was next to the old one. The needle was still there. The flower was not.

"When you find her," Elias said, "tell her the eastern path is clear. I've altered the records."

Hark nodded once. Didn't say thank you. Didn't say goodbye. Walked east.

The track led east. Two days to the old-growth forest. Two days to Renne. He had been walking for five weeks to cover a distance that should have taken two days. The arithmetic was terrible. The path was clean.

He didn't think about the ravine. He didn't think about the almost. He walked, and counted miles, and didn't name the thing that had been pulling him east since the moment Kellis put the fragments on the tavern table.

Hark folded the map. "Thank you," he said. Elias nodded. Neither looked at the other's eyes.
