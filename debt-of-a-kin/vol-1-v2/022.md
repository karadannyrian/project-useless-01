# Chapter 22 – The Reunion

The farmhouse was dark but the door was open. That was wrong.

Hark half-carried Jax through the treeline on the eastern side of the valley, the side the watchers weren't watching. The storm covered his approach—rain sheeting sideways, wind tearing at the canopy, thunder swallowing the sound of his footsteps. The weather-scar was useful for something after all.

Jax was barely conscious, his weight a dead drag on Hark's shoulder. The sailor's broken ribs ground with every step. The gash on his side had soaked through the makeshift bandage, a dark stain spreading under his coat. Hark's own body was a catalogue of failures—ash in his lungs rattling with every breath, the burn on his arm seeping through the sleeve, frost-scars burning cold, fever climbing. The nosebleed from the pier had dried black on his upper lip.

The farmhouse resolved through the rain: dark, silent, the door standing open. Open door meant breach. Breach meant the Order got here first. He set Jax against a tree trunk, the sailor's head lolling, and drew his knife.

He moved through the doorway low and fast, knife leading. The kitchen was empty. The candle had burned out. The table was overturned. The root cellar door was open, a salt barrel rolled aside, the tunnel entrance exposed—signs of evacuation, not violence. No blood. No bodies.

Exits: the tunnel, narrow and unknown. The back window, shattered—storm damage, not forced entry. The front door, his entry point. Threats: none visible. What was missing: the children. The woman.

Then he saw her.

Renne sat at the kitchen table, upright, hands folded in her lap. A kitchen knife rested on the table in front of her. She hadn't moved. She'd been waiting for the hunters, and he was the one who walked through the door. Her eyes found his. His chest did something the rules forbid. He sheathed the knife.

"You should have stayed gone."

Her voice was steady. It sounded tired.

Hark opened his mouth. Nothing came. The wall was still there, and the word behind it—the word he couldn't say on the last night, the word he'd been walking toward for five weeks and fifteen years—would not come out. His jaw clenched. His hands curled at his sides.

Renne watched him struggle. She saw the same clenched jaw, the same pressure of a door held shut from the inside. "You're still not allowed to want, are you?"

He didn't answer.

She stood. Crossed the kitchen. Touched his face—her scarred fingers tracing the new lines around his eyes, the grey at his temples, the dried blood on his lip. "You look terrible."

"Had worse."

Her hand drifted down to his left arm. The sleeve was torn, the fabric singed from the pier. The scars were visible—the old frost-lines on his knuckles, the new environmental-draw marks branching toward his elbow, jagged and pale. She didn't speak. Her fingers traced the new lines, the way she'd traced the old ones in the cottage sixteen years ago. Hark didn't pull away.

---

Renne's hand dropped from his arm. She reached into her apron pocket and pulled out the flower.

It was crumbling. The grey-blue petals had gone brittle, the stem bare in places where the years had worn it thin. She'd carried it through rain and root cellars and sieges, and it was still here. As she drew it out, the edge of a folded paper showed in the pocket—charcoal-smudged, creased—and then the flower was in her hand and the paper was hidden again. Hark's eyes caught it. He didn't ask. She didn't offer.

"You gave this to me. On the last night. You couldn't say the word, but you gave me this."

Hark looked at the flower. Then at her. His hand rose—hesitant, the same hesitation she'd seen in the ravine, when he looked at her offered palm and didn't know if he was allowed to take it. He took the flower. His scarred fingers closed around the crumbling petals.

"You kept it for four months before you gave it to me. I kept it for fifteen years."

She didn't say I love you. She didn't say I waited. The numbers were enough.

A groan from outside. Hark's head turned—the tactical override cutting through the moment. Jax. Still propped against the tree. Still bleeding.

Renne followed his gaze. "Who?"

"Sailor. The Admiral left him to die. He didn't."

Renne moved past him, out into the rain. She knelt beside Jax, her hands already moving—checking his pulse, his pupils, the gash on his side. The tremor in her fingers steadied. This was what she did. This was what she'd always done.

"Help me get him inside."

Hark obeyed.

---

They carried Jax into the farmhouse and laid him on the kitchen table—the same table where Renne had stitched wounds through years of siege and flight. She worked quickly, cleaning the gash, threading a curved needle, closing the wound with steady hands. Hark watched. He didn't offer to help. He knew better.

His own body was failing—fever climbing, ash rattling, the burn on his arm seeping through the sleeve. He didn't mention it. Renne didn't ask. She could see it. She'd get to him when Jax was stable.

Jax, half-conscious, muttered something—a name. "Varro."

Renne's hands paused for half a beat. She didn't ask. She filed it.

When the last suture was tied and the wound was bandaged, she washed her hands in the basin—the water pink with Jax's blood. Then she turned to Hark.

She pressed her palm to his forehead. Her hand frosted—the healing debt taking payment, ice crystals forming on her knuckles, the familiar cold threading up her fingers. She pulled the fever out. Not all of it—that would kill her. Just enough to steady him. The tremor in his hands eased. The flush on his skin receded by a shade. Her breath clouded in the warm kitchen air. Hark watched her knuckles whiten with frost. He knew what it cost. He didn't stop her.

She withdrew her hand. Flexed her fingers. The ice melted, dripping onto the floor. "That's all I can take. The rest is yours."

He didn't thank her. She didn't need him to.

Hark moved to the shattered window. The storm was still building—wind circling, rain hammering sideways. Beyond the treeline, the watchers were still there. He could feel them—the same wrong stillness Renne had felt, the same quality of held breath.

"Three," he said. "Savants. Not fighters. They're waiting for something."

"Team Three. The Ledger-Keepers. They've been there since dusk." Renne's hands were dry now, the frost gone, the tremor returning. "Team Two is coming from the east. The Hammer. A Silencer and a leashed Blood-mage. They'll be here by dawn."

Hark counted. Team Five dead. Team Four dead. Team Three watching. Team Two approaching. Team One invisible. "The invisible team?"

"Still invisible."

He didn't say what they were both thinking.

Jax was stable. Renne moved to stand beside Hark at the window. Not touching. Just near. Her shoulder an inch from his. The same distance as the cottage hearth, sixteen years ago, when she leaned against him and he didn't pull away.

"The storm," she said. "It doesn't move right."

"It's mine. Vyktor left it. It follows me."

She didn't ask who Vyktor was. She could guess. She filed it.

"You came back," she said.

He didn't answer. But his hand—the one still holding the dried flower—moved slightly toward hers. Not touching. Just closer.

"The river remembers. Do you?"

His jaw clenched. His hand stopped an inch from hers. The word was there—the one he couldn't say on the last night, the one he'd been carrying for fifteen years, the one that would crack the rules wide open. He didn't say it. But his hand didn't pull away.

The storm howled outside. The watchers waited. Jax breathed. The flower crumbled between his fingers.

*The river remembers.*

She didn't say it aloud. She didn't need to.
