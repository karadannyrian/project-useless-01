# Chapter 18: The First Hunter

Dawn. Grey light seeping up from the horizon, the sea a flat sheet of pewter, the mist clinging to the grass like breath on cold glass. The pillar of light had faded hours ago, but its ghost was still burned into Hark's vision—a white line across the dark, a beacon that had said *here I am, come find me*. They had found him.

Three figures in the mist. The two in front were broad, armoured, moving with the deliberate weight of men who carried steel and knew how to use it. Half-plate. Crossbows raised. The stocks were braced against their shoulders, the bolts already slotted, the strings already cranked. Silencers. He'd known before he saw their faces. The way they walked—steady, unhurried, the walk of men who'd never been burned by a firebolt or thrown by a wave or frozen where they stood. Immune. The single greatest threat a mage could face, and there were two of them.

The woman behind them was worse.

She stepped through the mist like it parted for her. Grey tabard. Grey sleeves. Hands folded in front of her—clean, pale, motionless. The hands of someone who'd never had to dirty them. Her face was thin, severe, the bones sharp under the skin. Her eyes were pale and cold and looked through everything they touched. Through skin. Through bone. Through the fifteen years he'd spent building walls.

A Savant. He knew it before she spoke. The way her eyes went to his hands and stayed there. The way she didn't bother looking at his face. She was reading him. The ledger. The frost-scars. The Light mutation. Every working he'd ever done, written in white lines under his gloves, and she could see all of it. He couldn't hide. He had never been able to hide from a Savant. He had spent his life running from this moment.

It was here. He was still standing. That was something.

"Damian Whiters."

He flinched. The name hit him like a blade—unexpected, unguarded, the sound of it strange and wrong and painfully familiar. He had not heard it in fifteen years. He had left it beside Lea's body in the forest. He had walked away and not looked back, and no one had spoken it since. Until now.

"You're wrong," he said. His voice came out flat. "I'm Hark."

The woman tilted her head. A small motion. Clinical. "You can call yourself whatever you like. The debt doesn't care."

The debt. The word settled into the mist like it belonged there. The instructor had said something similar, a long time ago, in the training chamber with the scorched walls and the cold stone floor. Hark had been ten years old, maybe eleven, his hands wrapped in silk, the frost already threading across his knuckles. He'd overextended—pulled too much heat, held it too long, collapsed from the fever that followed. The instructor had stood over him, patient, unblinking, his hands clean.

*"The debt always settles,"* he'd said. His voice was flat, the voice of someone who'd said the same words to a hundred children and would say them to a hundred more. *"You can't cheat it. You can't outrun it. You can only manage it."*

Hark had not understood, then. He had thought the debt was just frost and cold and the numbness in his fingers. He had thought it was a price you paid in flesh and forgot about.

He understood now. The debt was not just frost-scars and unpaid warmth. The debt was the carriage and the silk and the handler's hands that never moved. The debt was Lea's grey eyes and the bolt through her chest and the pressure collapsing. The debt was the instructor's body in the forest and the handler's body beside him and the years of running that followed. The debt was the ropewalk room and the Factorage and the Weeping Dog and the thousand meals eaten without tasting. The debt was Soric's rumor and Renne's vortex and Marta's stew and a kiss that tasted like salt.

The debt was standing here, in a coastal field at dawn, facing a woman who knew his name and could read every mistake he'd ever made in the white lines on his hands.

The debt always settled. It was settling now.

The Silencers raised their crossbows.

Hark moved. Not toward them—sideways, low, behind a boulder at the edge of the field. The bolts cracked against the stone. Shards of rock sprayed into the mist. He pressed his back against the granite, felt the cold of it through his coat, catalogued. Two Silencers. Two crossbows. Reload time—ten seconds, maybe twelve. The Savant was unarmed, but it didn't matter. The Savant didn't need a weapon. The Savant was reading his every move before he made it.

Sword on his back. Knife at his belt. The crossbow was still at the ferry—too far, always too far. He drew the sword. Joel's blade. Plain steel, no name, no history. Balanced right and it wouldn't break. The grip was warm in his hand. His body heat bleeding into the leather. He didn't notice. He never noticed.

The first Silencer rounded the boulder. Crossbow reloaded, stock braced, eyes scanning. The helm was steel, the gorget steel, the pauldrons steel. But the gap between helm and gorget was flesh. Joel had taught him that. *Armpits and groins and throats. The rest is just bone and attitude.*

Hark was already inside his reach.

The blade found the gap. In. Out. A handspan of steel through the soft tissue above the collarbone. The Silencer made a sound—wet, surprised—and his finger tightened on the trigger. The bolt went wide. He fell. The crossbow clattered on the stone.

The second Silencer fired. Hark turned, too slow, the bolt grazing his arm—a line of fire across his bicep, the fabric of his coat splitting, blood welling dark and hot. He didn't feel it. Pain was information. He used it. Sword, then dagger. The Silencer was reaching for a second bolt, and Hark was already closing, already inside the arc of the crossbow. The dagger went into the gap under the arm. The sword came down on the wrist that was reaching for the belt knife. The Silencer went down. Blood on the grass. Blood on the stone.

Hark stood over him. Breathing hard. The mist was still. The Savant was still watching.

She had not moved. Her hands were still folded in front of her, clean and pale and motionless. Her eyes were still on his hands. She had watched him kill two Silencers without flinching. That was the worst part. Not the grey tabard or the clean hands or the way she'd said his name. The way she watched. Like he was a ledger she was auditing. Like his survival was a line item she hadn't expected to see.

"You've gotten better," she said. Her voice was calm. Clinical. "The Order's training served you well."

Hark spat blood. It landed on the grass, red and bright in the grey light. "The Order's training tried to burn me out."

"And yet you survived. Interesting."

She turned. Walked back into the mist. Her grey tabard faded, then her grey sleeves, then her clean hands. The mist swallowed her the way the dark had swallowed Renne—completely, silently, without looking back.

Hark didn't pursue. He couldn't. His arm was bleeding—the graze was deeper than he'd thought, the blood running down to his wrist, soaking into the leather of his glove. The cold in his fingers was deeper than before. The frost had climbed past his wrists, up toward his elbows, and the new lines were still writing themselves, and the ledger was still updating.

He knelt. Wiped the blade on the grass. Sheathed it. The Silencers' bodies were still. The mist was lifting. The sun was coming up, pale and cold over the water. Somewhere in the islands, Renne was walking east. Somewhere in the dark, Corin was on a ferry. Somewhere in the quiet, Marta was stirring a pot with her steady hands.

The debt was settling. It was not done settling. The Savant would report back. The Order would send more hunters. The false trail was lit, and the hunters were on it, and he was the thing they were chasing.

He flexed his fingers. The leather creaked. The blood was drying on his wrist, tacky and warm. The cold was still there. It was always there.

He walked east. Toward the mainland. Toward the next fight. The Savant had called him by his name, and he had flinched, and he was still standing. That was something.
