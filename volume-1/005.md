# Chapter 5: The Payment

Graen's office was smaller in the afternoon. The window above the harbour was still too dirty to see through, but the light had changed—lower, yellower, catching the dust in the air and turning it to gold. Walnut shells crunched under Hark's boots as he crossed the threshold. There were more of them now. Graen had been eating his feelings again.

The guildmaster was behind his desk, the ledger open, the scale balanced. His fingers were stained brown at the tips, the nails cracked from a lifetime of cracking shells. He didn't look up when Hark entered. He was writing something. Or pretending to write something. The pen moved across the page in a rhythm too regular to be real.

"The contract was for three." Graen's voice was oil and gravel, same as always. He still didn't look up.

"It was for three or more. Clause in the fine print. Verrol wrote it in."

"Verrol writes a lot of things."

"He writes what you tell him."

Graen looked up then. His eyes were small, pouched in the broad slab of his face, but not stupid. He'd been doing this too long to be stupid. "You're claiming four."

"Four. Ears are in the sack. Salted. You want to count them, count them."

"I don't need to count them." He did. It was part of the ritual. The ears would be counted, weighed, logged, and buried in the midden behind the Factorage. Nothing went unrecorded in Graen's kingdom.

"Four ogres. The pay was for three. Standard rate, plus the overage clause. You owe me." Hark named the number.

Graen named a smaller number.

"Too low."

"That's the standard."

"The standard is per ogre. Four ogres, four payments. Not three plus a pat on the head."

"The guild takes a cut."

"The guild takes twelve percent. You've already taken it. This is the remainder."

Graen cracked a walnut. The shell splintered between his thumbs. He chewed, swallowed, brushed the fragments onto the floor. The pile was getting deeper. Another month and he'd be wading in them. "I don't have authorisation for that amount."

"Then get it."

"The budget's fixed. You know how it is. Caravans are down. Revenue's thin." The same words he'd used that morning. The same script. Graen was a man who rehearsed his arguments while he ate.

"Revenue's always thin. You're still sitting here. The Factorage is still standing. The funds are there. You just don't want to release them."

Silence. The pen had stopped moving. Graen's fingers were still on the desk, walnut-stained, the nails thick and yellowed. The hands of a man who'd never swung a sword but had signed a thousand contracts. Hark watched them. The hands told you everything. These hands were steady. These hands were calculating. These hands were going to crack.

"I can do three and a half."

Hark said nothing.

"Three and three-quarters."

Nothing.

A long breath through the nose. Graen's nostrils flared. He looked at the ledger, then at the sack of ears, then at Hark's face. The calculation was visible—cost of losing a reliable contractor, cost of setting a precedent, cost of admitting that the clause he'd approved was binding. The numbers turned over, one by one, and Hark watched them fall.

"Fine." The word came out like a splinter. "Four. Standard rate."

"Good."

"It's not good. It's robbery." But Graen was already reaching for the strongbox, already counting out the coins, the silver catching the yellow light. He stacked them on the desk in neat piles. Four piles. Four ogres. "You know, most contractors don't argue like this."

"Most contractors don't survive long enough to learn how."

Graen snorted. It might have been a laugh. It might have been indigestion. He pushed the coins across the desk and leaned back in his chair, the wood groaning under his bulk. "Take it. Get out. Try not to get killed before the next contract."

"No promises."

"Never are."

Hark scooped the coins into his pouch. The weight was right. He knew the weight of four silver payments, knew it in his wrist, in the pull of the leather strap against his belt. He counted anyway. Twice. The habit of hunger.

Joel had taught him to count coin. The old man's voice was still there, gravel and phlegm, a ghost in the back of his skull. *"Twice. Once for what's there. Once for what they might have skimmed. Trust the count, not the face. Faces lie. Numbers don't."*

Hark had watched Joel's hands shake as he counted. They were in a tavern outside Denton, the fire low, the night cold, the old man's cough getting worse. His fingers were yellowed, cracked, the nails thick and strange—blood cancer eating its way through the bones, the body paying a debt no magic had incurred. He'd counted his coins twice anyway. Stacked them. Restacked them. Made Hark do the same.

*"How many?"*

*"Eight."*

*"Count again."*

*"Eight."*

*"Good. Now count them like you're blind and they're lying. What's the weight? What's the sound? What's the edge feel like under your thumb? Coins don't change, kid. People do. The count's the only thing you can trust."*

The old man never stopped counting. Right up to the end, when his fingers were too weak to hold the coins, he'd made Hark count for him. The cancer took his body. It didn't take the habit.

Hark pulled the pouch strings tight and stood. Graen was watching him with an expression that might have been respect or might have been the early stages of a stomach complaint. Hard to tell with Graen. The guildmaster was built like a barrel—wide, solid, the kind of body that had been strong once and was now just heavy. A lifetime of walnuts and ledgers had settled into his gut, but there was still muscle under it, still the shape of a man who'd done physical work before he'd learned to delegate it. His face was built for bargaining, not for honesty.

He sat behind a desk that was too small for him and pretended the ledger was his kingdom. He wasn't cruel. He wasn't kind. He was just a man who'd never met a margin he wouldn't shave. Hark had known a hundred Graens across the Confederacy—guildmasters, merchants, minor lords who measured their worth in ledgers and died surprised that the numbers didn't follow them into the ground. They all died in their beds, rich and alone, and someone else counted their coin twice.

"Week after next," Graen said. "There's a contract coming down from the north. Caravan escort. Good pay. Interested?"

"Maybe."

"Maybe isn't a commitment."

"Nothing is."

Graen cracked another walnut. The shell joined the others. "Fair enough. Verrol will hold the papers. If you're still alive."

"If I'm still alive."

The door closed behind him. The Factorage was quiet—Verrol's desk empty, the ledger closed, the inkwell capped. The clerk had gone home. Or wherever Verrol went when he wasn't logging faces. Hark had never asked. Verrol had never told.

The iron sign swung overhead as he stepped outside. Scales with one pan broken. The afternoon was fading, the light going orange over the harbour, the gulls settling into their evening complaints. The streets were thinning. The fish market had closed. The taverns were starting to fill.

He walked toward the ropewalk. The coins were warm in his pouch—his own body heat bleeding through the leather, a small bright weight against his hip. He didn't notice. He never noticed. The warmth was just there, the same way the cold was just there, and neither of them meant anything.

The ropewalk stairs were dark. He climbed them one at a time, hand on the rail, the wood groaning under his boots. The door at the top was locked. He unlocked it. Stepped inside. Locked it again behind him. Checked the lock twice.

The room was the same. Bed. Chair. Washbasin. The salt-crusted window catching the last of the light. He sat on the cot and pulled off his boots. Left first. Then right. The leather was still cracked. The stitching was still frayed. The coins were still in the pouch on his belt.

He didn't take them out. He didn't count them again. He sat on the edge of the cot and stared at the wall—crack running east to west, water stain in the corner shaped like a hand—and the cold in his fingers was worse today. Deeper. The heat-theft in the cave had cost him. It always cost him. The debt was there, a new white line under the glove, a numbness that reached a little further up his wrist than it had that morning.

Tomorrow's problem. He'd been saying that for fifteen years. The debt didn't care. The debt just kept settling, slow and patient and utterly indifferent to his opinion on the matter.

He flexed his fingers. The leather creaked. The cold stayed.

The pouch was warm against his hip. The wall was grey in the fading light. Somewhere below, the ropewalk workers were finishing their shift, their voices low and tired. Hark sat on the cot and stared at nothing and tried his best not to feel anything at all.

He failed. He always failed. But the trying was a ritual, and rituals were what he had, and the coin was counted, and the contract was closed, and tomorrow there would be another one.

The day ended. The cold didn't.
