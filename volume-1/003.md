# Chapter 3: The Walk

The coastal trail unspooled ahead of him—limestone crushed to gravel, salt-scrub clawing at the edges, the sea a grey hunger to his left. Three miles to the ogre territory. Maybe less. The map in his head was drawn from a contract description and a boatman's warning, which meant it was half guesswork and half lie.

He walked. The morning was still cold, the sun still climbing, the gulls still screaming behind him. Salthaven shrank to a smear of rooftops and then vanished behind a headland. Good. Cities were useful. Cities were also full of people, and people were full of questions, and questions cost more than he was willing to pay.

Inventory. Mental check. The Litany of the Prepared. Joel had taught him that too—name what you carry before you need it, because needing it and not knowing you have it is the same as not having it at all.

Longsword. Joel's. Plain steel, no name, no history. Balanced right. Worn on the back, the hilt riding above his left shoulder. He'd drawn it a thousand times. It always came out the same way.

Dagger. Belt, left side. Cross-draw. The sheath was old leather, cracked along the stitching, but the blade was sharp and the grip was wrapped in cord that hadn't frayed yet.

Smoke pellets. Belt pouch, right hip. Three of them. Clay spheres the size of walnuts, packed with saltpetre and dried kelp. Useless against ogres—their noses were too bad—but useful against anything that breathed and thought. He'd made them himself. Joel had taught him that, too, in the last month before the cancer took his voice.

Wire. Pack, coiled tight. Twenty feet of steel wire, thin enough to cut, strong enough to hold a snare. He'd used it for traps, locks, and once, in a bad winter, to stitch a wound closed. The scar from that was still there, a white line among the white lines. He'd lost count of which was which.

Gloves. Intact. For now. The loose thread was still tucked under the seam. He flexed his fingers. The leather moved. The numbness stayed.

That was it. A sword, a knife, three pellets, a coil of wire, and the clothes on his back. No pack. No bedroll. If the job took more than a day, he'd sleep rough and wake cold. Had done it before. Would do it again. The cold was company he'd learned to live with.

The trail climbed. The cliffs rose on his right, limestone scarred by wind and salt, the rock face pocked with caves too small for ogres. Tide pools glittered below, trapped seawater going stagnant in the morning light. He didn't look at them. Small beauties were fifth on the list. He kept his eyes on the trail and his thoughts on the job.

Three ogres. The number was probably wrong. Graen's numbers were always wrong—not from malice, Graen didn't have the imagination for malice, but from a guildmaster's distance from the work. Three ogres on paper meant three to five in the field. Low risk meant the last courier who'd reported them had survived. Survived didn't mean assessed correctly. Survived meant ran fast.

He'd handled ogres before. Stupid, strong, predictable. They fought like avalanches—straight lines, no lateral movement, all weight and no grace. The trick was not being in front of them. The trick was wire across a cave mouth, a smoke pellet to blind them, a sword through the soft tissue under the jaw before they understood what they were fighting. The trick was never fighting fair.

Joel again. *"Fair is for duellists and dead men. You're neither."* Hark had been fifteen when he'd heard that. He was twenty-five now and it was still true.

A child laughed.

Hark stopped. The sound came from ahead—a doorstep carved into the cliffside, a fisherman's shack wedged between the trail and the rock. The child was small, maybe four, sitting on the step with a stick in one hand and a shell in the other. The laugh was unguarded, bright, the kind of sound that didn't know what the world was yet.

He noticed. He noticed the way the light caught the shell, the way the child's fingers curled around the stick, the way the laugh came from somewhere deep in the belly and spilled out without permission. He noticed all of it, the full inventory, the way he noticed exits and weapons and the weight of a coat over a holster.

Then he suppressed it. Small beauties were irrelevant. Small beauties got you killed. He walked faster.

Lea had laughed like that.

Small and quickly stifled. In the dark of the dormitory, after he'd whispered that she made the air weird. She'd clapped a hand over her mouth, eyes wide, both of them frozen in their cots, listening for footsteps. The instructor's footsteps. The handler's footsteps. Anyone's footsteps. No one came. And in the silence that followed, he'd felt something he couldn't name—a warmth that had nothing to do with his gate, a loosening in his chest that was either hope or the absence of fear.

*Maybe we survive this.*

He was nine. He didn't know yet that surviving was not the same as living. Didn't know that Lea would be dead before she turned twelve, a lightning bolt through her chest, her grey eyes wide and surprised and already fading. Didn't know that he'd leave his name beside her body and spend the next fifteen years walking trails like this one, counting inventory, suppressing the sound of a child's laugh before it could root itself in his chest.

He walked faster. The fisherman's shack fell behind him. The child's laugh faded. The trail turned inland, away from the cliffs, and the salt-scrub gave way to sparse trees and the smell of something old and rotten.

The wind shifted. Salt spray came off the water, a fine mist that settled on his face and his gloves and the hilt of Joel's sword. He tasted it. Salt and stone and the faint copper tang of the tide pools below. His mother had tasted salt once. A trip to the coast he barely remembered—a cart, a blanket, her hand on his forehead checking for fever. He'd been four, maybe five. The heat was already in him, the furnace with the door missing, but she hadn't known what it was. She'd just pressed her palm to his skin and frowned and said he was warm but not sick.

Warm but not sick.

He suppressed that too. The memory was a door he'd locked a long time ago, and he wasn't going to open it now. Not for salt spray. Not for anything.

The trees thickened. The light went grey and dappled. The smell got worse—offal and old blood, the signature of an ogre den, the particular rot of things that ate their prey where they killed it. Hark slowed. His hand went to the hilt of his sword. The grip was warm in his palm, the leather heating against his skin, his body's heat bleeding out through the gloves.

He didn't notice. He never noticed. The warmth was just there, the same way the cold was just there, and neither of them meant anything.

The cave mouth opened ahead. Low. Dark. A wound in the limestone, the rock above it stained black from old smoke. The ground in front of it was churned to mud, scattered with bones and fur and the shattered remains of something that might have been a crate. The smell was a wall. He breathed through his mouth and kept walking.

He drew the sword.

The steel came free with a whisper. Balanced right. The grip was warm. The blade caught the grey light and held it, dull and patient and without a story. Joel's sword. A sword that wouldn't break.

Hark stood at the edge of the dark and let his eyes adjust. The cold in his fingers was steady. The weight of the blade was familiar. The Litany of the Prepared was complete. Somewhere in the dark, something breathing shifted its weight.

Three ogres. Probably more. The contract was already wrong. The pay was already light. The day was already old and the job hadn't even started.

He stepped into the cave.
