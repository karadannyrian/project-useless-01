# Chapter 6: The Weeping Dog

The Weeping Dog crouched between a net-maker's shed and a boarded-up chandlery, its sign a hound with one tear carved into the wood. The tear had been painted blue once. Now it was just a gouge. Hark pushed through the door and the smoke hit him—peat and tallow and the ghost of a thousand cheap meals.

Low ceilings. Beams stained black from years of pipe-smoke. The fire was going in the hearth, more ash than flame, and the tables were mostly empty. Fishermen. A pair of net-menders arguing over something that didn't matter. A woman at the corner table nursing a cup of something amber. Soric behind the bar.

Hark took his usual seat. Back to the wall. Sightlines to the door and the kitchen and the narrow stair that led up to the rooms. The geometry of leaving, mapped fresh. Old habit.

Bread. Cheese. Watered ale. He ate the same thing every night, not because it was cheap—though it was—but because it was easy. The bread was yesterday's. The cheese was hard at the edges, the rind cracked like old leather. The ale tasted of the barrel it came from and not much else. He didn't taste it anymore. Taste was irrelevant. Food was fuel. Fuel was function. The rest was noise.

Soric watched him from the bar.

Old marine. Missing his left arm from the elbow down. The stump was smooth—clean amputation, not combat. Disease, maybe. Or a choice. He poured ale with his right hand and didn't spill. That was harder than it looked. Hark had tried it once, off-handed, and made a mess of his sleeve. Soric had watched him try and said nothing. That was Soric's way. He said nothing about a lot of things.

He knew more than he told. They all did, in this job. But Soric was the one who'd mentioned the rumors six months ago, wiping down the bar with his one hand, his voice low and casual like he was talking about the weather. *Escaped mages. Seven of them. Teaching each other. Down south, in the islands. The League's hiding them, or someone is.* Hark had pretended not to care. Soric had pretended to believe him. They'd been pretending ever since.

Tonight Soric just set down another ale without being asked. Hark hadn't ordered it. He looked at the cup. Looked at Soric. Soric was already wiping down the other end of the bar, his back half-turned, the stump of his arm moving in the same rhythm as the hand. Muscle memory. The body remembering what the flesh had lost.

Hark drank the ale. The warmth spread through his chest—a different kind of warmth, not the furnace of his gate, not the heat of a borrowed body dying under his hand. Just warmth. Just ale. He didn't name it. Naming things made them real. Real things cost something.

The woman at the next table smiled at him.

She was young. Dark hair pulled back, a few strands loose at the temples. Her hands were wrapped around a clay cup. No weapons. No calluses that he could see—the fingers were smooth, the knuckles unmarked. Not a fighter. Not a threat. Her smile was the kind that expected an answer. He didn't give one. He looked at her hands a moment longer—hands told you everything, and these told him nothing—then returned to his bread.

She stopped smiling. Turned back to her cup. The moment passed. Hark chewed his bread and felt nothing about it. Or told himself he felt nothing. The difference was academic.

Joel had lost fingers, not an arm. Two on his left hand, the middle and ring, lost to frostbite during a winter contract in the Hold-Kingdoms. They'd been camped in a mountain pass, the wind screaming through the rocks, the cold so deep it got into your bones and stayed there. Joel had taken his gloves off to fix a broken strap and hadn't noticed the frostbite setting in until the fingers went grey.

*"Didn't even notice until I tried to count coin,"* he'd said, holding up the ruined hand. The stumps were healed by then, the skin puckered and pale. *"Reached for a silver piece and my fingers weren't there. That's when you know it's bad. When the counting stops working."*

Hark had looked at the stumps. The missing fingers. The way Joel's hand had reshaped itself around the absence. He'd thought: that will be me. Not the frostbite—the loss. The slow accrual of damage, the body surrendering piece by piece, the cold taking more than it gave. He was fifteen. He was right.

He finished the bread. Finished the ale. Soric was still at the bar, still wiping the same stretch of wood, still not looking at him. The fire had died down to embers. The net-menders had settled their argument or given up on it. The woman with the dark hair was gone, her cup empty, her smile long faded.

"Contract went well," Soric said. Not a question.

"Four ogres."

"Graen pay for four?"

"Eventually."

Soric nodded. The rag kept moving. "Heard something today. Might interest you."

"What?"

"Order's moving people north. Cradle-hounds and a Savant. Looking for someone." Soric's eyes flicked up, met his for a half-second, dropped back to the bar. "Didn't say who."

The cold in Hark's fingers deepened. Not the frost. Just cold. "Not looking for me."

"Didn't say they were."

"Then it's not my problem."

"Didn't say that either."

Soric turned away, hung the rag over the tap, and started closing down the bar. The conversation was over. It had never started. That was Soric's way. He planted seeds and let them grow in the dark, and whether you watered them was your own affair.

Hark stood. Left coins on the table. The right amount. He didn't count twice—Soric had never skimmed, not once in three years. That was something. He didn't name what.

Outside, the night air hit him. Cold. Salt. The harbour was black except for the lanterns on the fishing boats, bobbing like small drowned stars. His breath steamed in front of his face. A Fire-mage steaming in the cold—the irony was not lost on him. He could feel the heat inside, the furnace still burning, the gate still closed. The warmth stayed locked in his chest where it belonged. The cold stayed in his fingers where it lived.

He walked to the ropewalk. The streets were empty. The tavern noise faded behind him. The stairs were dark and the door was locked and he unlocked it and locked it again behind him and checked the lock twice.

The room was the same. The cot was the same. He lay down in his clothes, the sword on the chair within reach, the boot knife still strapped to his calf. The ceiling was the same—crack east to west, water stain shaped like a hand. He stared at it.

The dream would come tonight. It always came after a kill.

Not the ogres. They didn't dream. They were just work, just bodies, just ears in a sack and silver in a pouch. But the heat-theft had opened the door. The heat-theft always opened the door. He'd put his bare hand on the fourth ogre's chest and pulled the warmth out of it, and the warmth had flowed into him, and for one second—two—he'd felt temperature again. Then the cold had come back, worse than before, and the door in his mind had swung open a crack, and now the dream was waiting behind it.

Lea's grey eyes. The bolt through her chest. The pressure collapsing. The scream that was light.

He closed his eyes. The ceiling didn't change. The cold didn't change. The dream would come, and he would wake, and tomorrow there would be another contract, another meal, another night in the ropewalk room with the door locked and the sword within reach.

The day ended. The dream began.
