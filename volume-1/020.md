# Chapter 20: The Reckoning

The cave mouth framed the dawn. Grey light. Mist rising from the grass. Blood on the stones—his blood, from the graze that still hadn't closed, from the fresh cuts he'd earned crawling through the dark. The Savant stood at the centre of the light, her grey tabard stirring in the wind off the sea. The three Silencers flanked her. Crossbows raised. Blades out. Their faces were blank. Their hands were steady.

Hark leaned against the cave wall. His legs were shaking. His sword was in his hand. The grip was still warm.

"You've led us on a nice chase, Damian." The Savant's voice was calm. Clinical. The voice of someone reading a ledger aloud. "But it's over."

"My name is Hark."

"Your name is Damian Whiters. You were collected at age seven. You escaped at age ten. You killed two Order personnel—your instructor and a handler—in the forest outside the Cradle." She tilted her head. The motion was small. Precise. "You have been running for fifteen years. That is the ledger. That is the truth."

The words landed like stones dropped into still water. Each one a fact. Each one irrefutable. He'd killed the instructor with light through the chest. He'd killed the handler with the same light, carving an arc through the dark. He'd left their bodies in the forest beside Lea's. He'd walked away and not looked back. The Savant had read it all. The frost on his knuckles. The debt-pattern of the Light mutation. The fifteen years of unpaid balance.

He didn't deny it. Denial was noise. Denial told them more than silence.

He thought of Renne.

The way she'd said *invitation*. Standing in the clearing with the mist rising from the grass, her hand outstretched, water pooling in her palm. *Water doesn't respond to force. It responds to invitation.* She'd looked at him with those grey eyes—grey like a winter sky, grey like the sea before a storm—and she'd said *try*. He'd reached out. His gloved fingers had hovered over the water. The water had shivered. Not from magic. Just from heat. The ordinary warmth of a body that was still alive.

*You're warm,* she'd said. *Even through the gloves.*

*That's not magic. That's just my body.*

*Same thing.*

He wanted to see her again.

The thought surfaced—unbidden, unbidden, unwelcome—and it didn't go away. It sat in his chest like a splinter. Like a door that had been closed for fifteen years and was finally swinging open.

He wanted to sit by the hearth in the cottage. The fire burning low. Marta stirring the pot with her steady hands. Corin fidgeting on his stool. The girl with the shaking fingers practicing her vortex in the corner. He wanted to eat stew that tasted of root vegetables and white fish and herbs he still didn't recognize. He wanted Renne to sit across from him and not watch him eat. That was why he'd stayed. He hadn't known it then. He knew it now.

He wanted to watch her hands shape water into silver vortices. The way they'd never stopped moving—mending the net, brushing a strand of dark hair from her face, pressing her thumb against the seam of his glove. Twelve stitches. Waxed cord. *Your hands are cold.* *They're always cold.* *I know.*

He wanted to stop running. He wanted to be still. He wanted to be warm.

He wanted.

He wanted.

He wanted.

He had not allowed himself to want anything in fifteen years. Wanting was a liability. Wanting was a door that opened onto grief. He'd wanted to see his mother again, once. Her hands on his face. Her voice saying his name like it was something she was losing. He'd wanted to sit by the river with Lea, watching the wind make the water shiver, the way she'd described it in the dormitory dark. He'd wanted to be warm. He'd wanted to be still. He'd wanted to be someone who wasn't running.

The Order had taken those wants, one by one. The carriage. The silk. The handler's clean hands that never moved. The instructor's flat voice saying *again*. The forest and the fever spikes and the years of being no one in particular. They'd taken everything until there was nothing left but the cold and the running and the debt.

He had not known that wants could grow back. They were growing now. They were painful and bright and terrifying. They had teeth. They had been growing teeth for a long time.

He didn't know what to do with them. He knew only that he would not let the Savant take them. She could read his ledger. She could recite his name and his crimes and the fifteen years of running. She could not have this. The wanting was his. It was the only thing that was his.

The wanting did not kill him. That was something.

The Silencers advanced.

Hark pushed off the wall. His legs screamed. His arm was still bleeding. The sword was in his hand. Joel's blade. The grip was warm. The edge was chipped now—three days of running, three days of fighting, the steel notched where it had caught on bone and armour. It would still cut. That was enough.

The first Silencer swung. A broad strike, meant to overpower. Hark parried—the impact jarring up his arm, through his shoulder, into his ribs—and riposted. The blade found the gap under the arm. In. Out. The Silencer grunted. Staggered. Fell.

The second was faster. The sword came in low, and Hark was too slow, too tired, the blade knocked from his hand. It clattered against the stone. He drew his dagger. The second Silencer pressed forward, and Hark stepped inside the reach—too close for the sword, too close for the crossbow—and the dagger went into the throat. The Silencer made a sound like wet gravel. Went down.

The third was behind him.

The crossbow cracked. The bolt took him through the leg—above the knee, through the muscle, the point punching out the other side. He felt it before he heard it. A sharp crack, and then nothing. The silence of a body that hadn't caught up to the wound yet. His leg gave out. He fell to one knee. Blood on the stone. His blood. The bolt was still in his leg. The shaft was wood. The point was steel. The pain was information. He used it.

The Silencers closed in. The two still standing. Blades out. The third on the ground, still breathing. The Savant watched from the cave mouth. Her hands were still folded. Her eyes were still on his hands.

He opened his gate.

Not the internal reservoir. The environment. The ambient heat of the cave. The faint warmth of the mist outside. The heat bleeding from the Silencers' bodies—warm-blooded, living, close. He pulled it all. Concentrated it. Held it. The frost screamed across his knuckles, his wrists, his forearms, climbing toward his shoulders. New lines. New entries. The ledger updating itself in permanent white.

The Light mutation ignited.

Not a beam this time. A sphere. Blinding white, expanding outward from his chest, swallowing the cave mouth, swallowing the Silencers, swallowing everything. The light was a wound in the world. The light was everything he had left.

The Silencers screamed. The sound was distant—muffled, unimportant. The light held for three seconds. Five. Then it guttered out, and the dark came rushing back, and the Silencers were on the ground. Burned. Blinded. Still breathing.

He didn't kill them. He didn't have to. He was not the Order.

The Savant was still standing.

She stepped through the dissipating light. Her grey tabard was untouched. Her hands were still folded. Her eyes were still on his hands. Insensitive. The light had passed through her like it was nothing, because to her it was nothing. She was immune. She had always been immune.

She drew a knife. The blade was plain. Functional. The blade of someone who didn't need a weapon to be dangerous but carried one anyway.

"You can't hurt me, Damian."

He pulled off his left glove.

The leather came away stiff with blood and frost. His hand was a ruin—white lines on white skin, the scars threading from his fingertips to his wrist, his forearm, climbing toward his elbow. The ledger written in flesh. Every working he'd ever done. Every debt he'd ever paid. The carriage. The training floor. The forest. The fifteen years of running. It was all there. It had always been there.

He held up his hand.

"Read me."

The Savant's eyes dropped to his hand. She couldn't help it. She was a Savant. Reading was what she did. The knife was still in her hand. Her face was still calm. But her eyes were moving—tracing the lines, reading the ledger, the full history of a mage who should have died a dozen times and hadn't.

"Read the whole ledger. The escape. The kills. The Light mutation. The fifteen years of running." His voice was hoarse. His leg was bleeding. His hands were shaking. "Read it and tell me I'm wrong."

She hesitated.

For the first time, she hesitated. The knife didn't move. Her eyes were still on his hand. The silence stretched between them—one breath, two, three. The mist rose outside. The sea crashed against the rocks below. The Silencers groaned on the ground.

"You're not wrong," she said. Her voice was different now. Not clinical. Something else. Something he couldn't name. "But you're not right either. The debt always settles."

"I know."

He held her gaze. His hand was still raised. The frost-scars were still visible. The ledger was still open.

"I'm ready."
