# Chapter 14: The Contract Within the Contract

Graen's office was smaller than Hark remembered. Or maybe he'd just been spending too much time in open air, on ferries and footpaths and the clearing behind a cottage where the mist rose like breath. The walls were the same. The ledger was the same. The walnut shells were fresh—Graen had been eating his feelings again, cracking them one by one, the fragments scattered across the floor like tiny bones.

"You look different," Graen said. He didn't look up from the ledger.

"Same clothes."

"Not what I meant." Graen cracked another walnut. The shell splintered between his thumbs. He chewed, swallowed, brushed the fragments onto the floor. "You been sleeping?"

"Some."

"You look like you haven't."

"Some means some."

Graen grunted. He didn't push. Graen never pushed on personal matters. The guildmaster cared about contracts and margins and the percentage the Factorage took off the top. The rest was noise. That was the arrangement. That was why Hark kept coming back.

"Got something for you." Graen pushed a paper across the desk. The ink was fresh—Verrol's hand, neat and precise. "Courier job. Package to the next island over. Easy money."

Hark read the contract. The pay was wrong. Not wrong by a little—wrong by a lot. Courier jobs paid in copper. This was paying in silver. Two days' work at most, and the number on the page was closer to a week's wages. He'd done enough contracts to know when a number didn't match the job. The number never matched the job when someone was hiding something.

"What's in the package."

"Doesn't matter."

"It matters to me."

Graen chewed a walnut. The crunch was loud in the small office. His eyes were small, pouched, calculating. He was weighing something—how much to tell, how much to withhold, how much Hark would push before he walked. The calculation was visible. The calculation was always visible with Graen.

"Fine." He brushed more shells onto the floor. "It's a message. From someone who doesn't want to be named, to someone who doesn't want to be found. You deliver it. You don't read it. You don't ask questions. That's the job."

"Someone who doesn't want to be found."

"That's what I said."

"That's a lot of silver for a message."

"That's a lot of silver for not asking questions." Graen leaned back in his chair. The wood groaned. "Look. I don't know who sent it. I don't know who's receiving it. Verrol took the contract from a third party. The third party was wearing a hood. It's all very mysterious and very stupid, but the coin is real, and the job is simple, and you're the only contractor I've got who can keep his mouth shut for more than five minutes."

"Verrol can keep his mouth shut."

"Verrol doesn't leave the building. This job requires leaving." Graen cracked another walnut. "You want it or not?"

He didn't want it. He wanted to walk out of the Factorage and back to the ropewalk room and lock the door and check the lock twice and stare at the ceiling until the feeling in his chest went away. He'd been back in Salthaven for three days. The feeling hadn't gone away. The feeling was a splinter, and the splinter was a door, and the door was still open.

A message to someone who didn't want to be found. That could be anyone. A debtor. A fugitive. A noble's bastard. A mage who'd escaped the Order and was hiding in the islands, teaching others to guard. That could be her. The description was precise—grey eyes, dark hair, hands that never stopped moving—and the description had followed him across three islands and back again. Someone else could have followed it too. Someone else could be sending messages. Someone else could be hunting.

"Fine," he said. "I'll take it."

"Good." Graen pulled a leather tube from under the desk. Small. No bigger than a scroll case. The wax seal was plain—no crest, no mark, just a blob of red wax pressed flat with a thumb. The package was light. Papers, probably. Or something thinner. "Delivery address is on the contract. Don't open the tube. Don't shake the tube. Don't do anything except carry it from here to there and hand it to the person whose name is on the paper."

"Who's the name."

"Read the contract."

Hark read the contract. The name was at the bottom, in Verrol's neat script. Just a name. No title. No address beyond the island and a landmark—a shrine, a specific tree, a cottage with a thatched roof and a garden and a woman who shaped water into vortices no bigger than a candle flame. The name was hers. The address was hers. The package was for her.

He didn't react. His pulse was steady. His face was still. Inside, the inventory was running.

*Who sent this?* Graen didn't know. The third party was anonymous—hooded, careful, the kind of person who knew how to move without leaving tracks. The wax seal was plain. The tube was light. Not a threat, probably. Threats were heavier. Threats came with demands.

*Why her?* She'd been hiding for a decade. The League knew about her. Soric knew about her. The rumor had spread through the archipelago, passed from sailor to merchant to barkeep, and the description was consistent, and the description was accurate, and if enough people knew the description, enough people could find the address.

*Is this a trap?* The Order didn't send messages. The Order sent hunters—Cradle-hounds and Savants and mage-detectors with clean hands and loaded crossbows. They didn't write letters. They collected children and killed the ones who ran. If the Order had found her, there wouldn't be a package. There'd be a raid. There'd be nothing left but ash and frost and the ledger settling.

But someone else might send a message. Someone who wanted to warn her. Someone who wanted to help. Someone who'd followed the same rumor Hark had followed, and found the same clearing, and decided to reach out instead of run.

He had not wanted to be found.

For fifteen years, that had been his only goal. Stay invisible. Stay small. Stay nothing. The Order hunted patterns, and he was not a pattern. He was a single thread, frayed and faded, lost in the weave of a continent that didn't look at men like him. He paid in cash. He slept in rooms with no names on the doors. He wore gloves and long sleeves and never let anyone see the frost-scars that wrote his history in white lines. He took contracts that paid in silver and asked no questions, and when the contracts were done, he moved on. No roots. No ties. No one to betray him. No one to bury.

He had succeeded. He was still succeeding. The cold in his fingers was the price, and he paid it gladly. Every white line was a receipt. Every numb knuckle was a ledger entry that said *you are still here, you are still running, you have not been caught.*

He had not known, then, that not being found was not the same as not being alone. The forest had taught him survival. Joel had taught him counting. Soric had taught him listening. No one had taught him the difference between hiding and living. He was still learning. He was twenty-five years old, and he was still learning, and the lesson was a woman with grey eyes who shaped water into silver vortices and said *invitation* instead of *again*.

He took the package. It was small in his gloved hand—a leather tube, sealed with wax, no heavier than a letter. He didn't open it. He didn't shake it. He didn't do anything except slide it into his coat pocket and stand up from the chair and walk out of Graen's office. The walnut shells crunched under his boots. Graen didn't say goodbye. Graen never said goodbye.

The ferry was at the dock. The same ferryman. The same dog. The crossing was calm, the sea flat and grey, the sky threatening rain. Hark stood at the rail and watched the water. The package was warm against his hip—his own body heat bleeding through the leather, same as it always did. He didn't notice. He never noticed.

He thought of Renne. The vortex spinning between her palms. The way she'd held out her hand and said *try*. The way the water had shivered when his fingers hovered over it—not from magic, just from heat, the ordinary warmth of a body that was still alive. She'd said *invitation*. She'd said *from you, that's the same thing*. She'd looked at him with grey eyes that were not Lea's eyes and asked no questions and let him sit on a stump in her garden until the sun went down.

He thought of Lea. Her grey eyes. The bolt. The pressure collapsing. He thought of her hands—how they'd moved when she was happy, how they'd stilled when she was afraid, how they'd squeezed his once and then gone cold. He thought of the vortex she'd made, silver and silent, a miniature storm contained in the cup of their fingers. She'd said she wanted to make the water shiver. She'd never made it to the river.

He did not name any of these thoughts. Naming them made them real. Real things cost something. The package was real. The contract was real. The silver in his pouch was real. The rest was noise.

The package burned a hole in his pocket. Not literally—he would notice that. He was Fire, and fire noticed heat, and the package was just paper and leather and wax. But it sat there like a hot coal, like a splinter, like the door in his chest that wouldn't stay closed. He didn't open it. He didn't shake it. He stood at the rail and watched the water and didn't name the feeling.

The island came into view. Green hills. Wind-twisted trees. The thin line of smoke rising from the cottage chimney. He knew the way. He'd walked it three days ago, down from the ridge, toward a woman who'd looked up from her garden with grey eyes and hands that never stopped moving. He'd walked it again yesterday, away from the cottage, toward the ferry, trying to leave the feeling behind. The feeling had followed him. The feeling was still there.

He walked up from the dock. Past the rock where the child had been fishing—empty now, the tide out, the shingle wet and gleaming. Up the path through the scrub. Past the first fork, the second, the goat that hated strangers. The cottage came into view. The garden. The rain barrel. The clothesline strung between two trees, empty now, the laundry taken in.

Renne was at the net again. Same frame. Same twine. Same hands, moving in the same rhythm, mending the holes. She didn't look up. She didn't need to. The water in the rain barrel stirred as he approached—her element responding to her, or to him, or to the space between them.

"Back," she said. Not a question.

"Back." He pulled the leather tube from his pocket. Held it out. "Package. Someone doesn't want to be named. Someone else doesn't want to be found. Contract says to deliver it here."

She looked at the tube. Her hands stilled on the net. "Who sent it."

"Don't know. Graen didn't know. Verrol didn't know. The person who gave it to Verrol was wearing a hood."

"Dramatic."

"Suspicious."

"Same thing." She took the tube. Turned it over in her hands. The wax seal was still intact. She didn't open it. She just held it, her fingers tracing the edges, the way his fingers traced coins when he was counting. "You didn't read it."

"No."

"You could have."

"I don't read other people's messages."

"That's either very principled or very stupid."

"Most things are both."

She looked at him. Her grey eyes were steady, unblinking, the same grey as the sea before a storm. The tube was still in her hands. She hadn't opened it. She didn't seem to want to.

"You brought it yourself," she said. "You could have sent someone else. You could have left it at the dock. You could have pretended the address was wrong and thrown it in the sea."

"Yes."

"Why didn't you."

He didn't have an answer. The splinter was still there. The door was still open. The feeling in his chest was still growing. He stood at the edge of her garden with his hands in his pockets and the cold in his fingers and the words locked behind a door he'd closed fifteen years ago. He didn't know how to open it. He didn't know if he wanted to.

"Someone's looking for you," he said. "Someone who knows how to find the Factorage. Someone who knows how to send messages through third parties. Someone who doesn't want to be named. That's either a friend or an enemy. Friends don't hide their names. Enemies do."

"I know."

"Then you know it's not safe here."

"It's never been safe here." She turned the tube over in her hands. "Safe is a lie the Order tells you. 'Come with us, we'll keep you safe.' 'Don't run, it's not safe out there.' Safe is a silk rope. Safe is a locked door. I've been not-safe for ten years. I'm still here."

"That doesn't mean you'll stay here."

"No." She looked up at him. "It doesn't." She tucked the tube into her belt. "Stay for dinner. Marta's making stew again."

"I don't—"

"You're too thin. Sit down."

He sat down. He didn't know why. The net hung on its frame. The water stirred in the rain barrel. The cottage chimney trailed smoke into the grey sky. Somewhere inside, Marta was stirring a pot with her steady hands, and Corin was fidgeting on his stool, and the girl with the shaking fingers was practicing her vortex.

He sat on the stump at the edge of the garden and watched Renne mend her net. The package was in her belt. She hadn't opened it. She didn't seem afraid. She didn't seem anything except present—her hands moving, her eyes on the twine, her breathing steady and slow. He watched her hands. He always watched hands.

The sun began to set. The stew began to smell like fish and root vegetables and herbs he still didn't recognize. He didn't leave. She didn't tell him to.
