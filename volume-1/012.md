# Chapter 12: The Others

The cottage was bigger than it looked from the ridge. The main room had a hearth, a table, three chairs that didn't match. A pot was bubbling on the fire—something with fish and root vegetables and herbs Hark didn't recognize. The smell filled the room. His stomach made a sound he was glad no one heard.

Renne had left him in the garden until the light was fully gone. Then she'd stood, stretched, and said "Come inside" without looking to see if he'd follow. He followed. He didn't know why. Her hands were still moving—wiping dirt from her trousers, checking the net one last time, brushing a strand of dark hair from her face—and the motion was familiar in a way that made his chest hurt. He followed anyway.

The first one was already in the cottage when they entered.

Young. Lean. Perched on a stool near the door like he'd been waiting to bolt. His hands were fidgeting—fingers tapping his thighs, his belt, the edge of his sleeve. Lightning Gate. Hark could feel it before he saw the tell: the air around the boy was slightly unsettled, a faint pressure differential that shifted when he moved. Pulse mutation, probably. The living communication relay. His gate was guarded but not well—the way a door is locked by someone who's only just learned how.

"You're the Fire-mage," the boy said. His voice was too fast, the words tumbling out ahead of each other. "The one Renne told us about. She didn't tell us you'd be so—" He stopped. Swallowed. Started again. "What do you want?"

Hark didn't answer. He was still standing in the doorway, still cataloguing. The boy's hands were shaking. Not from cold—from something else. Adrenaline. Fear. The particular tremor of someone who'd been running too long and hadn't learned how to stop. He'd seen that tremor before. He'd felt it in his own hands, the first year in the forest, when the fever spikes left him shaking and the cold was so deep he couldn't grip a blade.

Corin. Renne said his name like it was an afterthought, already moving toward the hearth. "He's been here three months. Pulse mutation. The League found him on a merchant ship, working passage. He'd been hiding in the cargo hold for two weeks."

"Three and a half," Corin said. "And I wasn't hiding. I was waiting."

"For what?"

"For the ship to stop." The boy's eyes were still scanning—the doorway, the window, Hark's hands. "It stopped. The League was waiting. I didn't know if they were Order or not. I almost jumped."

"But you didn't."

"No." Corin's hands slowed, fractionally. "Renne found me. Before I did anything stupid."

"Define stupid."

"Jumping off a ship in the middle of the archipelago with no boat and no plan." Corin's mouth twitched—not quite a smile, but close. "She said I'd drown before I hit the water."

"She was right."

"I know." The twitch faded. His hands started tapping again. "You're the one from the mainland. The mercenary. Soric's contact."

"I'm not Soric's contact. Soric talks. I listen."

"Same thing."

"Not even close."

The boy looked at him for a long moment. His eyes were pale, washed-out, the eyes of someone who'd been running for years and hadn't slept well in any of them. He was young—nineteen, maybe twenty. Old enough to have escaped the Order, young enough to still be angry about it. The anger was there, under the fidgeting. A low hum. A vibration in the air around him.

"You're Fire," Corin said. "Light mutation. The scars—"

"Are none of your business."

The words came out flatter than Hark intended. The boy flinched. The air pressure in the room spiked—a small thing, quickly controlled, but Hark felt it in his eardrums. Renne looked up from the hearth. Said nothing. Kept stirring the pot.

"Fine," Corin said. "None of my business. You're here now. That's something." He stood up from the stool. His hands were still tapping, but slower now, the rhythm evening out. "Marta's in the back. She's going to make you eat. Don't argue with her. No one wins."

He slipped out the door. The air pressure in the room settled back to normal. Hark watched him go—the lean shoulders, the restless hands, the way his weight shifted with every step like he was still on a ship's deck. He'd been running longer than Hark, maybe. Or maybe he just wore it differently. Some people ran with silence. Some people ran with noise. The boy was noise.

The second one was older.

She was at the back of the cottage, near the hearth, stirring the pot with a wooden spoon. Earth Gate. Hark felt it as soon as he stepped closer—a deep, slow resonance, the particular weight of someone whose element was structure and persistence. Bone mutation, if he was reading the debt-pattern right. Her hands were steady. The spoon moved through the stew in slow circles, never splashing, never stopping. The motion was practiced. Old. The kind of steady that came from decades of doing something and no longer needing to think about it.

Marta. Renne didn't introduce her. She didn't need to. The old woman looked up when Hark approached, and her eyes were pale and clear and utterly without fear. She looked at him the way Joel had looked at him—like she was seeing the shape of him, the wear patterns, the places where he'd been broken and put back together.

"You'll stay for dinner," she said. It wasn't a question.

Hark opened his mouth to say no. He had reasons. He always had reasons. He didn't eat with other people. He didn't sit at other people's tables. He didn't let other people feed him. Food was fuel. Food was function. The rest was noise.

"You're too thin," Marta said. "Sit down."

He sat down. He didn't know why.

Her hands were steady. That was why. The spoon kept moving. The stew kept bubbling. The fire kept crackling in the hearth, and the warmth was ordinary—no magic, just wood and flame—and Marta's hands were the hands of someone who'd outlived everything that tried to kill her and was still here, still stirring, still steady. Joel's hands had shaken. At the end, when the blood cancer was in his bones, he couldn't hold a cup without spilling. The wine would run down his chin—red, then clear when he switched to water—and he'd wipe it away with the back of his hand and make a joke about it.

"Body's a bad contract," he'd said, coughing into a rag. "Termination clause came early. Didn't even give me notice. Just showed up one morning and started repossessing the furniture." He'd held up his shaking hand. "Look at that. Can't even hold a cup. Pathetic."

Hark had held the cup for him. Steady. He could be steady. He had learned to be steady. The cold in his fingers was permanent, but the cold didn't make him shake. The cold just sat there, a weight in his bones, a ledger that never stopped tallying. He could hold a cup. He could hold a sword. He could hold a girl's hand while the light faded from her eyes and the pressure collapsed and the silence swallowed everything.

He had been steady for Joel. He had been steady for Lea. He had been steady for fifteen years, and the steadiness was a wall, and the wall was still standing.

Joel had noticed. Even at the end, when the cancer was in his lungs and the words came out wet and ragged, he'd noticed. "You'll outlast me, kid," he'd said. "You're too stubborn to die."

Hark had not corrected him. He was not stubborn. He was just too afraid to stop.

Renne set a bowl in front of him. Stew. Root vegetables, some kind of white fish, herbs he didn't recognize. The steam rose into his face. His hands stayed in his lap. He didn't reach for the bowl. He didn't know why.

"Eat," Marta said. Still stirring. Still not looking at him.

He ate.

The stew was warm. His chest was warm. The cold in his fingers did not recede—it never receded—but it stopped spreading. The numbness stayed where it was, at the edges, and didn't creep further. That was something. He didn't name it. He ate, and the bowl emptied, and Renne sat across from him and ate her own stew and didn't watch him. Her eyes were on her food, or the fire, or the window where the night was pressing against the glass. She didn't watch him eat. She didn't ask questions. She just sat there, her hands finally still around her own bowl, and let him be.

That was why he stayed. He didn't name that either.

He slept in the cottage that night. On the floor, by the hearth, because Marta had pointed at a pile of blankets and said "There" and he'd gone there without arguing. He didn't argue with Marta. No one did, apparently. Corin had been right about that.

The fire burned low. The cottage settled into the quiet of old timbers and sleeping bodies. Corin was in the next room, his breathing uneven even in sleep. Renne was somewhere—he didn't know where, he hadn't watched her go. Marta was in a chair by the hearth, a blanket over her lap, her eyes closed but her hands still moving slightly, the spoon forgotten, her fingers tracing patterns on the armrest. Bone memory. The body remembering motions the mind had stopped ordering.

Marta put a blanket over him without asking. He didn't say thank you. He didn't know how. The words were there, somewhere, but they were locked behind the same door as everything else, and he didn't open doors. He didn't know how to open doors.

He didn't sleep. He watched the fire. The flames flickered—orange, gold, the deep blue at the base where the heat was hottest. He could feel the warmth on his face, his hands, the blanket pulled up to his chest. The fire didn't speak to him. He was Fire, and fire spoke to Fire, but this was ordinary flame—no magic, no debt, no working. Just wood burning. Just heat rising. Just the quiet crackle of a thing consuming itself and asking nothing in return.

It was the first time in fifteen years he had slept near other people. He didn't dream of Lea. The grey eyes, the bolt, the pressure collapsing—none of it came. He waited for it. He braced for it. The fire burned low. The cottage creaked. Somewhere outside, the wind was moving through the scrub, and the sea was crashing against the rocks, and the world was still turning.

He didn't dream of Lea. He didn't know what to do with that. He stared at the fire until the embers were the only light left, and then he stared at the embers, and then the grey light crept through the window and the night was over.

He was still there. He hadn't left. He didn't know what to do with that either.
