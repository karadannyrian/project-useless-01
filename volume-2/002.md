# Chapter 2: The Heron on the Street

The door swung shut behind her. The lock clicked. The sound was familiar — she heard it every evening, the same mechanism, the same finality. The branch facility locked from the outside. It always had.

The street was grey. Late afternoon, the light already fading, the harbour a smudge of darker grey beyond the rooftops. The fishing boats were coming in — she could hear the bells, the shouts, the gulls screaming their idiot hunger. The salt air cut through the wool of her coat. She pulled it tighter. The Order issued coats to all personnel. Grey wool. Grey like the walls. Grey like the door. Grey like everything else.

The man in worn leathers was standing outside the Factorage.

He hadn't been there when she'd looked through the window an hour ago. He'd gone inside, done whatever business he had, come back out. Now he was watching the door. Not the street. Not the harbour. The door. The iron sign swung overhead — scales with one pan broken. He stood beneath it like he belonged there, like he'd been standing beneath broken scales his whole life.

She should keep walking. Her rooms were in the other direction — a boarding house on Anchor Street, a room with a window that faced the harbour, a lock that worked, a kettle she'd bought herself. The kettle was copper. It was the only thing in the room that wasn't grey.

She crossed the street.

"You're asking about things you shouldn't."

He turned.

He was older than she'd thought. Not old — thirty, maybe, the bones of his face sharp under the skin, the jaw clean-shaven in a way that meant he cared about appearances. That was the first thing she noticed. The second was his eyes. Grey. The same grey as the harbour, the same grey as the sky, the same grey as her own. He had the look of someone who'd been handsome once and had stopped caring, or had started caring about something else.

His debt-signature was nothing.

She'd seen it from the window — the absence, the blank space, the hole cut out of the world. Up close it was worse. Most people carried some residue. Environmental debt, the faint wash of magic worked near them, the echo of someone else's borrowing — a haze of colour that clung to skin and cloth like smoke. He had none of it. Not grey. Not white. Just absence. The visual equivalent of a held breath.

Insensitive. That explained the blankness. But it didn't explain the rest of it — the way the absence felt active, deliberate, like a door that had been locked from the inside.

He was performing.

"The knight is confronted by a woman with grey eyes." His voice was lighter than she'd expected. Quick. Almost musical. He spread his hands — the gloved left, the bare right — like an arena announcer addressing a crowd that wasn't there. "Odds uncertain. The crowd leans forward. Will she draw steel? Will she walk away? The tension is unbearable."

She didn't react.

The silence stretched. His hands stayed spread. The performance hung in the air between them, waiting for a response that didn't come. She watched his face. The grey eyes. The sharp bones. The clean-shaven jaw. He'd expected her to react. He'd expected her to be thrown, or amused, or offended. He'd expected something.

She gave him nothing.

The performance faltered. Not much — a flicker at the corner of his mouth, a slight shift in his weight. He recovered quickly. He was good at recovering. But she'd seen it. The crack in the facade. The person underneath, looking out.

He wasn't used to that. She filed it away.

"You're Insensitive," she said.

"And you're a Savant." He didn't ask. "The grey eyes give it away. That, and the way you're looking at me like I'm a puzzle you're trying to solve."

"I read debt. You don't have any."

"Is that a compliment?"

"It's an observation."

He smiled. The smile was part of the performance — quick, practiced, the smile of someone who'd learned to charm his way out of corners. But the grey eyes didn't smile. The grey eyes were watching her the same way she was watching him. Two people mapping each other's exits.

"What gave me away?" His voice was still light. Still performing. But there was something underneath now. A thread of genuine curiosity. "The sword? The leathers? The roguish good looks?"

"The way you stand."

That stopped him. The smile didn't fade, but it froze. A fraction of a second. She'd surprised him again.

"You stand like someone who's been in a lot of rooms he wasn't supposed to be in," she said. "Exits first. Threats second. The performance third. You're talking to me like I'm an audience, but you haven't taken your eyes off the street."

He looked at her. The performance was still there — it was always there, she was starting to understand, it was the water he swam in — but something had shifted behind it. An appraisal. A recalibration.

"And you stand like someone who's spent a lot of time in libraries and not enough in the street." He tilted his head. The grey eyes caught the fading light. "Except your hands. Your hands say something else."

She looked down. Her hands were at her sides. Still. Clean. The hands of a clerk, a noble daughter, a woman who'd never done anything but file reports. He was wrong about the hands.

Wasn't he?

"What do my hands say?"

"That you've been holding something heavy for a long time." He said it without the performance. Just the words. Just the grey eyes. "And you haven't decided whether to put it down."

The harbour bell rang. The gulls screamed. The fishing boats were at the docks now, the day's catch coming in, the street filling with men and nets and the smell of salt and blood. The world was still moving. It had never stopped.

She should report him.

Insensitive, no debt-signature, asking questions about things he shouldn't. Possible Order fugitive. Possible League asset. Possible nothing — just a man with grey eyes and a sword and a performance he wore like armour. She should file the report. She should describe his face, his gait, the heron sigil on his collar that she'd noticed without meaning to, small and silver and easy to miss if you weren't looking for it. House Corway. Minor nobility. Something flagged in the files — *possible exposure to precursor artifacts.* She'd seen the name in a footnote once, a report from the Cradle archives, a whisper of old secrets and older lies.

She should report him. The form was in her desk. The pen was beside it. The ink was still wet from the last child's name.

She didn't.

The omission was a choice. She'd made it before she knew she was making it. The ledger would note the gap. The building's foundations, already cracked, now carried one more stress point. She'd been standing on them for three years. She was still standing.

He saw it. The decision, or the hesitation, or something in her face that she hadn't learned to hide. The performance flickered again. The man underneath looked out — briefly, unguarded, something almost like surprise in the grey eyes.

"You're not going to report me."

"I haven't decided."

"You've decided." He wasn't smiling now. "You just haven't admitted it yet."

He stepped back. The movement was fluid — a fighter's movement, weight shifting from the balls of his feet to his heels, distance opening between them. He was giving her room to change her mind. Or he was giving himself room to run.

"The east door," he said. "At the branch. It's unguarded after the fourth bell."

She went still.

"If someone wanted to leave — hypothetically — they'd need a route. The east door. Fourth bell. The handler rotation changes then. There's a gap. Seven minutes, give or take." He shrugged. The performance was back, but it was gentler now. Less arena. More confidence. "Hypothetically."

"How do you know that?"

"I pay attention." He turned. The sword on his back caught the grey light — plain steel, no decoration, the weapon of someone who used it. "The heron remembers. That's what they say. I'm not sure I do anymore. But I remember the things that matter."

He walked away. The crowd swallowed him — fishermen and dockworkers and merchants closing their stalls, the ordinary chaos of a harbour town at dusk. The heron sigil caught the light once, a brief flash of silver, and then he was gone.

She stood outside the Factorage. The broken scales swung overhead. The harbour bell rang again. The gulls screamed. The world kept moving.

*If someone wanted to leave — hypothetically — they'd need a route.*

She walked to the harbour railing. The water was grey. The ships were grey. The sky was grey. Her mother's eyes were grey — the same grey, the same water, the same sky. She'd stood at this railing a hundred times. She'd never once thought about the east door. The handler rotation. The seven-minute gap.

She was thinking about it now.

The heron was still on his collar. She'd seen it — small, silver, the bird in flight, the long neck curved toward something she couldn't see. House Corway. Minor nobility. Precursor artifacts. A man who stood like he'd been in rooms he wasn't supposed to be in. A man who knew about the east door.

She should report him. The form was still on her desk. The pen was still beside it. The ink was still wet.

She didn't move.

The omission was a choice. She'd made it. The ledger would note the gap. The building's foundations carried one more crack. The door was grey. The walls were grey. The water was grey.

She stood at the railing and watched the ships and didn't think about the east door. She didn't think about it for a long time.

The heron was still on his collar. She was still standing at the railing. The world was still grey.

But the kettle in her room was copper. And she'd chosen not to write the report. And the man with grey eyes had seen her hands and said: *you've been holding something heavy for a long time.*

He wasn't wrong.

She stayed at the railing until the light failed. Then she walked back to the boarding house. The kettle was copper. The window faced the harbour. The lock worked.

The east door was unguarded after the fourth bell.

She didn't think about it. She made tea. The steam was white. The cup was warm. Her hands were cold.

They were always cold.
