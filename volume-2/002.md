# Chapter 2: The Heron on the Street

The branch emptied at the fifth bell. Alex waited until the corridors were quiet—the handlers gone, the clerks gone, the senior assessors retreating to their quarters in the upper floors. She locked her office. Walked the long corridor to the main door. The walls were grey. She didn't look at the kitchen door as she passed it. She always didn't look at the kitchen door.

Outside, the light was fading. Late afternoon, the sun already dropping behind the harbour, the water turning from grey to something darker. The fishing boats were coming in. The gulls were screaming. The street smelled of salt and tar and the particular rot of low tide.

The man in worn leathers was gone. She hadn't expected him to still be there—the Factorage closed earlier than the branch—but she looked anyway. The street was empty. The iron sign above the Factorage door swung in the wind off the water. Scales with one pan broken.

She walked toward the harbour. Not because she had a destination. Because the harbour was open and the branch was not, and after an assessment day she needed to be somewhere that didn't have walls.

He was standing outside the Factorage.

Not the same spot. Further down the street, near the net-drying racks, his back against a piling. He was watching the Factorage door the way she watched the Silk Ward stairs—indirectly, from an angle, with the stillness of someone who'd learned to wait. His left hand was in his pocket. His right was at his side. The glove was still on.

She crossed the street before she'd decided to cross it. She was good at that—acting before the hesitation could catch up. The hesitation always caught up later, in her office, in the dark, when the door was grey and the forms were filed and the paper was still blank.

"You're asking about things you shouldn't."

He turned.

He was older than she'd thought—not old, just worn. Thirty, maybe. His face was lean, the bones sharp under the skin, the jaw clean-shaven. He had the look of someone who'd been handsome once and had stopped caring. There was a thin scar along his left cheekbone, old and white, the kind that came from a blade rather than an accident. His eyes were grey—the same grey as the harbour, the same grey as the sky, the same grey as her own. He looked at her the way a duelist looked at an unfamiliar opponent. Measuring. Calculating. Deciding whether to perform.

"The knight is confronted by a woman with grey eyes," he said. His voice was dry, almost amused, the voice of someone who'd learned to turn everything into a performance. "Odds uncertain. The crowd leans forward."

The arena. He was doing arena commentary. She recognised the rhythm—the false gravitas, the self-mocking undertone. He was making a joke of himself before she could. That was a deflection. She knew deflections. She used them herself.

"The crowd is easily impressed," she said.

"All crowds are easily impressed. That's what makes them crowds."

She looked at his hands. The left was gloved—old leather, worn soft at the palm, the stitching visible along the thumb. The right was bare. No calluses from rope or blade, but the fingers were still in a way that meant training. Red Coin School, if she had to guess. The Single Exchange required stillness. This man had stillness in his hands.

She read him.

And stopped.

The debt-signature should have been there—everyone had something, even non-mages. Environmental residue. The faint wash of magic worked near them. The echo of someone else's borrowing. Most people carried a thin grey film, barely visible, the accumulation of living in a world where magic was everywhere.

He had nothing. Not grey. Not white. Not pale or faint or barely-there. Absence. A hole in the ledger. She'd never seen anything quite like it. The closest was the place where debt should be—the shape of a person, the outline of a life—but the space inside was empty. He was Insensitive. That explained it. But the blankness still unsettled her, the way a room unsettled you when you expected a door and found a wall.

"You're reading me," he said.

"Yes."

"What do you see."

"Nothing." She met his eyes. "That's the problem."

Something shifted in his face. Not surprise—he was too controlled for surprise—but something adjacent to it. The performance flickered. The arena commentary stopped.

"Most Savants don't admit that," he said.

"I'm not most Savants."

"Clearly." He leaned back against the piling. The motion was casual, but his weight was still forward, still balanced. Still ready. "You're Order. Grey tabard. Assessment division, from the way you're looking at my hands. You've been reading children all day, and now you're reading me. What's the verdict?"

"That depends on what you're doing outside the Factorage."

"Asking questions."

"Questions about what."

"Ogres. Caravan routes. The price of silver in Salthaven." His mouth twitched—not a smile, the suggestion of one. "Harmless things."

"Harmless things don't require standing in the street for an hour."

"Maybe I like the view."

"Maybe you're lying."

"Almost certainly." He pushed off the piling. The motion was fluid, economical, the same fighter's economy she'd noticed from the window. His left hand stayed in his pocket. His right stayed at his side. "You're not going to report me."

"How do you know."

"Because you've been standing here for five minutes and you haven't asked my name." He stepped past her. The heron on his collar caught the fading light—small, silver, easy to miss if you weren't looking for it. She was looking. She was always looking.

"House Corway," she said.

He stopped.

"The sigil. The heron. Your family's crest." She'd seen that name in the files. Minor nobility. Something flagged in the margins—*possible exposure to precursor artifacts. Recommend observation.* She'd read the file six months ago and filed it away in the part of her memory she reserved for things that didn't add up. "You're a long way from home."

"So are you. House Rubbert. The shipyards. Your family built half the League's navy." He didn't turn around. His voice was still dry, still performing, but there was something underneath it now—a sharper edge, a note of warning. "We all have things we don't want to be asked about."

He walked away. The street swallowed him—past the net-drying racks, past the fish market, toward the harbour and the ships and the grey water beyond.

Alex stood at the railing. The harbour was darkening. The fishing boats were in. The gulls had settled. She should report him. He was asking questions outside the Factorage. He was wearing a Corway sigil. He'd been flagged in the files. He'd walked away from an Order assessor without giving a name or a reason.

She didn't report him. The omission was a choice. She'd made it before she knew she was making it.

The east door was unguarded after the fourth bell. If someone wanted to leave the Order—hypothetically—they'd need a route. They'd need a place to go. They'd need someone who knew how to read a file and not file a report.

She didn't finish the thought. The harbour was grey. The ships were grey. The sky was grey. Her hands were cold.

She walked back to the branch. The kitchen door was still there. The stairs were still there. The children were still there. The paper was still blank.

She didn't write the report.
