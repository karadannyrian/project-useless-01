# Chapter 5: The Road

The road unspooled ahead of them, grey and empty, the Confederacy's enormous sky pressing down on the plains like a held breath. Dawn had given way to morning, pale light seeping through the cloud cover, the sun a rumour somewhere behind the grey. Hark walked. Renne walked beside him. The farm was gone.

He didn't look back. Looking back was a door he'd closed a long time ago. The farm was small. That was the point. Small meant invisible. Invisible meant safe. Safe meant—he stopped the thought before it finished. Old habit. The ropewalk room. Salthaven. The Factorage. The Weeping Dog. All of them behind him now. All of them still there, still standing, still full of people who didn't know his name and wouldn't remember his face.

The road was empty. Good. Empty roads meant no witnesses. No witnesses meant no questions. No questions meant no lies he had to remember later.

Inventory. Sword—Joel's blade, plain steel, no name, no history. Balanced right. The grip was warm against his back, his body heat bleeding into the leather. Knife—belt, left side. Crossbow—new string, three bolts, the stock worn smooth from use. Smoke pellets—two, clay spheres the size of walnuts, saltpetre and dried kelp. Wire—twenty feet, coiled tight in the pack.

The weight was right. He'd counted twice.

Renne didn't speak. He didn't need her to. The silence between them was a language—built over months, over the farmhouse, over the long recovery from the cave. She'd spent three weeks flushing the debt from her lungs, and he'd spent three weeks pretending he wasn't watching her breathe. They'd learned to talk without words somewhere in there. The silence was a tool. Most people couldn't sit in it. She could. That was why they worked.

Her hand brushed his. Not holding—just brushing. A reminder. She was there. Her fingers were cool against the leather of his glove. He didn't pull away. He'd stopped pulling away somewhere between the cave and the farm. He didn't remember when.

The cold in his fingers was worse on the road. It was always worse on the road. The frost-scars ached—the oldest ones, the ones from the Cradle, the ones from the forest, the ones Lea had traced in the dark of the dormitory when they were nine years old and still believed they might survive together. Those scars were deep. They'd been there so long they were just part of him now, like his pulse, like his breath, like the furnace in his chest that never fully shut down.

He didn't think about the girl. The one they were walking toward. The one Marta's network had been tracking for weeks. The one the Order was already hunting.

He thought about the girl.

She was young. A Fire Gate, probably. Sensitive, probably. The Order wanted her because the Order wanted every Sensitive, and this one was important enough to chase into the desert. That meant she was either powerful or connected or both. That meant the hunters would be good. That meant the odds were bad.

Bad odds. Not worth the trouble. Going anyway.

The road stretched ahead. Grey sky. Grey fields. The Confederacy was flat and wide and indifferent, the kind of place that made you feel small even when you weren't running from anything. He'd crossed it a hundred times—caravan guard, courier, mercenary for hire. He'd walked these roads with a dozen different names and a dozen different contracts and the same cold in his fingers every morning.

This was different. He wasn't walking toward a contract. He wasn't walking away from a hunter. He was walking toward a girl he'd never met, through a desert he'd never crossed, on the word of a network he'd never fully trusted.

Renne's hand brushed his again. Still there. Still not holding. Still a reminder.

He'd said okay. Three words. She'd asked him to stay, and he'd said okay, and that was fifteen years of running undone in a single syllable. He'd been running since the forest—through the Confederacy, through the Dominion, through the Archipelago and back again. He'd been running from the Order, from the debt, from the cold in his chest that he refused to name. He'd been running for so long that running had become the only thing he knew how to do.

The difference was that now he was running toward something. He didn't know what it was called. He was learning.

The farm was behind them. The well was behind them. The roof hatch he'd installed with his own hands—the rope knotted, the exit clear—was behind them. The lock he'd checked twice every night was behind them. The bed where Renne's hand reached for his side in the dark was behind them.

He didn't look back. He never looked back. But he carried the farm with him the way he carried the cold—permanently, without comment, a weight that had become so familiar it was almost comfort.

The road ahead was empty. The sky was grey. The desert was waiting.

"How long to the border," Renne said.

"Three days. Maybe four. Depends on the roads."

"Depends on the Order."

"Depends on the Order."

She nodded. Her hand found his—not brushing this time, holding. Her fingers were cool through the glove. She held on for three steps. Then she let go.

They walked. The road unspooled. The sky stayed grey.

His fingers were cold. They were always cold. He flexed them. The leather creaked. Renne was beside him. The road was ahead.

That was enough. That had to be enough.
