# Chapter 4: The Message

Cold in the fingers. Always cold.

Hark opened his eyes. The ceiling was wood — pine beams, the grain running east to west, a knot above the window shaped like a closed fist. He'd mapped it the first morning. He mapped it every morning. The farmhouse was three years old, built by his hands and Renne's and a neighbour who'd asked no questions and accepted payment in silver and a knife he'd never used. The ceiling was familiar. The cot was familiar. The woman beside him was still the least familiar thing in the room, and he'd been waking up next to her for three years.

Gloves. On. He flexed his fingers inside the leather. The numbness was there, same as always. The frost-scars were old now — the ones from the Cradle, the ones from the forest, the ones from fifteen years of running and hiding and spending heat he couldn't afford. They threaded from his fingertips to his elbows, white lines on pale skin, the ledger he'd never agreed to sign. The debt had settled into the joints years ago. It wasn't getting worse. It wasn't getting better. It was just there. The body remembering what the mind had learned to carry.

Blades. Boot knife, left calf. Belt knife, right hip. Joel's sword on the chair, where he'd left it. He reached over, pulled it free an inch, checked the edge. Still sharp. He'd honed it before sleep. He always honed it before sleep.

Exits. Door — locked, the bar in place. Window — shuttered, the latch secure. Roof hatch — accessible from the beam. He'd installed it himself. Old habit. The geometry of leaving, mapped fresh every morning. He'd built the farm. He'd put the roof hatch in with his own hands, measured the beam, fitted the hinge, tested the weight. A house he'd built from the ground up, and he'd still given himself a way out.

Joel's voice in his head. Gravel and phlegm. *Survive long enough. Never get caught again.*

He'd survived. He'd not been caught. He'd built a farm and planted a field and slept in the same cot for three years. The longest he'd stayed anywhere since the carriage. The longest he'd stayed anywhere since ever. The ropewalk room had been a waystation. The farm was—

The farm was where Renne was.

He pulled on his boots. Left first. Then right. The leather was newer than the gloves — two years, maybe three. Good stitching. The cobbler in town had a daughter who was Sensitive, uncollected, hidden. Hark had paid double. The cobbler had asked no questions. A transaction. Clean.

Renne stirred. Didn't wake.

Her hand was on the blanket. Palm up. The scar on her thumb — twelve stitches, waxed cord, the first thing she'd ever mended on him. The first thing she'd touched without asking. Her fingers were curled slightly, the way they always curled when she wasn't holding anything. Restless, even in sleep. He'd watched those hands shape water into silver vortices. He'd watched them press against his leg in a cave and pull his blood back where it belonged. He'd watched them knead bread, mend nets, plant seeds, hold a girl's shaking fingers steady while she learned to shape a vortex of her own.

He looked at her hand. Didn't touch it.

Didn't not touch it.

He stood. The cot creaked. Renne's hand moved — a small shift, the fingers uncurling, reaching toward the space where he'd been. She didn't wake. The hand settled on the empty blanket and stayed there.

He walked to the window. The field was grey. Dawn was still deciding whether to arrive. The sky was the colour of old iron, the grass the colour of nothing. The well was a dark circle in the centre of the yard. He'd dug it himself. Twelve feet down, lined with stone, the water cold and clean and endless. The first thing he'd built. Water before walls. Renne had insisted. She'd been right.

The farm was small. That was the point. Small meant invisible. Invisible meant safe. Safe meant—

He stopped the thought before it finished.

---

She came from the road.

The courier. He saw her before she saw him — a shape at the edge of the field, moving with the steady gait of someone who'd walked a long way and wasn't stopping. She was in her forties, maybe older. Hard to tell with people who spent their lives on the road. Her face was weathered, the skin creased around the eyes and mouth, the kind of lines that came from squinting into wind and not complaining about it. Her coat was patched in three places — shoulder, elbow, hem — each patch a different fabric, each one stitched with the same practical hand. She had a crossbow on her back and a knife on her belt. She walked like someone who'd learned to wait.

Her hands were empty. Her eyes were pale — washed-out, the colour of old water, the kind of eyes that had seen too many bad roads and stopped being surprised by them. When she reached the gate she stopped. Didn't call out. Didn't knock. Just stood there, hands at her sides, waiting. The particular stillness of someone who'd delivered bad news before and knew that rushing it didn't help.

He opened the door.

"Hark."

"Inside."

She stepped through. Her boots left dust on the floorboards. Her eyes swept the room — door, window, hearth, table — and then settled on him. Not the sword. Not the gloves. Him. The way a professional looked at another professional. Assessing. Filing. Moving on.

"Kaelen sends his regards."

The name landed. The safe house in the Confederacy, years ago. The man who'd patched his ribs and asked no questions. The man who'd said the League had eyes everywhere and doors for people who needed them. The man whose words the woman on the ship had carried: *The tide is high at the Sign of the Broken Keel.* Hark hadn't opened that door. He'd walked away.

Now the door had walked to him.

"I didn't ask for his regards."

"No. You didn't." The courier reached into her coat and pulled out a folded paper. The seal was unbroken. The wax was plain — no sigil, no crest, nothing that would identify the sender if the message was intercepted. "He said you wouldn't take the door on the ship. He said you'd need a different one."

"I don't need any door. I have a farm."

"You have a farm." She looked around the room. The hearth. The table. The window with its view of the field and the well. "Nice farm. Quiet. Far from the Order. Far from the League. Far from everyone who'd want to find you." She set the paper on the table. "Kaelen's been tracking something. The network's been tracking something. A girl in the Dominion. The Order wants her. The Dominion wants her dead. She's fourteen."

"Not my problem."

"She's a Fire-mage with a mutation no one's ever catalogued. The Order wants to study her. The Pharaoh wants to bury her. She's been hidden in a desert monastery since birth. The monastery is no longer safe."

"Still not my problem."

The courier's pale eyes didn't blink. "Kaelen said you'd say that. He also said you'd want to know that the hunters are already moving. Silencers. A Savant. A priest who burns villages. The girl has no one. The network has assets in the region, but none of them have escaped the Order and lived. You have."

"I'm not an asset. I'm a farmer."

"You're a man who knows how to run from the Order and survive. The girl needs that. The network is asking."

The network. The safe houses and the pulse-relay stations and the hidden mages. The people who'd decided the Order's monopoly wasn't unbreakable. They'd been tracking this girl for weeks, maybe months. They'd been preparing. They hadn't just found out. They'd been waiting for the right moment to send a courier to a farm in the middle of nowhere with a message they knew he'd refuse.

"Tell Kaelen I'm not interested."

"I'm not a negotiator." The courier stood. Her chair scraped against the floorboards. "Kaelen said you'd say that too. He said to leave the message. He said you'd read it eventually."

She left. The door closed behind her. Her boots crunched on the gravel path. The gate swung shut. The field was grey. The sky was grey. The paper was still on the table, unopened.

The tide is high at the Sign of the Broken Keel. He'd stood at the rail of a ship with Renne's shoulder against his arm and watched the woman walk away. A door. An option. He hadn't taken it. He'd built a farm instead. He'd dug a well. He'd planted a field. He'd slept in the same cot for three years beside a woman who held his hand and didn't pull away.

The door had found him anyway.

---

Renne was packing.

He found her in the bedroom. The pack was on the cot — the same pack she'd carried out of the cave three years ago, the same worn canvas, the same frayed straps. She was folding a blanket. Her hands were steady — not the stillness of waiting, the calm of a decision already made. She didn't look at him. She didn't need to.

"No."

"Yes." She folded the blanket. Placed it in the pack. Reached for the next thing — a waterskin, the good one, the one that didn't leak. "You're going. I'm going with you."

"Your lungs."

"I know what my lungs can do." She looked at him now. Grey eyes. The same grey as the harbour, the same grey as the sky, the same grey as the first time he'd seen her, standing in the rain with water pooling in her palm. "I'm not a liability. I'm her best chance."

"The girl—"

"Has no one else. You haven't read the message."

He hadn't. The paper was still on the table. He walked back, picked it up, broke the seal.

Kaelen's handwriting. Small. Precise. The letters angled forward like he'd been writing in a hurry but refused to be sloppy.

*Hark —*

*Her name is Seres. Fourteen. Fire Gate, uncatalogued mutation. The Order doesn't have a name for it yet. The monks who raised her call it Null-Flame. She's been hidden in a monastery in the Dominion since birth. The monastery is compromised. Hunters are already moving. Silencers. A Savant. A priest of the Dominion who burns villages to flush out fugitives. The Order wants to study her. The Pharaoh wants her dead — she's third in line to the throne, and he didn't know she existed until six months ago.*

*I'm not calling in a debt. You don't owe me anything. You never took the door. That was your choice. This is a different thing.*

*The girl has no one. The network has people who can slow the hunters down. We don't have anyone who can get her out. You escaped the Order at ten years old and stayed free for fifteen years. You're the only person I know who's done that and lived.*

*I'm asking. Not the network. Me. The girl needs someone who knows how to run. If you're not that person anymore, burn the letter. I won't contact you again.*

*If you are — the monastery is at the edge of the Sun-Scorched Expanse. The name is in the second sheet. Renne will know how to find it.*

*—K*

He read it twice. Kaelen. The man who'd patched his ribs. The man who'd given him a name when he'd had no name to give. The man who'd offered a door and hadn't held it against him when he'd walked away. He wasn't calling in a debt. He was asking. The difference mattered.

The debt always settled. But this wasn't a debt. This was a choice.

Renne was still packing. Her hands were still moving. The scar on her thumb flashed in the grey light — twelve stitches, waxed cord, the first mending. She'd been packing before the courier arrived. She'd known. She'd probably known for weeks, watching the road, waiting for the shape of a woman with a crossbow and a message he'd refuse.

"We'll need supplies."

"I already packed."

"Water. Rations. The map of the Dominion—"

"In the side pocket. Next to the salt."

He looked at her hands. The scar. The way her fingers curled when she wasn't holding anything. She'd been packing while he was still reading the message. She'd known before the courier arrived. She'd known before he'd broken the seal. She'd probably known the moment she'd seen the woman at the gate, the pale eyes, the patched coat, the particular stillness of someone carrying bad news.

She'd asked him to stay. Three years ago, in a cave above the sea, her hand in his, her breathing still shallow from the healing she'd paid for with her lungs. *Stay.* Not a question. He'd said *okay.* Three words. Took fifteen years to get there.

Now she was packing. Now she was going. Now the farm was a place they'd been, not a place they'd stay.

"We'll need horses."

"Already arranged. The neighbour owes you—"

"I paid him for the roof."

"You paid him double. He remembers." She closed the pack. Cinched the straps. Looked at him. "You're not running. We're moving toward something. There's a difference."

He didn't answer. The farm was quiet. The field was grey. The well was a dark circle in the grass. The roof hatch was still there — the exit he'd built into a house he'd meant to stay in. Old habits. The geometry of leaving, mapped fresh every morning. He'd mapped it this morning. He'd map it tomorrow, on the road, in whatever room they found, in whatever shelter they built.

She was right. He wasn't running. He was moving toward something. The difference was harder to explain than he'd thought.

Renne's hand found his. The scar on her thumb pressed against his glove. She didn't say anything. She didn't need to. Her hands were warm. His were cold. The warmth didn't fix anything. It didn't need to.

"Kaelen said you'd know how to find the monastery."

"I know the region. The Expanse is desert. The monastery is at the edge of it — old, pre-Order, built into a canyon wall. I've seen it on maps." She squeezed his hand once. Released it. "It's a long way. We should leave before dawn."

"Tomorrow."

"Tomorrow."

She went back to the pack. Her hands were steady. The scar on her thumb moved with the rhythm of her work — folding, cinching, checking the straps. She'd been a healer once. She was still a healer. The lungs would hold. They had to.

Hark walked to the window. The field was grey. The well was a dark circle. The courier was gone. The farm was quiet. Tomorrow they would leave. Tomorrow the road would open. Tomorrow a girl in a desert monastery would become his problem, his job, his choice. Not a contract. Not a debt. Something else. Something he didn't have a word for yet.

He checked the lock on the door. Twice. Old habit.

Then he went back to the cot and sat down and waited for Renne to finish packing.

Her hand was still on the blanket. He didn't touch it.

He didn't not touch it.

He sat there. That was enough.
