# Chapter 4: The Message

Cold in the fingers. Always cold.

Hark opened his eyes to the farmhouse ceiling—grey light through the shutters, dust motes turning in the cracks. The beam above the bed was new. He'd raised it himself, six months ago, when the old one started bowing. Renne had held the ladder. Marta had handed him nails one at a time, patient, unhurried, the way she did everything.

Renne was still asleep. Her hand was on the blanket, fingers curled loose, the scar on her thumb pale in the morning light. Her breathing was slow and even. No whistle. The debt from the healing had flushed out months ago, but he still listened for it. Old habit. He'd been listening for her breath since the cave.

Gloves. On. He pulled them over the frost-scars—white lines on white skin, the ledger written in flesh. The leather was new. Renne had traded for it in the village, three months back, and he'd stitched the seams himself. Twelve stitches. Waxed cord. He flexed his fingers. The numbness was there. It was always there.

Blades. Boot knife, left calf. Belt knife, right hip. Joel's sword on the chair, plain steel, no name, no history. He pulled it free an inch. The edge was sharp. He'd honed it before sleep. He always honed it before sleep.

Exits. The door—locked, the bar in place. The window—shuttered, the latch hooked. The roof hatch—clear, the rope knotted the way he'd left it. He'd installed the hatch himself. Renne had asked why a farmhouse needed a roof exit. He'd said old habits. She'd said nothing. She'd understood.

He checked the lock twice.

The farm. Not the ropewalk room. The ropewalk room was gone—Salthaven, the Factorage, the Weeping Dog, all of it behind him. The farm was small. That was the point. Small meant invisible. Invisible meant safe. Safe meant—he stopped the thought before it finished. Old habit. He didn't name things. Naming things made them real. Real things cost something.

Renne stirred. Her hand moved on the blanket, reaching for his side of the bed. Empty. Her eyes opened. Grey eyes. Grey like a winter sky, grey like the sea before a storm.

"Someone's coming."

He'd heard it before she spoke. Footsteps on the road—single set, steady, not trying to hide. The rhythm of someone who wanted to be heard before they were seen.

"Courier," Renne said. She was already sitting up, already reaching for her boots. "Marta's network."

"How do you know."

"The footsteps. Too steady for a farmer. Too slow for a hunter. She wants us to know she's coming."

Hark moved to the window. The road was grey in the dawn light, the field beyond it grey, the well a dark circle in the centre of the yard. The woman at the gate was in her forties, maybe older—hard to tell with people who spent their lives on the road. Her face was weathered. Her coat was patched in three places. She had a crossbow on her back and a knife on her belt and the particular stillness of someone who'd learned to wait. Her eyes were pale, washed-out, the kind of eyes that had seen too many bad roads and stopped being surprised by them.

Her hands. Clean. Callused from rope and leather, not from magic. The fingers were still. Not the stillness of hiding—the stillness of patience. She wasn't a mage. She was just a woman carrying a message.

Hark opened the door before she knocked.

"Marta sent you."

The courier didn't blink. "Girl. Desert. Order coming. You're the only one who knows how to run."

The words came out without inflection. The rhythm was Marta's—clipped, efficient, no syllables wasted. The courier had memorised them. She'd probably memorised a dozen messages, each one wrapped in a different voice, each one waiting for the right door to open.

Hark read her hands again. Still clean. Still patient. The crossbow on her back was well-maintained—the stock oiled, the string new, the bolts fletched with grey feathers. She'd travelled light. She'd travelled fast.

"How long has the network been tracking her."

"Weeks. Maybe months. Marta's been watching the Dominion's intelligence. The girl was flagged before the Order knew she existed." The courier's voice was flat. "This isn't coincidence. This is the network doing its job."

Weeks. Maybe months. The network had been sitting on this information, waiting for the right moment to call him in. They hadn't panicked. They hadn't rushed. They'd watched, and assessed, and then they'd sent a courier with Marta's words in her mouth.

He filed the information. The network was more organised than he'd thought. More patient. That was good. That was also dangerous. Patient organisations had long memories. Patient organisations kept ledgers.

"How long do we have."

"Don't know. The Order moves fast when they have a target. The desert slows them down. Could be days. Could be less."

Renne was already packing. Her hands moved through the supplies with the same rhythm she'd once used to shape water—precise, efficient, never still. She didn't look at him. She didn't need to.

"We're going," she said. Not a question.

"No."

"Yes."

"Your lungs—"

"Are fine." She folded a blanket. Tucked it into the pack. "The debt passed. You know it passed. You listen to my breathing every night."

He didn't answer. She was right. He'd been listening to her breathe since the cave. The whistle was gone. The debt had flushed out. The cost was paid.

"I'm not a liability." She turned to face him. Her grey eyes were steady. "I'm her best chance. A girl with a Fire Gate, running from the Order through the desert. She needs someone who knows how to hide. Someone who knows how to guard. Someone who can teach her what the Order never will."

"You."

"Me. And you." She pulled the pack straps tight. "You're the one who knows how to run. I'm the one who knows how to stay hidden. She needs both."

He looked at her hands. The scar on her thumb. The way her fingers curled when she wasn't holding anything. She'd been packing before the courier arrived. The blankets were already folded. The dried fish was already wrapped. She'd known he'd say no. She'd planned around it.

"Supplies," he said.

"I already packed."

"How long have you been waiting for this."

"Since the cave. Since Marta started building the network. Since I knew the Order would never stop, and neither would we." She pulled on her coat. "You're not the only one who plans for worst cases."

The courier was still at the gate. She hadn't moved. Her pale eyes tracked their conversation without comment. She'd delivered her message. The rest was not her problem.

"Tell Marta we're going," Hark said.

"She already knows." The courier turned. Walked back up the road. Her footsteps were steady, unhurried, the rhythm of someone who'd done this a hundred times and would do it a hundred more.

Hark stood by the window. The field was grey. The well was a dark circle. The farm was small. That was the point. Small meant invisible. Invisible meant safe. He'd built something here—a roof hatch, a locked door, a woman who packed supplies before he knew he needed them. He'd said okay. Three words. She'd asked him to stay, and he'd said okay, and that was fifteen years of running undone in a single syllable.

He didn't regret it. That was the strange part. He'd spent fifteen years regretting everything—the carriage, the silk, the handler's hands, the bolt through Lea's chest, the light through the instructor's. He'd built a wall of regret and called it survival. The wall was still there. But Renne was on the other side of it, packing supplies, ready to walk into a desert for a girl she'd never met.

He checked the lock twice. Old habit. The farm would still be here when they got back. Or it wouldn't. The farm didn't care. The farm was just a place. He'd learned the difference between a place and a home. He was still learning. He didn't name it. He didn't know how.

"Ready," Renne said. She was at the door. Pack on her back. Knife on her belt. Her hands were still—not the stillness of waiting, the calm of a decision already made.

"Ready."

They walked out together. The road was grey. The sky was grey. The well was behind them. The farm was behind them.

The girl was ahead. The desert was ahead. The Order was ahead.

His fingers were cold. They were always cold. He flexed them. The leather creaked. Renne's hand brushed his—not holding, just brushing. A reminder. She was there.

He didn't look back. He never looked back.
