# Chapter 3: The File

The branch was quiet at night. Not silent—the old warehouse had a voice of its own, the creak of settling timbers, the groan of stone cooling after a day in the harbour sun—but quiet enough that Alex could hear her own pen scratching across the paper. She'd been sitting at her desk for two hours. The form in front of her was blank. The file beside it was not.

House Corway. The heron sigil on the cover, stamped in silver foil. The file was thin—thinner than she'd expected for a noble house with a flagged note in the margins. She opened it.

Troy Corway. Third son. Age: thirty-one. Red Coin School, trained under Master Sera Kein. No magical ability. Insensitive. The debt-reading confirmed what she'd already seen on the street: a blank space where the ledger should be, a hole in the colours that surrounded everything else. He was immune. He'd always been immune.

Notable: Single Exchange proficiency—timed at under two-tenths of a second from neutral. She paused on that line. Two-tenths of a second. The Red Coin School trained for exactly that—the single strike, the decisive moment, the duel ended before it began. He'd achieved mastery, or close to it. That meant years of training. That meant discipline. That meant he could kill someone faster than most mages could open their gates.

No criminal record. No Order contracts. No formal affiliation with any School since leaving Master Kein's tutelage eight years ago. A note in the margin, handwritten, the ink a shade darker than the rest: *Possible exposure to precursor artifacts. Recommend observation.*

Precursor artifacts. The Order's most closely guarded category. Objects that predated the Cradle, the Order, the current civilization. Objects that were confiscated and buried in the vault and never spoken of. Debt-nulls—obsidian discs that allowed working without debt. Resonant bones—amplifying spell effect without increasing cost. The Still Children—petrified corpses that Savants read and saw nothing, not a blank, an absence.

House Corway had been exposed to them. Possibly preserved them. Possibly knew things the Order would kill to keep buried.

She closed the file. Opened it again. Read the note twice. The ink was old. The observation order had been filed years ago. No follow-up. No resolution. Someone had flagged a noble house and then forgotten about it, or been told to forget, or decided that forgetting was safer than pursuing.

She should report this. The protocol was clear: any contact with precursor artifacts required immediate notification of a Curator. The Silencers would come. The questioning would follow. The vault would open. The man with the heron sigil would disappear into the same silence that swallowed everyone who knew too much.

The children under the stairs—

She stopped the thought. The file was still open. Her pen was still in her hand. The form on her desk was still blank.

The dry-docks at dawn. The skeletons of half-built vessels against a pale sky. Her mother's grey eyes, the same grey as the water, the same grey as the sky. She'd been six years old, standing at the edge of the largest dock, her hand in her mother's hand. The ships rose around them like the ribs of enormous creatures, and the smell of tar and sawdust and salt was the smell of home, and she'd believed—truly believed—that the world was as solid as the keels her family laid down.

Before the Order. Before the guilt. Before she'd learned that buildings could be beautiful and rotten at the same time.

She pulled a fresh sheet of paper. Not a report. A letter.

*Mother—*

*The shipyards are still here. I can see them from my window. The light on the water in the mornings is the same light I remember from the dry-docks at dawn. I think about that light more than I should. I think about the way you used to stand at the edge of the largest dock, watching the hulls take shape, and I think about the way you never said what you were thinking. I know now why you didn't. Some things can't be said aloud. Some things have to be read between the lines.*

*The harbour is grey today. The fishing boats are out. The gulls are screaming. It's all very ordinary. It's all very—*

She stopped writing. The word she wanted was *wrong*, but she didn't write it. Her mother would read between the lines. Her mother always read between the lines.

*I met someone. A man with a heron on his collar. He made a joke about crowds. I didn't laugh. He wasn't used to that. I think he might be—*

She stopped again. The word she wanted was *dangerous*, but that wasn't right. He was dangerous. That was a fact. But there was something else underneath the danger, something she'd seen when she'd told him she saw nothing. The performance had flickered. The arena had gone quiet. For a half-second, he'd been just a man with grey eyes, looking at her like she was a person instead of a Savant.

*I think he might be useful. I don't know why I think that. I don't know why I'm writing this. I miss the light on the water. I miss the dry-docks at dawn. I miss you.*

*Your daughter,*
*Alex*

She didn't seal it. She left it on the desk, the ink still damp, the words still incomplete. She would finish it tomorrow. Or she wouldn't. The letter was like the file—open, unresolved, a door she hadn't decided whether to walk through.

She closed the Corway file. Locked it in the drawer. The heron sigil disappeared into the dark. The note in the margin—*possible exposure to precursor artifacts*—disappeared with it. The omission was a choice. She'd made it. She'd made it before she knew she was making it, on the street outside the Factorage, when she'd looked at a man with a hole in the ledger and decided not to ask his name.

She closed the file. She locked the drawer. She did not send a message. She did not tell anyone. The paper was blank. The ledger would not note it.

She would carry it instead.

The window was dark. The harbour was dark. The branch was quiet—the creak of timbers, the groan of stone, the distant lap of water against the docks. Somewhere below, in the place under the stairs, a child with frost-scarred hands was lying on a cot in the dark. *I'm used to it,* she'd said. Two and a half years, and Alex could still hear her voice.

She didn't go to the kitchen door. She didn't walk down the stairs. She didn't open the grey doors and read the debt-signatures and see the colours that never stopped, the colours that followed her into sleep and waited for her when she woke. She sat at her desk. The letter was open. The file was locked. The next child would arrive tomorrow.

The door was grey. The walls were grey. The light through the window was grey.

She was not.
