Two hours at the fourth mile marker, and the crowd was getting restless.

I could feel them in the stands of my mind—the imaginary spectators who'd been watching me since I was twelve years old, the ones who expected a show. The knight waits at the marker, odds uncertain, the sun bleeding out on the horizon like a wound that won't close. Is this the moment the escort arrives? Is this the moment the Order's Silencers crest the rise? The tension is unbearable. The knight agrees. The knight is tired of bearing it.

But the road stayed empty. The marker stayed silent. And I stayed exactly where I was, which was not at the fourth mile marker at all.

The ruse had been Father's idea. Graen gets a meeting point—the fourth mile marker, north road, dusk—and Graen tells the mercenary, and the mercenary plans around it, and if the mercenary is captured or followed or simply bought, the Order learns exactly where to send their people. But I wasn't at the fourth mile marker. I'd been in Salthaven since noon, tucked into the upper window of a derelict ropewalk that overlooked the Canal district, watching my half-brother buy supplies with a man who was supposed to be the best in the city.

The mercenary was hard to read. Wiry, watchful, moving with an economy that reminded me of the School—no wasted motion, no unnecessary weight shifts, the kind of body that had been trained to strike before it telegraphed. He kept the boy on his left, close to the wall, his sword-arm free. He counted coins like every one of them was a personal insult. He caught a pickpocket's wrist without looking and sent her off with advice instead of a broken hand.

And the knight observes his unknown ally with cautious approval. The mercenary is competent. The mercenary is perhaps more than competent. The crowd murmurs its interest. What School did this man train in? What war did he crawl out of? The knight does not know. The knight is not yet sure he trusts it.

I didn't trust it. Graen had called him "reliable," which in guild-speak meant "won't sell you out unless the price is very good." But Graen hadn't mentioned the gloves—thin leather, worn soft, the kind you wore to hide something. He hadn't mentioned the way the mercenary's left hand moved a fraction slower than his right, or the way he'd positioned himself between Rane and a carriage that rolled past with its windows set too high.

A man who shielded a child from carriages was a man who'd been in a carriage himself. I knew the type. I'd been raised to protect the type.

I followed them through the Supply borough. Through the clothier, where the mercenary spun a story about a country nephew and dressed my half-brother in grey wool and a deep hood. Through the chandler and the dry-goods stall, where Rane stood quietly while the mercenary haggled over smoked fish. The boy was behaving perfectly—no questions, no complaints, the kind of stillness that came from being raised on secrets. I felt a flush of pride. Then I felt the pride curdle into something else. He was ten years old. He shouldn't have to be this good at silence.

The crowd's mood darkens. The knight's mood darkens with it. Somewhere in the stands, a spectator mutters about the Order. The knight does not silence him. The knight would like to mutter about the Order himself.

They were cutting through the Canal district when the street went quiet.

I felt it before I understood it—a pressure-drop, a wrongness in the air. The gulls had stopped fighting. The vendors had stopped calling. The silence was spreading like a stain, and it was the wrong kind of silence. Not the silence of the marsh at dawn, the waiting silence, the heron's silence. This was the silence of a room with no breath in it.

The knight's hand finds his blade. The crowd holds its collective breath. Something is coming. Something—

The Cradle-Hound came around the corner like smoke poured from a jar.

It was smaller than I'd expected—mastiff-sized, grey hide, eyes the colour of wet stone. The resonance hit me a second later, a vibration in the chest that had nothing to do with magic and everything to do with the wrongness of a thing that had been bred to hunt what I'd been raised to protect. Behind it, two handlers in grey tabards. Silencers—I could tell by the ceramic plate, the treated gloves, the way they moved like people who'd been trained to kill mages and had done it before. Behind them, a woman in Order greys. A Savant. Her eyes were grey, and they were fixed on the mercenary, and something in her face shifted when she looked at him.

The odds have shifted. The crowd is on their feet. The knight is—

The mercenary moved.

I had never seen a mage fight. Mages didn't fight. Mages destroyed, suffocated, threw fire from a distance and called it battle. They didn't close to melee range. They didn't put a knife through a Silencer's throat before the crossbow cleared its holster.

This one did.

The heat-build was visible—a shimmer in the air around him, the kind of distortion that came from borrowing too much too fast. He crossed the distance in two steps when it should have taken five, and the male Silencer's training caught up a fraction of a second too late. The knife came from the small of his back. It went up under the jaw, where the tabard ended and the throat began. The Silencer dropped.

Clean strike. No follow-through. The crowd erupts. The knight is too stunned to join them.

The female Silencer was already moving. The mercenary grabbed Rane—grabbed him, slung him over one shoulder like a sack of grain—and then he burned again. Brighter this time. Hotter. The thermal updraft launched him over the canal railing in a single, brutal arc, the boy clinging to his neck, his left hand trailing frost as the debt settled in real time.

They landed on the opposite walkway. Stumbled. Nearly went into the water. The mercenary found his footing and kept running, and the frost-scars were spreading up his arms in white lines I could see from fifty paces, and the boy was still clinging, and the Savant was standing in the street with her mouth open, and I was—

The knight is moving. The knight has been moving since the mercenary cleared the railing. The knight is already circling behind the Order's position, because the mercenary has left loose ends, and loose ends are the kind of thing that get a ten-year-old boy killed before he reaches the fourth mile marker.

I'd been trained to wait. The heron waits. The Single Exchange waits. But the waiting was over. The mercenary had done his part—he'd gotten Rane out, burned his own body to do it, paid in frost and numbness and a debt that would take weeks to settle. Now it was my turn.

The female Silencer was shouting something—a signal, a report, a curse. The Savant was walking away, her back straight, her face unreadable. The Hound was whining at the canal railing, its resonance faltering. And the male Silencer was dead on the cobbles, which left exactly one loose end.

I followed her.

She was the tall one, the broad one, the one whose blade was built for chopping through armour. She'd drawn it when the mercenary launched, and she hadn't sheathed it since. She was moving east, toward the facility, her pace quick but not panicked. Reporting in. She'd report what she'd seen—the unmarked mage, the boy with the linen-wrapped eyes, the Savant who'd frozen at the wrong moment. The Order would escalate. More Silencers. More Hounds. A full retrieval team.

Unless she didn't report.

The knight stalks his prey through the narrowing streets. The crowd whispers its approval. This is what he was trained for. This is what the Single Exchange was made for. One action. No waste. The heron strikes.

She turned down a narrow alley between a tannery and a pawnshop. Dead end. She didn't know the city—she was walking the route the Hound had taken, retracing the chase instead of finding the fastest way back. A tactical error. I'd been mapping Salthaven since I arrived.

I came in behind her. She heard me. She was good—her weight shifted, her blade came up, her stance widened to meet the charge.

But I was already inside the arc.

The Single Exchange was not a strike. It was a decision. The decision happened in the silence before the motion, in the space where the waiting lived. I'd been waiting since the street went quiet. I'd been waiting since the mercenary's hand closed on his sword. I'd been waiting since Father told me about the vault, about the artifacts, about the secret the Corways had kept for six generations. The waiting was over.

The blade went through her throat before she registered the motion. One stroke. No telegraph. The Red Coin at its purest—a single clean action, and the fight was over.

She dropped. The chopping blade clattered on the cobbles. The alley was silent.

The crowd goes wild. The knight does not. The knight is already turning, already looking for the next threat, already—

The Savant was standing at the mouth of the alley.

She hadn't gone back to the facility. She'd followed. She was standing there in her Order greys, her hands empty, her winter-sky eyes fixed on the body of the Silencer and the blade in my hand and the blood on the cobbles. Her face was pale. Her mouth was open. She looked like someone who'd just watched a stranger kill an Order operative and was trying to decide if she was next.

I pulled the blade free. Cleaned it on the Silencer's tabard. Sheathed it.

"The crowd is waiting," I said. My voice was steadier than I felt. "Are you going to scream, or are you going to let me explain why I just did that?"

She didn't scream.

Her eyes moved from the body to my face. I saw her read me—the debt-signatures, the hands, the history written in the skin. I was Insensitive, debt-clean, nothing for her to see but the blankness of someone who'd never worked magic in his life. But she was looking at the sword. At the sigil on my collar—the heron, small and silver, easy to miss if you weren't looking for it.

"A Corway," she said. Her voice was hoarse. "You're a Corway."

"The knight is recognised. The crowd is intrigued. The odds have shifted again, and no one is quite sure in whose favour." And how did she had recognized a Corway from a glance? Savants did live up to their reputation.

Her eyes met mine. Grey, like the sky over the marsh at dawn. Grey, like the colour of the silence before the heron took flight. She was terrified. But under the terror, there was something else. The look of a woman who'd been given a choice she didn't know how to make.

"The boy," she said. "The one with the mercenary. He's yours."

"He's my half-brother. And if you're going to report what you just saw, I need to know now. Because the heron only strikes once."
