# Chapter 1: The Door Was Grey

The child's hands were small.

Alex held them between her own and let the debt-signature resolve. Wind Gate. First-deepening, barely. The pale grey shimmer of an unguarded reservoir — not the dull grey of damage, not the sick yellow of Affliction residue, just the natural leakage of a child who had never been taught to close the door. The colour was faintly luminous, the colour of a winter sky before snow. Clean. Undamaged. Frightened.

She released the hands. The child didn't pull away. They never pulled away. The handlers told them to cooperate, and the children learned, quickly, that cooperation meant the assessment ended faster. Cooperation meant the silk came off. Cooperation meant you got food, a cot, a blanket that didn't smell of the carriage.

"Wind Gate," Alex said. Her voice was steady. It was always steady. "First-deepening. No mutations. Debt is clean."

The clerk wrote it down. The scratch of the pen was the loudest sound in the room. Stone walls. A narrow window. Grey light through the glass — the harbour a smudge beyond it, the fishing boats already out, their lanterns smeared orange across grey water. The office was a converted storage room. It still smelled of salt and old rope, the previous tenants never fully evicted. Alex had stopped noticing the smell. She'd stopped noticing a lot of things.

The child was watching her. Dark eyes. Too still. The stillness of someone who'd learned not to move. Seven years old, maybe eight. Malnutrition did things to growth. So did fear.

"Are you going to put me in the place under the stairs?"

The question landed like a stone in still water.

The door was grey. Grey like the walls. Grey like the silk the handlers had removed before the assessment, white coils of it still pooled on the clerk's desk. Grey like the winter sky over the Rubbert yards. Grey like her mother's eyes, the morning the carriage came, the morning she'd stood at the window and watched and said nothing.

The door was grey.

"I don't know what you mean," Alex said.

The child nodded. Didn't believe her. Alex could see it in the set of the small shoulders — the particular tension of someone who'd learned that adults lied, that questions were dangerous, that knowing things was a currency you kept to yourself. The child had probably learned it in the carriage. They always learned it in the carriage.

The handlers came. Two of them — a woman with a face like a closed door, a man whose debt-signature carried the dull brown residue of old Affliction. He'd been used as a reservoir. Years ago, from the look of it. The colour was faded but permanent, the way all debt was permanent. Alex read him in the half-second before she looked away. She always read. She couldn't stop. The ledger was always there, an overlay on everything she looked at, colour and pattern and the particular texture of unpaid balance.

The woman took the child's arm. Gentle enough. The silk would go back on for transport — white silk, clean silk, silk that didn't leave marks. The child didn't resist. The door closed behind them. The clerk finished writing and left. Alex was alone with the form and the grey light and the question still hanging in the air like smoke.

*The place under the stairs.*

The children knew. They always knew.

She filled out the form. Name. Gate. Depth. Debt. Recommendations: full training curriculum. Her handwriting was steady. The letters were even, the spacing precise, the signature at the bottom a flourish she'd practiced until it was automatic. *Alexandria Rubbert, Assessor, Salthaven Branch.* The name meant something, once. House Rubbert of the Maelstrom League — shipbuilders, engineers, nobility. The youngest of five. The second daughter. The spare.

Now it meant a signature on a form. A woman in a converted storage room. A ledger of children's names she tried not to remember.

---

The Silk Wards. She'd found them by accident.

Two and a half years ago. A door behind the kitchens — grey, like all the doors in this facility, grey like the walls and the floors and the light through the narrow windows. Unlocked. She'd been looking for a store room. The clerk had said third door on the left, and she'd miscounted, or the clerk had miscounted, and she'd opened a door that led to stairs.

Going down.

The air changed as she descended. Cooler. Damper. Carrying a smell she couldn't immediately identify — mildew, old silk, and something else. Something that registered as a colour before she could name it. The dull grey of containment. The sick yellow of Affliction worked on children. The particular wrongness of debt that was never meant to exist.

The corridor was a line of identical doors. Grey. Numbered. Locked from the outside.

She opened the first one.

The girl was maybe twelve. Thin. Her hair was the colour of old straw, unwashed, matted at the back where she'd been lying down. Her hands were frost-scarred — the deep white of repeated Fire-debt, the kind that came from being used as a reservoir for years. The frost had climbed past her wrists, past her elbows, pale lines threading toward her shoulders. She was sitting on a cot. There was a basin. There was a blanket. There was nothing else.

She looked at Alex with eyes that had stopped expecting anything.

"I'm used to it," she said.

Not in answer to a question. Alex hadn't asked one. The girl had just seen the expression on her face — whatever expression it was, Alex still didn't know, still couldn't picture it — and offered the reassurance first. *I'm used to it.* The words of someone who'd learned that other people's distress was a problem she was expected to solve.

Alex closed the door. She stood in the corridor. The grey walls. The grey doors. The numbers. She didn't open any more of them. She knew what was behind them. Children who'd been deemed unsuitable for training but too valuable to release. Practice subjects. Reservoirs. Things.

She went back up the stairs. Closed the door behind her. Went to her office. The walls were grey.

She hadn't told anyone. She hadn't reported it. The paper was still blank.

---

The window was narrow. The harbour was grey.

The Rubbert yards were visible in the distance — the dry-docks, the cranes, the skeletons of half-built vessels against a pale sky. Her mother would be walking the yard floor now. Maren Rubbert, née Ashford, cartographer's daughter, the only person in the family who'd ever looked at Alex like she was a person and not a piece of furniture. Grey eyes. The same grey as the water. The same grey as the sky. The same grey as the door.

Before the Order. Before the carriage. Before the first child's hands in hers, small and frightened and already learning that adults lied.

She thought about writing a letter.

*Mother — The work is fine. The harbour is cold. I can see the yards from my window. I wonder if you're walking the floor. I wonder if you're thinking of me. I'm not sure I want you to be.*

She didn't write it. She never wrote it. The paper stayed blank. The pen stayed on the desk. The light stayed grey. The door stayed closed. The child's question stayed in the air, unanswered, unanswerable. *Are you going to put me in the place under the stairs?*

*I don't know what you mean.*

The lie sat in her chest like a stone. She'd been carrying stones for three years. She'd gotten very good at standing up straight.

---

He was walking toward the Factorage.

She saw him through the window — a man in worn leathers, moving with an economy that reminded her of something. A fighter's gait. No wasted motion. His left hand was gloved. The right was bare. The right hand was bare, and the debt-signature was—

Nothing.

No colour. No pattern. No luminosity. Just the absence where the overlay should have been, a blank space in the shape of a man, like a hole cut out of the world. Insensitive. Debt-clean. Nothing to read.

She'd never known what to do with that.

He was wiry. Watchful. His hair was dark, cut short, the kind of cut that meant he wanted to be unremarkable. His eyes were grey — not the grey of debt, not the grey of the harbour, just grey. Ordinary grey. The grey of a winter coat, of stone, of nothing in particular. He was wearing a sword on his back. The hilt was plain. No decoration. The weapon of someone who used it.

She watched him cross the street. He didn't look up at her window. He didn't look up at anything. He moved like a man who knew exactly where he was going and exactly how long it would take to get there. The walk of someone who'd mapped the exits. She recognized it. She mapped exits too. The noble daughter and the fugitive — or whatever he was — both needing to understand the space.

He reached the Factorage. The iron sign swung overhead — scales with one pan broken. He pushed through the door without hesitation. A regular. Someone who'd been here before.

She let him go. She had forms to fill. The next child would arrive soon. The carriage was already at the gate.

The door was grey. The walls were grey. The light through the narrow window was grey. The man was gone. The question was still in the air. The stone was still in her chest.

She picked up the next form. Her hands were cold. They were always cold when a child arrived.

The door was grey.

She waited.
