## FID Framework: Alexandria (Silencer, age 15)

### Pillar 1: Baseline Vocabulary

| Source Domain | Examples | Function |
|---|---|---|
| military/tactical | flank, perimeter, threat geometry, exit, sightline, cover, angle | Frames all experience as combat assessment |
| Order hierarchy | assignment, asset, liability, protocol, chain, debrief, leash (others), clean | Frames relationships as command structures |
| weaponcraft | blade weight, draw speed, edge, point, balance | Frames self as instrument |
| architecture/geometry | doorway, window, gap, shadow, choke point, elevation | Frames space as tactical map |

**Syntax — Default pattern:**  
Short, clipped observations. Subject-verb-object. No wasted words. Classifies then moves on.  
*Example: "Door's ajar. Two windows. Cross-breeze. No."*

**Syntax — Pattern under stress:**  
Questions posed to herself as tactical recalculations. Shorter fragments. Third-person self-reference possible.  
*Example: "Where's the second exit? [...] Right. Behind the tapestry. Go."*

**Syntax — Pattern during emotional breakthrough (rare):**  
Run-on sentences. Unexpected sensory details. Then immediate cut-off.  
*Example: "His hands are scarred like mine—no, not like mine, worse, older, and he— ...irrelevant. Focus."*

### Pillar 2: Living Metaphors

| Metaphor | Domain | Function | Self-Aware? |
|---|---|---|---|
| "The body is a weapon" | martial/mechanics | She assesses herself and others by combat utility, never by appearance or feeling | No (she thinks this is objective fact) |
| "Trust is a tactical error" | military/strategy | Makes emotional closeness feel like exposing a flank | Partial (she can articulate the rule but not its origin) |
| "The Order is the only map" | navigation/territory | The hierarchy provides orientation; without it she is lost | No (she hasn't imagined leaving yet) |
| "Worth is measured in clean kills" | accounting/ledger | Internalized scorekeeping; she tracks her value by successful assignments | No (inherited from Silencer training) |

### Pillar 3: Forbidden Words

| Forbidden Word/Concept | FID Strategy |
|---|---|
| "mother" | Avoids entirely. When forced, uses clinical: "the woman who gave birth" or "the foundling-house file." Cannot picture a face. |
| "afraid" | Describes tactical disadvantages: "odds are bad," "exposure too high," "this geometry fails." |
| "lonely" | Observational displacement: "Mess hall's quiet tonight." "No one on the west wall." |
| "beautiful" (emotional, non-tactical) | Translates to functional: "Well-maintained blade." "Clean sightline." Or suppresses: "Not relevant." |
| "want" (genuine desire, not mission need) | Reframes as tactical requirement: "Need eyes on that door." "Requires more data." |

### Pillar 4: Cognitive Refuge

| Level | Refuge | Mechanism | Trigger |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1st | threat geometry | Mentally maps exits, weapons, angles, weight distribution of everyone in the room | Mild stress, unfamiliar environment, any pause in action |
| 2nd | weapon check | Physically or mentally inventories her blade, draw speed, edge condition, balance | Primary fails (no exits, already mapped); social stress |
| 3rd | Orin's voice | Replays a specific instruction he gave her ("Slow is smooth, smooth is fast" or "The ledger doesn't blink") | Threat of overwhelm; after a mistake |
| 4th (optional) | flat tactical assessment | Third-person self-description: "The Silencer waits. The Silencer has no name here." | Catastrophe only (grotto choice, desertion) |

### Pillar 5: Observational Targets

| Order | Target | Function / Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | exits and sightlines | Safety scanning (Silencer training, hypervigilance) |
| 2nd | other people's hands | Assessing threat: empty? reaching? weapon-grip? |
| 3rd | weapon placement (others') | Tactical threat assessment; also comfort (she is always the best-armed in her mental model) |
| 4th | Orin's hands (specific) | Only person whose non-weapon hands she watches — for trembling, for aging, for the lie she suspects he's telling |
| 5th | scars that match her own | Unconscious kinship detection. She doesn't know why she notices frost-scars on strangers' hands. (Hark's hands in grotto trigger her medical response.) |

### Summary — Alexandria

**Who she is:** A 15-year-old Silencer prodigy, raised from infancy in the Order's foundling house. She has no memory of family. Her entire framework for understanding the world is tactical, hierarchical, and weaponized. She is exceptionally competent and profoundly isolated — she just doesn't have the vocabulary to name the isolation.

**What she wants but cannot name:** She wants to be *seen* as more than a weapon, but she doesn't know that's what she wants. She pursues excellence because the Order's approval is the only mirror she has. By the end of Volume 1, she has made her first choice that violates Order protocol (saving Hark, deserting). She does not yet know that the comatose man is her father or that Renne is her mother.

**What she fears:** Being irrelevant. Being discarded. Being returned to the foundling house as a failure. Also — unnamed, unacknowledged — being touched gently.

**How the five pillars interact:** Her military vocabulary (Pillar 1) and living metaphors (Pillar 2) are perfectly aligned with her training. The forbidden words (Pillar 3) cluster around family, fear, and desire — the exact territories she has never been allowed to explore. Her cognitive refuge (Pillar 4) is unusually layered for her age; the third-person collapse in particular suggests pre-existing dissociation. Her observational targets (Pillar 5) are almost entirely threat-based, with one exception: she watches Orin's hands for trembling, the first crack in her purely tactical worldview.

**The likely change across Volume 1:** Alexandria begins as an instrument of the Order. She ends as a deserter — someone who saved a stranger's life (her father, unknown), watched her mother (unknown) from across a boat, and walked away from everything she knew. Her FID voice in Volume 2 should show: weakening of military-first framing, emergence of sensory observations that aren't tactical, and the first tentative approaches to forbidden words.

## FID Framework: Renne "The Seed" (Healer, age 33)

### Pillar 1: Baseline Vocabulary

| Source Domain | Examples | Function |
|---|---|---|
| healing/medicine | suture, fever, pulse, wound, infection, bleed, scar, nerve, bone | Frames problems as injuries to be treated |
| gardening/soil | seed, root, graft, harvest, wither, bloom, fallow, rot | Frames people (especially children) as growing things needing protection |
| debt/ledger (reluctantly) | owed, paid, balance, cost, counterfeit, falsify | Frames her own moral calculus; she hates this domain but uses it |
| water/weather | current, tide, still, storm, flood, erode, drift | Frames emotion and memory |

**Syntax — Default pattern:**  
Observational then immediate care-assessment. Longer sentences than Hark or Alexandria, but not ornate.  
*Example: "Lina's hands are cold again. Too cold for this season. Need to check her pulse before dinner."*

**Syntax — Pattern under stress:**  
Shorter, more imperative. Commands to herself or others. Less metaphor, more direct action.  
*Example: "Pressure on that. No — not there. Here. Hold. Breathe. Again."*

**Syntax — Pattern during emotional breakthrough (rare):**  
Lyrical, almost elegiac. Memory intrudes. Water metaphors surface.  
*Example: "His hands were warm once. Before the frost took them. Before I walked away into rain that felt like it would never stop — and it didn't, not really, not inside."*

### Pillar 2: Living Metaphors

| Metaphor | Domain | Function | Self-Aware? |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Children are seeds that must be hidden from frost" | gardening/protection | Frames her rescue network as husbandry, not revolution; reduces moral weight to something manageable | Partial (she knows she's displacing guilt about Alexandria) |
| "Healing is borrowing from tomorrow" | debt/economics | Every spell has a cost; she pays in chronic hand pain, but also in emotional ledger she can't balance | Yes (she is acutely aware of this) |
| "Love is the wound you don't heal" | medical/trauma | Keeps her from pursuing Hark across 15 years; frames attachment as injury | No (she thinks she's being practical) |
| "The body keeps what the mind forgets" | medical/memory | Her chronic pain is also a memory archive; she touches her scars to remember | Partial (she knows this intellectually but not emotionally) |

### Pillar 3: Forbidden Words

| Forbidden Word/Concept | FID Strategy |
|---|---|
| "daughter" (specific to Alexandria) | Uses "the child" or "the foundling" or avoids reference entirely. Cannot say the word aloud in internal monologue without physical hand pain. |
| "hope" (for herself) | Reframes as "not impossible yet" or "the network still holds" or "Lina's drawing might mean something else." |
| "Hark" (his name, before reunion) | Thinks of him as "the boy from the river" or "the one with frost-hands" or "Damian" (his real name, which she alone knows). After reunion: allows "Hark" but with pain. |
| "deserve" | Cannot articulate her own deservingness. Says instead: "This is what needs doing." Or: "The children didn't ask for this." |
| "stop" (quitting the network) | Doesn't appear in her internal vocabulary. The concept is replaced by "rest" or "regroup" or "find safer ground." |

### Pillar 4: Cognitive Refuge

| Level | Refuge | Mechanism | Trigger |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1st | tending to someone's body (any patient) | Physical exam, wound care, pulse-check. The ritual of healing silences her own fear. | Mild to moderate stress; any time a child is nearby |
| 2nd | hands ritual | Flexes her scarred fingers, touches each scar in sequence. A private accounting of every healing she's ever done. | Primary fails (no patient); when she's alone |
| 3rd | river memory (Hark at 16) | Replays a specific safe scene: Hark teaching her to skip stones, his frost-scarred hands, the moment he almost smiled. | Threat of overwhelm; after evacuating children; before sleep |
| 4th (optional) | withdrawal/functional flatness | Continues to work, speak, decide — but describes herself in third-person or avoids all feeling-words. "The healer finishes her rounds." | Catastrophe only (grotto aftermath, Hark's coma) |

### Pillar 5: Observational Targets

| Order | Target | Function / Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | hands (others') — specifically for scars, trembling, warmth | Medical assessment: Is this person injured? Cold? Afraid? Lying? |
| 2nd | children's breathing (rate, depth, sound) | Caretaking hypervigilance; leftover from losing Alexandria |
| 3rd | exits and hiding places (but differently from Hark) | Not tactical for combat — tactical for evacuation. Where can children hide? Where is the second door? |
| 4th | Hark's hands (after reunion) | Unfinished business. She looks at his frost-scars compulsively. Compares to her healing scars. Does not touch without permission. |
| 5th | small beautiful things (flowers, smooth stones, a clean window) | Private resistance against despair. She collects these mentally but rarely physically. One dried flower on her windowsill for 15 years. |

### Summary — Renne

**Who she is:** A 33-year-old healer with a hidden water mutation and a buried history. She surrendered her newborn daughter to the Order's foundling house at 17, walked away in the rain, and has spent 15 years building a network to save other Sensitive children — as if she could retroactively save the one she couldn't keep. She is exhausted, grieving, and relentlessly competent. She has never healed herself.

**What she wants but cannot name:** She wants to be *forgiven* — by Alexandria (who doesn't know her), by Hark (who doesn't know he has a daughter), and by herself (the hardest). She also wants, in the smallest, most hidden part of her heart, to stop running. To have a door that stays closed. To place the dried flower in a new windowsill and leave it there.

**What she fears:** That Alexandria was better off surrendered. That the Order will find her children. That Hark will wake from his coma and look at her with the same cold threat geometry he gives strangers. That she has no right to any of the love she's too afraid to name.

**How the five pillars interact:** Her healing vocabulary (Pillar 1) and gardening metaphors (Pillar 2) are beautifully matched — she saves lives the way a gardener tends soil, quietly, without applause, accepting the physical cost. The forbidden words (Pillar 3) cluster around her two central traumas: Alexandria (daughter) and Hark (love she couldn't keep). Her cognitive refuges (Pillar 4) are unusually physical — tending patients, touching her own scars, replaying a river memory from 15 years ago. Her observational targets (Pillar 5) are caretaking-first, with a private aperture for small beauty that she allows herself only in glimpses.

**The likely change across Volume 1:** Renne begins as a woman who saves everyone except herself. By the end, she has failed to save her safehouse and her anonymity — but she has gained a comatose Hark (unresolved), a daughter who doesn't know her (unresolved), and a new safehouse where she places the dried flower on a new windowsill. Her arc in Volume 1 is not resolution; it is *relocation* of her impossible choices. In Volume 2, she will have to face what she has avoided for 15 years: Hark awake, Alexandria present, and the word "daughter" forcing its way into her internal monologue.
