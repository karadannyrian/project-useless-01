# PROJECT USELESS-01: HARK & RENNE – FULL BACKSTORY (REVISED)

## Sixteen Years Before Volume 1

### Ages: Hark 15, Renne 16–17

---

## PART ONE: THE DROWNING BOY

### Setting: The Grain‑Lords' Confederacy. A wide, slow river. Late spring.

Hark was not trying to die.

He was trying to wash away the memory of Lea's eyes going dim – those grey eyes like a winter sky, the light draining out of them while the Order's bolt still crackled in her chest. He was trying to forget the instructor's blood on his hands, the way the white beam had punched through flesh like paper. He was trying to silence Joel's last rattle – the mercenary who'd named him, who'd taught him the running joke, who'd died of blood cancer in a copse of trees after less than a month.

He was failing.

The river was cold. Not cold enough.

He knelt in the shallows, just past a bend where willows trailed their fingers in the current. His hands were submerged. The frost‑scars on his forearms – white lines like cracked earth – stood out against his pale skin. His internal reservoir was bleeding. A hairline fracture in the guarding he'd held perfectly for two years. He hadn't noticed.

He wasn't praying. He didn't know how to pray anymore. He was just kneeling.

The water moved around him. He watched it. He didn't watch anything else. That was his way now – narrow focus, reduced world. Hands. Exits. Weapons. Faces only when necessary. Small beautiful things never.

A shadow fell across the ripples.

He didn't hear her approach. That was the first thing about Renne – she moved like water. One moment he was alone; the next, a girl stood at the river's edge, watching him with eyes that had seen too many things for someone still carrying a girl's roundness in her cheeks.

"You're leaking heat," she said.

Her voice was low, quiet – not a challenge, not a question. An observation.

He looked up. She was younger than him, small‑framed, with dark hair pulled back from a face that was sharp and tired. Her hands were wrapped in clean bandages. A Water‑mage – he could feel it now, the way the river hummed around her presence. She'd been pulling water from the current, spiralling it up in thin, invisible columns. Practising.

The frost‑scars on his forearms were visible. His sleeves had ridden up when he fell. He didn't move to cover them. He didn't care.

She didn't flinch. Most people flinched. She just looked at them, then at his face, and said: "Come inside. You'll freeze."

He stared at her.

She extended a hand – not her scarred one, but the other – and waited. No pressure. No pity. Just an open palm, steady as the river.

His chest ached.

That was new. He didn't have a word for it. The word was *trust*. The word was *want*. The word was *please*. He didn't have any of those words. He hadn't spoken in years.

He took her hand.

## PART TWO: THE COTTAGE

### A small cottage a quarter‑mile from the river. One room: hearth, bed, table, root cellar.

Renne's cottage was small but warm. A fire burned in the hearth, even though it was spring – she ran cold, he would learn, the way Water‑mages often did. The walls were lined with drying herbs and shelves of medicine. A hidden trapdoor led to the root cellar, where she kept a bedroll for herself and emergency supplies for the children.

She didn't ask his name. She didn't ask where he came from. She set a pot of water to boil and began pulling bandages from a wooden chest.

"Sit," she said.

He sat.

She knelt in front of him and took his hands. Her fingers were gentle, clinical – she turned his palms over, examined the frost‑scars, the old knife nicks, the healing wound on his forearm where a guard's sword had caught him three weeks ago.

"This is old," she said, tracing the frost‑scars. "Years old. Magic debt?"

He nodded.

"Fire Gate?"

Another nod.

"You don't talk."

He shook his head.

"That's fine," she said. "I talk enough for both of us."

She cleaned the wound on his forearm. Her hands – even through the bandages – were warm. He watched her work. He'd never seen anyone use their hands like this – not for violence, not for survival, but for *care*. The Cradle had taught him that hands were weapons. Joel had taught him that hands were tools. Renne's hands were something else entirely.

She asked no questions. She fed him. She gave him the bed and took the root cellar for herself. She asked nothing in return.

That made him suspicious.

He would not have named it "grateful."

## PART THREE: THE RIVER REMEMBERS

### Days became weeks. Hark didn't leave.

He told himself he was resting. Healing. His body needed it – he'd been running for five years, since the night Lea died. But his body had needed rest before, and he'd never stayed. Never wanted to stay.

He stayed because she didn't push.

She talked. He listened. She told him about the Order – how she'd escaped at fifteen, how she'd been running her network for almost two years already. How she took in Sensitive children, taught them to hide, trained them not to use magic because using magic meant debt and debt meant the Order would find them.

"I don't heal with magic unless I have to," she said one evening, stirring a pot of stew. "Every working leaves a scar." She held up her hands. "These are from healing a broken leg last winter. The child lived. The scars will fade. Not the debt, though. The debt stays."

He looked at her hands. The bandages were off – she trusted him enough for that now. Fine white lines crisscrossed her palms and knuckles, like cracks in a dry riverbed.

"How many?" he asked. His voice was rough from disuse. It was the first word he'd spoken since the river.

She looked up, startled – not by the question, but by the sound of his voice.

"Seventy‑three," she said. "Seventy‑three workings since I escaped. I remember every one."

He had nothing to say to that. He went back to sharpening his knife.

She smiled. It was a small thing, barely a curve of her lips, but it was the first time he'd seen her smile without sadness behind it.

"Seventy‑four," she said. "I'm counting this one." She gestured at him – at his presence, his silence, his stubborn refusal to leave.

His chest ached again.

He looked away. Counted the exits. One door. Two windows. The hearth was not an exit, but the flue might be. He suppressed the thought. It was a reflex, not a fear.

She didn't push.

## PART FOUR: THE LIGHT IN THE DARK

### One month. Then two.

Hark learned things about Renne:

She hummed when she worked. Old Water‑mage lullabies, she said – songs her mother had sung before the Order took her. The melodies were slow and sad and made the air in the cottage cool and damp.

She was afraid of the dark. Not the dark itself – the things that moved in it. The Order's hunters. The blood‑trail hounds. The leashed mages.

She had given up a child. A younger girl, two years ago, a Sensitive she'd tried to save. The Order found them. Renne escaped. The child did not.

"I left her behind," she said one night, staring into the fire. "I tell myself I had no choice. I tell myself I would have died too. But I left her behind."

Hark said nothing. His chest tightened. He touched his knife.

He learned things about himself, too – things he hadn't known because he'd never stayed still long enough to notice.

He dreamed of Lea. Every night. The same dream: her grey eyes, the bolt's yellow light, the silence when the pressure collapsed. He woke with his hand on his knife, the frost‑scars aching, his heart pounding.

He dreamed of his mother, too – her hands, her voice, the day the man in grey came to take him away. *Silk on his wrists because silk was kind.* He woke from those dreams colder than the river.

He didn't tell Renne about the dreams. He didn't tell anyone.

But she knew. She always knew.

One night, she sat beside him on the bed. Not touching – just near. Her shoulder an inch from his. The fire had burned low. The cottage was dark.

"You don't have to talk," she said. "You don't have to tell me anything. But you don't have to be alone, either."

He sat very still.

She leaned against him – just slightly, just enough that he could feel her warmth through his sleeve.

His chest ached. His jaw clenched. His hand went to his knife, then stopped.

He didn't pull away.

They sat like that until the fire died.

## PART FIVE: THE KNIFE AND THE FID

### The third month.

Hark realised he wanted to stay.

Not for a week. Not for a month. *Forever.*

The wanting terrified him.

Everyone he had ever wanted had been taken away. His mother – given to the Order's debt, though he didn't know the details. Lea – killed by the Order's bolt. Joel – dead of cancer in a copse of trees. Even Elias, the Soil Savant he'd met a year ago, was dying slowly, his bones turning to chalk from the soil debt.

Renne would be next. The Order would find her, or his luck would run out, or the fever would come back, or the frost‑debt would claim her hands. And the grief would kill him.

Better not to want.

Better to be a bad contract – written in provisional ink, ready to be voided at any moment.

He went to the river. Alone. He sat on the bank where they'd first met, watching the water move.

*"I will not name fear."*

He said it aloud. His voice was quiet, almost swallowed by the current.

*"I will not let myself want."*

The words felt like stones in his mouth.

*"I will not need anyone."*

He repeated them. Again. Again. He made them a ritual, a prayer without a god. Every time he caught himself feeling something soft, something warm, something that might grow into attachment, he would go to the river and whisper the rules.

He didn't carve anything into his flesh. There were no scar‑mages, no magical prohibitions. He carved the rules into his *mind* – through repetition, through punishment, through sheer force of will.

When he felt fear, he counted exits until his pulse slowed.
When he felt want, he sharpened his knife until his hands bled.
When he felt loneliness, he went for a walk. A long walk. A walk that lasted hours, until his legs burned and his lungs ached and he couldn't feel anything except the road.

He came back to the cottage. He was gone all day. Renne was sitting at the table, her hands wrapped around a cup of tea, her eyes red.

"Where were you?" she asked.

He looked at her. His eyes were different now – colder, emptier. He could feel it. The softness was gone. The wanting was locked away.

"I fixed myself," he said.

She stood. Walked to him. Touched his face – not looking for scars, just looking at *him*.

"You broke yourself," she said. "You fool."

He didn't deny it. He couldn't. The word *"want"* was already forbidden.

That night, she held him. He didn't hold her back. But he didn't pull away.

They lay in the dark, on the narrow bed, her head on his shoulder, his arm rigid at his side.

"You're still here," she whispered.

He didn't answer.

"That's something," she said. "That has to be something."

## PART SIX: THE LAST NIGHT

### The fourth month.

They had four months together. Four months of mornings by the river, afternoons in the cottage, nights in front of the hearth with Hark learning to laugh again – broken, rusty sounds that surprised him every time – and Renne learning to believe that maybe, maybe, she could have something that wasn't just survival.

The last night, a courier arrived.

Renne met her at the door – a woman in a grey cloak, face hidden, voice low. Hark stayed in the shadows, his hand on his knife.

The courier brought news: an Order patrol was three days north. Not hunting Renne specifically – not yet – but sweeping through the Confederacy, scanning for unregistered debt. They would find the cottage. They would find the children in the root cellar. They would find her.

After the courier left, Renne sat at the table. Her hands were trembling.

"I have to move," she said. "The safehouse isn't safe anymore."

Hark nodded.

"You should go," she said. "Your face is known in the Confederacy. If someone sees you with me – "

"I know."

She looked at him. Her eyes were wet.

"This isn't goodbye," she said. "It can't be. I won't let it be."

He wanted to say something. The words were there – *I want to stay. I want to come with you. I want –*

His chest tightened. His jaw clenched. The rules held.

He reached into his pocket. He pulled out a dried flower – one he'd picked from the riverbank, months ago, pressed between the pages of a book she'd lent him. He'd kept it. He'd never told her.

He held it out.

She took it. Her fingers brushed his. Her hands were warm.

"You kept this," she said.

He nodded.

"You kept it for four months."

Another nod.

"You're not allowed to want," she said. "But you kept it anyway."

He didn't answer. He couldn't.

She put the flower in her pocket. She stood. She kissed his cheek – a brief, soft press of her lips against his jaw.

"The river remembers," she said.

Then she was gone.

He stood in the empty cottage for a long time. Then he walked to the river. He knelt in the shallows. He didn't cry. He hadn't cried since Lea died.

He stayed until dawn.

Then he walked south.

He never looked back. He never looked back.

---

## PART SEVEN: THE FOUNDLING HOUSE

### Six months later. Renne, alone.

She didn't know she was pregnant until after Hark left. By then, he was gone – no address, no courier, no way to find him. She wasn't sure she wanted to find him. The grief was hers to carry.

The pregnancy was hard. She was small‑framed, underfed, running on adrenaline and stubbornness. The children in her network needed her. The Order was getting closer. She couldn't slow down.

She slowed down anyway. Her body forced her.

In the eighth month, she stopped travelling. She found a safehouse – a farmer's abandoned barn, far from any road – and she waited. A midwife came. A woman Marta knew, old and kind and sworn to secrecy.

Alexandria was born on a winter night. The barn was cold. Renne held her daughter for three days, singing old Water‑mage lullabies that made the air in the barn cool and damp.

The baby had grey eyes. Hark's eyes.

Renne looked into those eyes and saw the river, the cottage, the boy who couldn't say he wanted to stay.

*"The river remembers,"* she whispered. *"I hope you remember too."*

On the third day, she wrapped Alexandria in clean cloth. She walked three miles to the Order's foundling house. The snow was deep. Her legs were weak. She didn't stop.

She gave her daughter a name – *Alexandria* – and her own name as *"No one."*

She walked back into the snow. She didn't look back.

She found the river – the same river, miles downstream, where the willows still trailed their fingers in the current. She knelt in the shallows. The water was cold. Colder than she remembered.

She pressed her hands to her chest. The healing debt scars burned.

*"I'll find you again,"* she whispered. *"I swear it."*

She stood. She walked back to the network. She didn't stop crying until she reached the next safehouse, where Marta was waiting with a cup of tea and a silence that asked no questions.

## PART EIGHT: THE ROAD SOUTH

### The same winter. Hark, alone.

He didn't know.

He was two hundred miles south, in the Crown of Winter, killing a man for a bag of debt‑null fragments. His hands were steady. His knife was sharp. His chest was empty.

He didn't dream of Renne. The rules forbade it – not magically, but through years of self‑discipline. When her face surfaced in his mind, he counted exits. When her voice echoed in his memory, he sharpened his blade. When his chest ached – and it ached often – he told himself it was the cold. Or the old wound. Or hunger.

Not want. Never want.

He kept the dried flower. He didn't know why. He told himself it was a reminder – of a job completed, a contract fulfilled. He told himself it had no meaning.

He was lying. He knew he was lying. The rules didn't care about lies. The rules only cared about words.

He didn't speak her name. He didn't think her name. He called her *the Seed* in his mind, even after he knew her real name, because *Renne* was too close to *want*.

He walked south. The snow covered his tracks. It always did.

He didn't look back.

He never looked back.

But he kept the flower.

---

## PART NINE: FIFTEEN YEARS LATER

### The windmill. Renne sits beside Hark's comatose body.

She looked at his face. The lines were deeper. His hair was grey at the temples. His hands – the same hands that held hers by the river – were covered in frost‑scars and knife nicks.

She took his hand. His fingers were cold.

"You kept the flower," she said. "Fifteen years. You kept it."

He didn't answer. He couldn't.

She touched his face. No scars. There never were. The FID rules were never carved into his flesh. They were carved into his mind – alone, by the river, by a boy who was too afraid to want.

"You fool," she whispered. "You spent fifteen years running from me. And now you're lying here, and I'm still here, and you can't run anymore."

She looked at the window. Lina's drawing was on the sill – a woman, a girl, a man. All smiling.

She looked back at Hark.

"The river remembers," she said. "Do you remember?"

His breathing didn't change. His eyes didn't open.

But his fingers twitched in hers.

She held on.

She didn't let go.
