Free indirect discourse 5 pillars of Hark (Damian Whiters)

## PILLAR 1: Baseline Vocabulary

| Source Domain | Examples | Function |
|---------------|----------|----------|
| Coin & commerce | "Cost," "worth," "overpriced," "bargain," "settled" | Measures people and actions in transactional terms. Emotional investment = bad debt. |
| Blades & edges | "Sharp," "dull," "edge," "point," "blunt," "nick" | Judges situations by potential to cut or be cut. Clean = safe. Ragged = dangerous. |
| Distance | "Close," "far," "arm's length," "horizon," "reach" | Safety = distance. Danger = proximity. Emotional closeness translated as physical threat. |
| Games & odds | "Fold," "call," "bluff," "stacked," "play," "pass" | Social interaction = rigged game. Best move = not playing. |
| Body (self-deprecating) | "Stringy," "too long," "wrong hand," "barely functional" | His own physical form as a joke at his own expense. Deflection via mockery. |

**Syntax:**
- **Default:** Short declarative. Subject-verb-object. No conjunctions if avoidable. *"Rain. Contract's bad. Going anyway."*
- **Under stress:** Fragments. Single words. Complete withdrawal from syntax. *"...Right. ...Fine. ...No."*
- **During breakthrough (rare):** Run-on. Unexpected clauses. Almost conversational, then cuts off. *"Look, I know it doesn't make sense, I know you think I'm— ...forget it."*

## PILLAR 2: Living Metaphors

| Metaphor | Domain | Function | Self-Aware? |
|-----------|--------|----------|--------------|
| *Life is a bad contract* | Legal/commercial | Explains why everything costs more than it returns. Protects against disappointment. | No. He thinks he's being realistic. |
| *Other people are open wounds* | Medical/body | Physical revulsion toward emotional intimacy. Closeness = infection risk. | Half. Knows he avoids people. Doesn't know why. |
| *His body is a salvage job* | Repair/junk | Self-deprecation as armor. If he calls himself broken first, no one can weaponize it. | Yes, performatively. No, genuinely. |
| *Judgement is distance* | Spatial | The act of assessing someone holds them at arm's length. To stop judging = to let them in. | No. Believes he's just "paying attention." |

## PILLAR 3: Forbidden Words

| Forbidden Word/Concept | FID Strategy |
|------------------------|--------------|
| **Trust** | Will describe the *mechanics* of reliability instead. *"He pays on time. Hasn't run yet. That's something."* Never names the feeling. |
| **Afraid** | Becomes *"not worth the trouble," "bad odds," "exposure,"* or simply silence and a changed subject. |
| **Want** (genuine desire) | Becomes *"wouldn't mind," "could be worse," "had worse."* Desire reduced to absence of objection. |
| **Lonely** | Becomes observational: *"Tavern's loud tonight." "No one at the fire."* External facts that imply the internal state without naming it. |
| **His real name** (if Hark is an alias) | Never appears in interior monologue. Only "Hark" as a label for the version others see. The real name exists in a locked drawer. |

## PILLAR 4: Cognitive Refuge

| Level | Refuge | Mechanism | Trigger |
|-------|--------|-----------|---------|
| **1st** | **Weapon maintenance** | Physical, repetitive, tactile. Counting strokes. Checking edge. The body knows the motions so the mind can stop. | Mild social friction. A question too personal. A long silence. |
| **2nd** | **The running joke** | Internal sardonic commentary on everything happening. Turns pain into material. The voice in his head is a bitter stand-up. | Sustained interaction. Being watched. Someone being earnest at him. |
| **3rd** | **Cold inventory** | A military-style catalog of exits, weapons, cover, threats, and his own physical state. Reduces the world to a threat matrix. | Fear he won't name. A job going wrong. Being cornered. |
| **4th** | **A specific image** (the flower or equivalent) | A single mental image — something small, fragile, and his alone. No narrative attached. Just the picture. Holds it like a stone in the mouth. | Impending collapse. The moment before he'd do something irreversible. Grief. |

## PILLAR 5: Observational Targets (in order of attention)

| Order | Target | Function / Trigger |
|-------|--------|---------------------|
| **1st** | **Hands** (others') | Are they empty? Twitching? Too still? Reaching? Hands predict action. Hark reads them before faces. |
| **2nd** | **Exits** | Doors, windows, alleys, gaps in a crowd. The geometry of leaving. Automatic since childhood. |
| **3rd** | **Weapons & weight** (others and his own) | Where is the knife? The shift of a coat over a holster? Is his own dagger positioned correctly? |
| **4th** | **Faces** (reluctantly) | Eyes specifically. Micro-expressions of contempt or pity (the two he expects). Looks away immediately after reading. |
| **5th** | **Small beautiful things** (denied) | A clean line of light. A coin's wear pattern. A child's unguarded laugh. Noticed, then immediately suppressed as irrelevant. The FID can betray him here. |

## SUMMARY

**Hark** is a Fire Mage(who had escaped the order at 10. Three years after he was taken) and Class C mercenary who reads the world through transaction, edge, and distance — a vocabulary drawn from coin, blades, and bad odds. He lives inside four unconscious metaphors: life as a bad contract, people as open wounds, his own body as salvage, and judgement as a tool of distance. His interior monologue is sharp, self-deprecating, and bitterly funny, but certain words (*trust, afraid, want, lonely, his real name*) are inaccessible; his free indirect discourse must dance around them with mechanics and external facts. When reality presses too hard, he retreats in layers: first to weapon maintenance, then to internal stand-up, then to cold threat inventory, and finally to a single private image he will never explain. He observes hands first, exits second, weapons third, faces fourth, and small beauties fifth — the last against his will. The core tension: a man who believes closeness is a wound and judgement is safety, who has mistaken his armor for his skin, and whose sardonic voice may be the only thing keeping him alive — or the only thing keeping him from living at all.

## Hark (Damian Whiters) — A Backstory 

BEFORE (AGE 0–7) 

He was born in a village too small to have a name that mattered, in the eastern reaches of the Grain-Lords' Confederacy. Flat land, enormous sky, wind that never stopped moving. The soil was black and deep and someone else's. His father was absent before he could remember—gone to a war, or a city, or a woman, his mother never specified. She was a tenant on a Grain-Lord's estate, thin-wristed and quiet, and she loved him with a ferocity she had no other outlet for. 

The heat came early. As an infant he ran warm in her arms, a little furnace against her chest. By the time he could walk, his skin steamed faintly in cold air. The village called it a quirk. His mother called it nothing—she just pressed her palm to his forehead a dozen times a day, frowning, muttering that he was warm but not sick, warm but not sick. The word she was looking for was sensitive. She didn't have it. Neither did he. 

He was seven when the man in grey came to the house. The man spoke to his mother in a voice too low to hear, and she cried, and then the man tied Damian's wrists with white silk and led him out. The neighbours watched from their doorways. No one stopped it. No one ever stopped it. That was the Confederacy—land was power, and power was quiet, and you did not make noise about the Order. 

His mother's hands were the last warm hands he felt. She touched his face before the carriage door closed, her fingers shaking, and said his name—Damian—like it was something she was losing. He held onto that warmth. He would hold onto it for years, through everything, a small bright coal in a chest that would later fill with ash. 

 

THE CRADLE (AGE 7–10) 

The carriage was black-lacquered and the windows were set too high to see out of. The handler across from him had grey sleeves and clean hands that never moved. Damian learned his first lesson in that carriage: hands tell you what's coming. These hands told him nothing. The silk didn't chafe—someone had chosen silk because silk was kind. None of this was kind. 

They took him to the Cradle, the Order's citadel at the continent's exact centre. He was assessed within hours of arrival. A Savant—a thin woman with cold fingers and eyes that seemed to look through his skin—held his hands, turned them over, traced the heat-bleed that radiated from his unguarded reservoir. She nodded once. Someone wrote something in a ledger. Damian understood, in that moment, that his body was not his own. 

He was assigned to a dormitory with other collected children. The training floor was grey stone that drank heat and never gave it back. The instructor was a Fire-mage himself, a man with frost-scars up to his elbows and a voice worn flat by repetition. He called Damian by name—they never took names, names were useful, names let them call you forward to demonstrate, to fail, to be corrected. 

 

Guarding 

For the first year, Damian couldn't guard. His internal reservoir was a furnace with the door missing, heat bleeding out of him constantly. Every mage in the facility could sense him from across the training floor. He burned, and the burning was a beacon, and he couldn't stop it. 

Learning to guard took eleven months. It was not a spell—it was a conscious partitioning of attention, holding his internal element behind a locked door. When it finally clicked, the relief was indescribable. For the first time in his life, he wasn't broadcasting. He was still hot inside, still burning, but the heat was his. He could hide. 

The instructor pushed him relentlessly. Again. Again. You can. Again. Damian's knuckles whitened with frost after every session, the debt writing itself in thin lines across his fingers. He learned that magic was borrowing, and borrowing meant payment, and payment was carved into the body. He learned to read the frost-scars on his own hands the way a farmer reads weather. He learned not to cry. He'd stopped crying in the carriage, when he'd realised the handler's hands weren't going to move no matter what sound he made. 

 

Lea 

She was in the cot next to his. 

Lea was new, collected later, still carrying the flinch of someone who remembered life before the Order and hadn't yet learned to stop missing it. Small, dark-haired, grey eyes like a winter sky. Wind Gate. She talked in her sleep—not words, just little gusts of breath that made the air around her cot stir and shift. She was loud in presence. When she was afraid, the air went tight, pressing against his eardrums. He could feel her fear before he saw her face. She could feel his heat before he spoke. 

They recognised each other the way animals recognised their own species in a foreign habitat. 

"You run hot," she whispered one night, after the instructor's footsteps had faded. 

"You make the air weird." 

"That's not a word." 

"It's what you do." 

Her laugh was small and quickly stifled. They both froze, listening. No one came. 

That was the beginning. They talked most nights after that, whispered exchanges in the dark, tucked between the rhythms of other children's breathing. She told him about her village, the river, the way the wind came down from the hills and made the water shiver. He told her about his mother's hands. He didn't tell her about the handler's hands. He didn't have words for that yet. 

She was the first person he trusted. He wouldn't have named it if asked. He just knew that when Lea was in the cot beside his, the dark was less dark, and the cold in his hands was easier to ignore. 

One night, she showed him. "Give me your hand." 

A small vortex formed in the space between their palms—no bigger than a candle flame, spinning silver and silent. It was precise, controlled, the kind of working that took more finesse than force. A miniature storm, contained in the cup of their fingers. 

"It's beautiful," he said. 

"It's nothing. The instructors want me doing pressure-blades and gust-forcing. Combat aeromancy." Her voice went flat. "I don't want to fight. I want to make the water shiver." 

The vortex guttered. Lea's breath caught. Not the normal catch of exertion—something deeper, wrong. She pulled her hand back and pressed it to her chest, and her breathing went shallow and ragged. The space around her cot went utterly still. Her lips lost colour. Damian sat up, helpless, as she fought for air. 

"The healers say my lung capacity is permanently reduced," she said, when she could speak again. Her voice was hoarse. "Every working shaves a little more off. By the time I'm thirty, I might not be able to run. I'm eleven years old." 

"You shouldn't have shown me." 

"I wanted to. You showed me your frost-scars. I wanted to show you mine." 

He reached across the gap between their cots and took her hand. He held it until her breathing evened out. He held it until she slept. That was the first time he understood she was stronger than him in the ways that mattered. 

 

Light 

The instructor pushed him toward Mutation A. 

"You have the control for it," the man said. They were in a private training chamber, stone walls scorched black from decades of fire. "Most Fire Gates plateau at base state. Bolts and walls. Combat pyromancy. You're refining faster than expected. We'll test for Light." 

Damian was ten. His guarding was solid now. He was also angry—a low, banked fire that had been burning under everything else for as long as he could remember. The Order didn't discourage anger in Fire Gates. Rage raised heat. Heat raised power. He didn't know how to harness it. He just knew it was there, and he fed it to the flame. 

"Strip the heat," the instructor said. "Leave only the light. Your body will pay the thermal debt internally. Fever-heat. It's dangerous if held too long. You'll learn your limit." 

He reached into his reservoir. The flame in his palm flickered, guttered, then changed. The orange bled out. The warmth bled out. What remained was white, pure, so bright it hurt to look at. A narrow beam shot outward, thin as a needle, and scored a line across the stone wall. His skin flushed. Sweat beaded on his forehead. His body was paying the debt differently—not in cold, but in rising heat, a furnace with the door closed. 

"Good. Again." 

He cast again. The fever-heat climbed. His vision swam. The beam was sharper, more focused. 

"Again." 

A third time. His skin steamed in the cold air. The light was so bright it left afterimages burned into his vision. The frost-scars on his knuckles were spreading, threading up toward his wrists, white lines on white skin. He was burning alive from the inside and freezing to death from the outside, and the anger in his chest was a sun. 

"Enough." The instructor's hand closed on his shoulder. "You'll build tolerance. Or you won't. The Light mutation is lethal if overused. Hyperthermia. Organ failure. Some mages survive it. Most don't reach mastery. You have the potential for mastery. Whether you reach it depends on whether you learn your limits before you exceed them." 

That night, Lea looked at his hands. "They're worse." 

"The instructor says I'm advancing fast." 

"The instructor wants you to burn out." The air around her cot was tight, pressure building. "He pushes you harder than anyone. Because you're Fire. Because Fire Gates are weapons and the Order wants weapons. You're not a student to them, Damian. You're an investment." 

He didn't answer. He knew she was right. 

"When I get out," she whispered, "I'm going back to the river. The one near my village. The wind comes down from the hills and makes the water shiver. I'm going to stand there and breathe, and no one's going to tell me to do it again." 

"You're not getting out." 

"I am. And you're coming with me." 

 

The Escape 

They ran on a night with no moon. 

Lea had planned it for weeks. The handler at the east door slept through the third bell—she'd watched, she knew. There was an unmarked door to the delivery yard, a low wall, the forest beyond. She led. She'd learned to guard her breath, and she moved like silence itself. Damian followed, his heat-sight flickering open just enough to read the warm bodies of small animals in the undergrowth and the cooler void where a stream cut through the dark. 

They cleared the wall. The sky was huge and black and full of stars he'd forgotten. He'd forgotten stars. 

They ran until Lea stumbled. 

The bolt took her through the chest. Yellow light, crackling, the smell of ozone and burning cloth. She went down without a sound, her grey eyes wide and surprised and already going dim. Damian heard himself shout her name—a raw, animal sound—and then he was on his knees beside her, his hands pressing against the wound, her blood hot on his fingers. 

The instructor stepped out of the trees. Two handlers flanked him, grey tabards, clean hands. One held a crossbow. The other held a length of white silk. 

"You're predictable," the instructor said. "Both of you. The east door handler has been awake for weeks. We've been waiting for you to try." 

"Lea— Lea, guard. Guard your gate. You have to—" 

She couldn't. Her grey eyes were on his face, fading. Her lips moved. A small gust of air escaped her mouth. Not a word. Just breath. A Wind mage's last working, and it was only breath. 

Her hand found his. Squeezed once. Went still. 

The pressure in the air collapsed. The silence that followed was absolute. 

Damian looked up. The instructor was watching him, patient, unblinking. The handlers stood behind him, their hands still clean. The crossbow was still raised. The silk was still ready. 

"The Light mutation is lethal if overused," the instructor said. "You have the potential for mastery. Let's see if you've learned your limits." 

Damian opened his gate. 

Not from the environment—the night forest was cold, no sun, no hearth. He pulled from himself. Everything. Body heat. Blood-warmth. Metabolic fire. Three years of banked rage, the grief that was already replacing it, the memory of his mother's hands and Lea's voice and the carriage and the silk—he fed it all into the working. 

Light. Not flame. Pure, white, searing light. A cutting beam, thin as a wire, brighter than the stars he'd just remembered existed. He aimed it at the instructor, and the instructor's eyes went wide, and the beam punched through his chest before he could raise a hand. 

The handler with the crossbow fired. The bolt went wide. Damian was already moving, already spinning, the light carving an arc through the dark. The second handler went down. The first dropped the crossbow and ran. 

Damian let him go. 

He stood in the forest, steam rising from his skin, his blood a furnace, his hands shaking with the fever-heat of the Light mutation pushed past every limit he'd been taught. The frost was spreading across his knuckles, his wrists, his forearms—the debt settling, the ledger writing itself in permanent white lines. He was burning alive from the inside and freezing to death from the outside, and Lea was dead on the ground beside him. 

He knelt. Closed her eyes. Her face was still warm. The last of her breath was still stirring the air around him, a faint pressure against his skin, already fading. 

He stayed there until the cold in his hands was too deep to ignore. Then he stood. He walked. The forest swallowed him, and the stars watched him go. 

He did not speak her name. He did not speak at all. 

The boy who had been Damian Whiters left his name beside Lea's body and didn't go back for it. 

 

THE FOREST (AGE 10–15) 

He survived. 

He didn't remember deciding to. Survival was simply what his body did, the same way it had learned to guard: automatically, without conscious instruction, because the alternative was unthinkable and he had stopped thinking. 

The first year was the worst. He was ten years old, alone, carrying a debt so deep his body barely knew how to function. The hyperthermia from the Light overdraw came in waves—fever spikes that left him shaking and vomiting, then plunging chills as the frost-debt reasserted itself. He learned to read the signs. He learned to find water, to build shelter, to hunt small animals with base-state fire he barely had the strength to cast. Every working cost him something. His internal reservoir was shallow, depleted, slow to replenish. He paid in frost-scars and fever-sweat and a bone-deep exhaustion that never fully lifted. 

He didn't speak. Not from a decision—his voice simply stopped. The shout he'd made when Lea fell had been the last sound he produced for years. Words were for people, and there were no people. There was only the forest, and the cold, and the heat inside him, and the silence where her voice used to be. 

 

Concealing the Scars 

The frost-scars were the problem. They marked him as a mage to anyone who knew how to read them—and in the Confederacy, plenty of people knew. The Order had trained physicians, Mage-Auditors, Savants who could read a mage's entire history from a single glance. Even ordinary veterans, soldiers who'd fought beside or against placed mages, recognised the pattern: white lines on the hands, the fingers, spreading up the arms. The ledger in flesh. 

He couldn't hide what he was. But he could hide the evidence. 

Early on, when he was still small enough to pass for younger than he was, he wrapped his hands and forearms in strips of scavenged cloth and pretended his arm was broken. He kept it cradled against his chest, a child with an injury, nothing to see. People looked at the sling and didn't look at his fingers. It wasn't a good disguise—it invited sympathy, questions, offers of help—but he didn't need it to be good. He needed it to be enough to get past a village gate, a market stall, a patrol. 

He stole what he needed. Food, mostly. Blankets, knives, a waterskin. He learned to pick pockets with his off hand while the "broken" one hung useless in its wrappings. He learned to slip through crowds, to read the rhythm of a street, to know which merchants watched their stalls and which didn't. He learned to run. He was always running. 

By the time he was twelve, he'd graduated to gloves. Thin leather, the kind a farmhand might wear, worn soft and stained with use. They covered the scars completely, from knuckles to wrists. He replaced them when they wore through, stole new ones, always the same style—unremarkable, forgettable, the gloves of a thousand other working men. The frost-scars spread further up his forearms over the years, white lines threading toward his elbows. He wore long sleeves. He never rolled them up. He never let anyone see. 

 

The Silence and the Running 

He moved constantly. The Confederacy was vast—open plains, wide rivers, scattered towns separated by miles of farmland and old-growth forest. He crossed it in fragments, never staying anywhere long enough to be remembered. He slept in barns and hedgerows and, when the weather turned, in the attics of inns where no one looked up. He stole coin when he could, worked for it when he had to—fetching water, mucking stables, any task that didn't require a name or a story. 

He didn't give his name. He didn't give any name. People called him "boy" or "you there" or nothing at all, and that was fine. Words were a risk. Words were a door, and he'd learned in the carriage that doors could be locked from the outside. 

He dreamed of Lea. Not every night—not anymore—but often enough. The dream was always the same: her grey eyes, surprised and dimming, her hand squeezing his. The bolt's yellow light. The silence when the pressure collapsed. He woke with his heart hammering and his internal reservoir flaring, heat bleeding out of him before he could clamp down on it. He learned to guard even in his sleep, the door of his furnace held shut by a part of his mind that never rested. 

 

Grief as Survival 

He was trying his best not to feel anything. He failed. 

The grief didn't leave. It settled into his bones the way the frost-debt had, a permanent cold that had nothing to do with temperature. He missed his mother. He missed Lea with a ferocity that made his chest ache. He missed the sound of his own name—Damian—spoken like it mattered. He'd left that name behind, and no one had spoken it since. 

But feelings were dangerous. Feelings made you stupid. Feelings had made him trust Lea, and trusting Lea had got her killed. Feelings had made him stay by her body long enough that the cold nearly took him. If he'd been smarter, colder, faster—if he'd run the moment she fell—maybe he'd have been caught. Maybe he'd have survived easier. He couldn't afford to feel anything. So he shoved it down, packed it tight, buried it under the daily work of staying alive. 

It didn't work. But he kept trying. 

By the time he was fourteen, he was a wiry, silent thing, all sharp angles and watchful eyes. He had a collection of scars that had nothing to do with magic—knife nicks, a burn on his forearm from a cooking fire he'd misjudged, a crooked finger that had healed wrong after a fall. He could steal a purse and vanish before the mark felt the weight change. He could go days without speaking to another human being and prefer it. He could kill small game with a sling, cook it with a flicker of base-state fire, and move on before the smoke cleared. 

He was alive. He didn't know if that was the same as surviving. 

 

JOEL (AGE 15) 

He found the camp by smell—woodsmoke, old blood, a body that hadn't moved in a while. 

It was a small camp, tucked into a copse of trees off a minor trade road in the eastern Confederacy. A single tent, a cold firepit, a bedroll with a man in it. The man wasn't moving. Hark—though he wasn't Hark yet—watched from the treeline for a full hour, reading the stillness, calculating. The man was old, grey-haired, his breathing shallow and irregular. Dying, probably. Good. Dying men didn't fight back. 

He slipped into the camp and started looking for valuables. 

"You've got terrible instincts, kid." 

The voice was gravel and phlegm, but it wasn't weak. Hark spun, a stolen knife already in his hand. The old man was propped on one elbow, watching him with bloodshot eyes and a grin that had more gaps than teeth. He looked like death had already started the paperwork and was just waiting for a signature. 

"First rule of looting," the old man said. "Check if the corpse is actually a corpse. I could've put a bolt in your back." He coughed—a wet, ugly sound—and spat something dark onto the ground. "Relax. I'm not going to. I'm too tired. What's your name?" 

Hark didn't answer. He hadn't answered anyone in five years. 

"Nothing, huh? Fine." The old man's eyes dropped to Hark's gloved hands, then flicked to his face, then back to the gloves. Something shifted in his expression—recognition, maybe, or calculation. "You're a mage." 

Hark's grip on the knife tightened. 

"Don't bother. I'm dying anyway, and I don't work for the Order. Never did." The old man settled back onto his bedroll with a grunt. "I'm Joel. Class B mercenary. Unorthodox, which is a polite way of saying I never went to a real School and I fight dirty. You want to loot my camp, go ahead. There's coin in the pack, dried meat in the satchel, and a half-decent sword I won't be needing. Take it. Just don't take the waterskin. I'm still thirsty." 

Hark should have left. Every instinct told him to run. But he didn't run. The old man was dying, and dying men were safe, and it had been five years since anyone had looked at him and seen a person instead of a threat or a target. 

He didn't take the coin. He sat down by the cold firepit and waited. 

 

The Name 

Joel talked. It was what he did, apparently—had been doing it for decades, to anyone who'd listen, and now the only person around was a mute boy with suspicious gloves and a knife he hadn't sheathed. Joel talked about mercenary contracts gone wrong, about the Schools he'd stolen techniques from, about a woman he'd loved in a port city whose name he couldn't remember anymore. He talked about the blood cancer that was eating him from the inside, slow and painless and utterly uninterested in his opinion on the matter. 

"Got maybe a month," he said. "Maybe less. Body's giving out. Been looking for a place to die, but I keep finding reasons not to. You're a reason, I guess. Someone's got to listen to all this." 

Hark listened. He couldn't have spoken if he'd wanted to—his voice was a locked room, the key lost somewhere in the forest with Lea's body—but he listened. He'd always been good at listening. The Order had trained him to listen, to watch, to read the thousand small signals that preceded violence. Joel was a different kind of signal. Joel was dying, and lonely, and didn't seem to want anything except an audience. 

On the third day, Joel said: "You got a name?" 

Hark didn't answer. 

"Nothing, still. All right." Joel studied him for a long moment, his bloodshot eyes sharp despite everything. "I'm gonna call you Hark." 

Hark tilted his head. 

"It means listen. Old word, I think. Or maybe I made it up. Point is, you don't talk, but you hear everything. You've been sitting there for three days and you haven't missed a word. So that's you. Hark." 

The name landed. It wasn't his name—his name was Damian, and Damian was dead in a forest beside a girl with grey eyes—but it was a name, and he hadn't had one in five years. He nodded once. 

Joel grinned his gap-toothed grin. "Good. Hark it is." 

 

The Lessons 

Joel didn't intend to teach. He was too sick, too tired, too far past the age where he'd cared about passing anything on. But he was a talker, and talking about fighting was what he knew, and Hark was there, and something about the boy's silence made Joel want to fill it. 

He started with swordsmanship. Basic stances, basic cuts, the economy of motion that separated a trained fighter from a brawler. He couldn't demonstrate—he could barely stand—so he talked Hark through it, correcting his form with words alone, patient in a way that surprised them both. 

"Don't grip it so tight. You're holding a sword, not a drowning man. Loose fingers, firm palm. Let the blade do the work." 

"Your feet are too close together. A stiff breeze would put you on your ass. Wider. There. That's your foundation. Everything starts from the ground." 

"Block like that again and I'll haunt you. You're stopping the blade with your arm. That's what the sword is for. Sword takes the hit, you redirect. Stop taking hits meant for the steel." 

Hark learned fast. He'd always learned fast—the Order had said as much, before they'd tried to burn him out. He had good instincts, a low centre of gravity, and five years of survival-honed reflexes. Joel's lessons filled gaps he hadn't known he had: how to commit to a strike, how to read an opponent's weight shift, how to lose a fight without dying. 

Survival skills came next. Joel had spent forty years living out of a pack, and he knew things Hark's feral years hadn't taught him—how to negotiate a contract, how to spot a bounty hunter, how to read a room for threats in the first three seconds after entering. How to hide from mages. 

"They can't track you if you don't use magic," Joel said. "That's the trick. You've got a gate, but you don't have to open it. The Order looks for patterns—mage-debt, environmental anomalies, reports of unexplained fire. No fire, no pattern. No pattern, no hunt. You've been smart about that, from the look of you. Stay smart." 

He didn't ask about the scars. He didn't ask what Hark was running from. He knew—he'd seen enough mage-scars to recognise the ledger—but he didn't push. That was Joel's kindness. He let Hark keep his silence. 

 

The Joke 

Joel joked about dying. It was his way of coping—turning the cancer into material, the same way Hark would later turn everything else. 

"Body's a bad contract," Joel said one evening, staring up at the canvas of his tent. "You put in all this work, all this maintenance, and at the end it just stops paying out. No renegotiation. No extension. Bad terms." 

"I had a good run. Better than most. Killed more people than I should've, saved fewer than I wanted. That's the trade. You try to balance it out, but the numbers don't work. The numbers never work." 

"When I'm gone, take the sword. It's nothing special—plain steel, no name, no history. But it's balanced right and it won't break. A sword that won't break is worth more than a sword with a story." 

Hark absorbed it all. He didn't laugh—he still couldn't laugh, any more than he could speak—but he memorised the jokes. He would need them later. He would build a voice out of them, a way of talking that kept the world at arm's length, a bitter stand-up routine that turned pain into material. Joel was giving him a language for the thing he'd been doing silently for five years. 

 

The Last Advice 

On the final day, Joel was too weak to sit up. His breathing was shallow and wet, and his skin had gone grey, and the grin had faded into something smaller and more honest. 

"All right," he said. "I'm done. You should go. Don't stay for the end. It's not pretty, and you've seen enough ugly things." 

Hark didn't move. 

"Stubborn. Fine." Joel's eyes found his. "One last thing, then. Listen." 

Hark listened. 

"You got out. I don't know how, and I don't want to know—plausible deniability, you understand—but you got out. That means you're one of the lucky ones. Most don't. Most never see the sky after they're collected. You saw the sky. You're still seeing it." 

A cough wracked him. He rode it out, spat, continued. 

"So here's the advice. It's simple. Just survive long enough and never get caught again." 

He closed his eyes. "That's it. Survive. Outlast the bastards. Every day you're still breathing is a day they didn't win. Got it?" 

Hark nodded. It was the only answer he could give. 

"Good." Joel's voice was fading. "Now go. Take the sword. Take the coin. Take the name—it's yours. You're Hark. You listen. That's enough." 

Hark stayed until Joel's breathing stopped. He didn't cry. He'd spent five years not crying, and he wasn't going to start now. But his hands were cold—not from the frost-debt, just cold—and his chest was tight, and the silence in the tent was the same silence that had followed Lea's last breath. 

He was trying his best not to feel anything. He failed. 

He took the sword. He took the coin. He took the name. 

He left Joel's body wrapped in the bedroll, a cairn of stones over the grave, the way Joel had described once in passing—the mercenary's burial, practical and anonymous. He didn't speak over it. He didn't have words yet. 

But he carried the jokes with him when he walked away. And somewhere in the months that followed, alone on the road with Joel's sword on his back, his voice began to come back. Slowly. Sparingly. A word here, a sentence there, always short, always declarative. The silence didn't break. It cracked, just enough to let the minimum through. 

 

THE LONG GAME (AGE 15–25) 

He became a mercenary because it was what Joel had been, and because it was work that didn't ask questions, and because the skills he'd acquired—survival, blades, reading a room, hiding from mages—were the skills a mercenary needed. 

He started small. Caravan guard. Message runner. Muscle for hire in towns too small to have their own law. He was young, wiry, and his silence read as competence rather than damage. The gloves stayed on. The long sleeves stayed down. He used his magic sparingly—a flicker of heat to start a fire, a thread of warmth to stay alive in a blizzard, never more than the minimum. No environmental draws that would register as a debt signature. No fire bolts. No Light. He kept his head down and his gate closed and his ledger clean enough that a casual observer would see nothing. 

He learned to talk again, after a fashion. Short declarative sentences. No conjunctions if he could avoid them. The voice was flat, dry, a little sardonic—Joel's dark humour filtered through years of practice. He made jokes about his own body, his own failures, because if he called himself broken first, no one else could use it against him. He measured everything in transactions: cost, worth, debt. He trusted no one, but he learned to work with people anyway, because solo survival had limits and a crew meant cover. 

He drifted. The Confederacy, then the Dominion for a season, then the Hold-Kingdoms for a winter contract guarding a caravan through the mountain passes. He learned the terrain of each realm, the local customs, the local dangers. He learned which cities had Order branches and which didn't, and he never entered the ones that did unless he had no choice. 

He never went looking for the Order. He never tried to find out what had happened after his escape—whether the handler who'd fled had reported him, whether they'd identified the bodies, whether there was a file somewhere with the name Damian Whiters and a note that said escaped, dangerous, Light mutation. He assumed the worst and acted accordingly. The Order was always behind him, somewhere, a shadow he couldn't shake. The trick wasn't to outrun it. The trick was to never be interesting enough to catch its attention. 

 

Class C 

He never climbed higher than Class C. Not because he lacked skill—he was better than his rating, sharper than half the B-class mercenaries he'd worked with—but because rating up required reputation, and reputation required visibility, and visibility was the one thing he couldn't afford. 

Class C was a sweet spot. Respected enough to get work, not prominent enough to be remembered. The contracts were dirty but paid in coin rather than glory, and coin was what mattered. He built a quiet career out of the jobs no one else wanted: escort duty through bandit country, recovery of stolen goods, the occasional bounty on someone who'd skipped a debt in a town too small to have its own enforcers. He was reliable. He was professional. He didn't cause problems. When a job was done, he took his pay and disappeared. 

The other mercenaries called him strange but harmless. The quiet one. The one with the gloves. He deflected questions with jokes and silences and the occasional hard look that discouraged follow-up. No one knew he was a mage. No one knew he'd been collected. No one knew his name had once been Damian, or that he'd killed two Order men with light and grief, or that he still dreamed of a girl with grey eyes and woke with his chest aching and his reservoir flaring. 

He was Hark. He listened. He survived. That was enough.

The Merc (AGE 25, THE SOUTH) 

He's in the Shattered Archipelago now—the Maelstrom League, the realm of a thousand islands and no single ruler. He's been here for three years, and it's the longest he's stayed anywhere since the forest. 

He ended up in the South almost by accident. A contract had gone sour in the Hold-Kingdoms—a merchant who'd tried to pay in mineral scrip instead of coin, a local lord who'd taken the merchant's side, a border Hark had decided to cross rather than argue. The first ship out of the nearest port was bound for the Archipelago. He'd taken it. He'd stayed. 

The town is called Salthaven, a port city of middling size and no particular importance, built on a limestone spine that juts into a shallow bay. It's the kind of place the Order's attention slides past—no strategic value, no magical demographic worth monitoring, no noble houses with Sensitive children to hide. The League's naval academy is three hundred miles north. The nearest Order branch is a two-day sail away on a different island. He checked. He always checks. 

He lives in a rented room above a ropewalk, the long shed where fibres are twisted into rigging for the fishing fleet. The smell of hemp and tar comes through the floorboards. The room is small, bare, functional—a cot, a lockbox, a window that faces the harbour. He pays by the week. He's never paid by the month anywhere in his life. A month is a commitment. A week is a bet you can walk away from. 

 

The Factorage 

He takes contracts through the Factorage, a mercenary guild that operates out of a stone building at the edge of town. The sign is iron, not wood—a set of scales with one pan broken off, leaving the other dangling useless from its chain. He'd always assumed the broken scale was an accident of weather. After three years, he suspects it's the most honest advertising a guild has ever hung. 

The clerk is a thin man named Verrol. Hark appreciates Verrol in a way he's never articulated and never will. The man offers the same flat, transactional neutrality to everyone who walks through the door. No curiosity. no pity, no grudging reassessment. He counts ogre ears with the tip of his pen and doesn't flinch at the smell. His hands are clean, steady, a clerk's hands. The safest kind. 

The guildmaster is a man named Graen, who eats walnuts at his desk and always, always tries to short on payment. Graen is petty in a way Hark almost respects—not malicious, not cunning, just a man who's never met a margin he wouldn't shave. Their negotiations are a ritual now. Graen offers less than the contract is worth. Hark holds silence until Graen cracks. They settle somewhere between the lie and the truth. Hark always gets his number. Graen always pretends he's won. 

The relationship is stable. That's rare. Hark doesn't trust the Factorage—he doesn't trust anyone—but he trusts the pattern. Contracts come in. He completes them. Coin changes hands. Verrol logs everything in the ledger. Graen tries to short him. The ritual repeats. Three years of this, and no one has asked about the gloves or the long sleeves or the fact that he sometimes comes back from jobs with a chill on his skin that has nothing to do with the weather. 

 

The Work 

The jobs are Class C standard: pest control, cargo recovery, escort duty through the island chains, the occasional bounty on someone who's skipped a debt in a port too small to have its own enforcers. The pay is adequate. The risk is manageable. He's better than his rating—sharper than half the B-class mercenaries he's worked beside—but rating up would mean reputation, and reputation means visibility, and visibility is the one thing he can't afford. 

So he stays at C. Reliable. Professional. The quiet one with the gloves. When a job is done, he takes his pay and disappears into the ropewalk room until the next contract. 

He uses magic sparingly. A flicker of heat to light a fuse or dry out wet gear. A thread of warmth in his own chest on cold nights, paid for in numb fingers by morning. Never more than the minimum when there are witnesses. When there are no witnesses—when a job goes wrong and the terrain is used up and his body is used up and the only thing left in his inventory is the thing he doesn't use—he lets the gate open.
