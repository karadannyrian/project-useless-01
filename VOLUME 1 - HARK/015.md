# Chapter 15: The Delivery

The house was small and grey and leaned toward the sea like it was thinking about jumping in. Salt-scoured shutters. A roof patched with tarred canvas. The path from the village was mud, churned by hooves and feet, and the sky was threatening rain again. Hark stood at the door with the package in his hand and the contract in his pocket and the cold in his fingers deeper than it had been that morning.

One door. One window. The window was painted shut. He noted it. Noted the gap between the house and the next—narrow, dark, a dead end if the door was blocked. Noted the path behind him, the village below, the ferry at the dock. The geometry of leaving. Old habit.

He knocked.

The woman who opened the door was old. Not Marta-old—older. Her hair was white, pulled back tight against her skull, and her face was a map of everything that had happened to her. Deep lines at the eyes. Deeper lines at the mouth. Her hands were callused, the knuckles swollen, the fingers crooked from years of hauling rope or pulling nets. Former sailor. Maybe fisher. No weapons that he could see. No rings. No blade at the belt. Just the hands, shaking with a tremor that was age, not fear.

She looked at the package. Looked at Hark's face. Looked at his gloves. Her eyes were pale and watery and saw more than they let on.

"You're not the usual courier."

"The usual is dead."

He didn't know if this was true. It sounded true. He said it anyway. The woman's expression didn't change. She reached out and took the package from his hands—her fingers brushing his gloves, the leather warm from his body heat—and turned it over. The wax seal was still intact. She broke it with her thumb. The sound was small and final.

She read. The paper was thin, covered in writing too small to read from a distance. Hark didn't try. He watched her face instead—the way her eyes moved across the lines, the way her mouth tightened, the way her hands kept shaking. The tremor didn't worsen. That was something. If the news was a threat to her personally, she wasn't showing it.

Her face changed. Not fear. Not surprise. Something harder. Something that looked like the moment before a storm.

"You'd better come in."

The inside of the house was as grey as the outside. A single room. A hearth with a low fire. A table with two chairs. Nets hung on the walls, half-mended, the twine old and frayed. The smell of salt and old wood and something medicinal—liniment, maybe, or herbs steeping in a pot. He catalogued as he entered. The window from the inside—still painted shut. The back door—closed, barred. The hearth—no poker, but a heavy iron pot that could be swung. The woman—still no weapons visible, but the table was between them, and the distance was wrong if she moved suddenly.

She gestured to a chair. He didn't sit. She didn't seem to care.

"You're the Fire-mage," she said. "The one who's been asking questions."

He didn't deny it. Denial was noise. Denial told them more than silence.

"The questions stop now. The answers are above your pay grade."

"What's the message."

She looked at him. The paper was still in her hand, the writing still hidden. Her eyes were pale and steady and full of something he recognized. The look of someone who'd been carrying bad news for so long it had become part of her bones.

"The Order is coming. The cell needs to move."

His chest went cold. Not the frost-debt. Something else.

*Door. Window. Table—two paces, maybe three. Her hands—shaking, empty. The back door—barred. The nets on the walls—no threat. His own knife, still in the sheath. Sword at the ferry. Too far.*

*Renne. Corin. Marta. The girl with the shaking fingers. The cottage. The clearing.*

*Bad odds.*

The splinter was a door. The door was open. Something was coming through it.

Moss on north-facing stone. Vivid green against grey.

The image surfaced—unbidden, unwelcome—and he held it. The stone was wet with mist. The moss was soft and thick and alive in a way that had nothing to do with fire or frost or the ledger in his flesh. Small. Fragile. His alone.

One breath. Two. The panic receded—not gone, never gone, but far enough. The moss faded. The grey room came back. The woman was still watching him.

"Which cell."

She didn't answer. She didn't need to. The contract was in his pocket. The address was on the paper. The package had been delivered to this woman, and this woman was reading a message from someone who didn't want to be named, and the message said *the Order is coming*, and the cell that needed to move was the one he'd been visiting. The one with the clearing and the cottage and the girl who was learning to shape water. The one with Corin's fidgeting hands and Marta's steady spoon and Renne's grey eyes watching him from across the fire.

"The one you've been visiting," she said. Her voice was flat. Not cruel. Just tired.

He said nothing. His hands were cold. His chest was cold. The word surfaced—a word he hadn't used in fifteen years, a word that had been locked behind the door with the handler's clean hands and Lea's grey eyes and the carriage window set too high to see out of. He didn't say it. The word was a liability. The word was a door he couldn't afford to open.

He stood at the table with his hands in his pockets and the cold spreading through his ribs and the inventory still running, and the inventory said *leave now, run now, this is not your fight*, and the inventory was right, and he didn't move.

The carriage had come when he was seven.

Grey sleeves. Clean hands. White silk around his wrists that didn't chafe. The handler sat across from him, his hands folded on his lap, still and pale, and Damian Whiters—seven years old, his body a signal fire he didn't know how to put out—had watched those hands and learned his first lesson: hands tell you what's coming. These hands told him nothing. The carriage jolted. The windows were set too high to see out of. His mother had cried. Her hands had been the last warm hands he'd felt—shaking, pressing against his face, pulling away as the door closed. He hadn't understood why, then. He'd understood that something bad was happening. He'd understood that the silk meant he couldn't move his arms. He hadn't understood that the Order always came.

He understood now.

The Order came for children. They came with silk and Savants and clean hands that never moved. They came for Sensitives whose gates were unguarded and whose bodies broadcast their presence like signal fires. They came for escapees who'd run and run and never stopped running. They came for anyone who borrowed without permission, who taught without authority, who built something outside the Order's walls.

They came, and they took, and they left silence behind.

He had been running from them for fifteen years. Through the forest, through the Confederacy, through a dozen towns and a hundred contracts and the long slow grind of staying alive when everyone who'd ever cared about him was dead. He had never stopped. He had never been fast enough. There was no fast enough. The Order was patient. The Order was everywhere. The Order waited, and watched, and when the pattern emerged, they closed their hands and squeezed.

"How long."

The old woman folded the paper. Tucked it into her sleeve. "Days. Maybe a week. The message didn't say. It said *soon*. It said *be ready*."

"Who sent it."

"Someone inside. Someone who still has access to the Order's communications. Someone who's been feeding us information for years." She looked at him. "That's more than I should have told you. It's more than you need to know."

"Need to know is a flexible concept."

"Not with the Order it isn't." She stood up. Her knees cracked. Her hands were still shaking, the tremor steady and slow. "You delivered the package. The contract is closed. Take your coin and go home."

"Don't have a home."

"Then go wherever you go. This isn't your fight."

He didn't answer. The words were there—*it is now, it became my fight the moment I saw her hands, it became my fight fifteen years ago when the carriage came and I didn't know why my mother was crying*—but they were locked behind the door, and the door was still closed, and he didn't know how to open it.

He left the house. The grey sky was still threatening rain. The path was still mud. He walked back to the village, to the dock, to the ferry that would take him across the strait. The package was delivered. The contract was closed. The coin was in his pouch—silver, too much silver for a courier job, and now he knew why. Danger pay. The sender had known what the message said. The sender had known what it was worth.

The ferryman was silent. The dog was asleep in the bow. The crossing was calm, the sea flat and grey, the sky threatening rain that still hadn't fallen. Hark stood at the rail and watched the water.

He should run. The smart play. Fifteen years of running, and running had kept him alive. The Order was coming. The Order would sweep the cell—Renne, Corin, Marta, the girl with the shaking fingers, anyone who'd helped them. Hunters. Cradle-hounds. Savants. Silencers who walked through fire without flinching. They'd leave nothing behind. They never did.

Head north. The Hold-Kingdoms. The Confederacy. Disappear into the map the way he'd always disappeared.

The way he was good at.

He thought of Renne's hands. The vortex spinning between her palms, silver and silent. The way she'd held out her hand and said *try*. The way the water had shivered when his fingers hovered over it—not from magic, just from heat, the ordinary warmth of a body that was still alive.

*Invitation.* She'd said *invitation* instead of *again*.

He thought of Marta's hands, steady on the spoon. Corin's hands, fidgeting at his sides. The girl's hands, shaking as she reached for the rain barrel. All of them running. All of them building something the Order would destroy.

Salthaven came into view—the harbour, the fish market, the iron sign of the Factorage swinging in the wind off the water. He didn't get off.

The ferryman looked at him. "You getting off or staying on?"

Hark looked at the dock. The ropewalk room. The salt-crusted window. The lock he checked twice every night. The life he'd built—small, cold, safe. A cage, but his cage. Stitch by stitch.

Then the open water. The islands. The cottage with the thatched roof. The woman who'd said *from you, that's the same thing*.

"Staying on."

"Where to?"

"Back. The same island. The one you just came from."

The ferryman shrugged. The dog lifted its head, looked at Hark, put its head back down. The ferry turned. The water churned. Salthaven receded behind them, and the islands opened ahead, and Hark stood at the rail with his hands in his pockets and the coin unspent in his pouch.

He was heading back. The odds were bad. The contract was worse. The door in his chest was open, and something was coming through it, and he didn't know what was on the other side.

That was tomorrow's problem. The cold in his fingers was today's. He flexed them. The leather creaked. The ferry crossed the strait, and the islands grew closer, and Hark didn't run.

He didn't know why. He didn't name it. But the ferry was moving, and he was on it, and that was something.
