# Chapter 2: The Broken Scale

The Factorage smelled of ink and old wool and the particular dust that gathers on ledgers no one opens. Hark pushed through the door and the iron sign swung overhead—scales with one pan broken, the symbol of a guild that weighed everything and promised nothing. Morning light came through the grimy windows in slabs, cutting the room into stripes of grey and gold. He stepped through them without thinking.

Verrol was already at his desk.

Thin man. Clerk's hands—clean, steady, no calluses. Eyes that didn't quite meet yours but saw everything anyway. He logged contracts with the tip of his pen and didn't flinch at the smell of blood. Hark had seen him count ogre ears without blinking, stacking them on the scale like coins. The most dangerous person in the building. Not because he carried a weapon. Because he never forgot a face.

They exchanged the same greeting they'd exchanged three hundred times.

Nod.

Verrol pushed the contract across the desk. The paper was cheap, the ink still wet in places. Hark didn't read it. Not yet. He watched Verrol's hands instead—the way they returned to the ledger, the way the pen fitted into the groove between finger and thumb, the way the knuckles moved under the skin. Clean hands. Pale. Motionless when they weren't writing.

The handler in the carriage had clean hands.

Hark remembered them vividly. Pale, folded on grey sleeves. The carriage was black-lacquered and the windows were set too high to see out of. He was seven. The silk was still tight around his wrists—white silk, clean silk, silk that didn't chafe. The handler sat across from him and those hands never moved. Not when Hark cried. Not when he begged. Not when he stopped doing either and just sat there, small and hot and already learning that sound was a currency that bought nothing.

Clean hands were the most dangerous kind. They meant the person had never had to dirty them. They meant someone else did the dirty work. The instructor's hands had been clean too, until the end. Until Hark had put light through his chest and the frost had started its slow crawl up his own arms. Then the instructor's hands had been red, and Hark's had been white, and the ledger had balanced itself the way it always did.

He blinked. Verrol's hands were still writing. The pen scratched. The moment passed.

He picked up the contract. Standard terms. Payment on completion. No advance. The guild took its cut off the top—twelve percent, the broken scale in practice—and the remainder was his, minus expenses, minus the cost of the ink on the page if Verrol was feeling petty. The job description was vague. *Pest control. Ogres. Details from the guildmaster.* Graen liked to deliver the bad news himself. It was the only part of the job he still did.

Hark signed.

The pen scratched. His gloved hand left no prints on the paper. He liked that. A signature that wasn't his name—*Hark* in block letters, the same mark he'd used for ten years—and no evidence of the hand that made it. The gloves were good for that. The gloves were good for a lot of things. He pushed the contract back across the desk and Verrol took it without looking up.

"Haven't seen you in three weeks."

"Been working."

"Ogre job in the pass. Caravan escort before that. Before that—" Verrol's pen paused. "—nothing on the books. You were gone."

"Personal."

"Personal." The pen resumed. "You don't take personal time."

"Took some."

Verrol's eyes flicked up. A fraction of a second. Then back to the ledger. The clerk never pushed. That was the arrangement. Hark brought in contracts and Verrol logged them and neither of them asked questions that mattered. Three hundred times they'd done this dance. Three hundred times Verrol had looked at him the same way—like he was filing a face alongside a name, cross-referencing, building a dossier in the back of his skull. The most dangerous person in the building.

"Graen wants to see you."

"I know."

"He's in a mood."

"He's always in a mood."

"This one's worse." Verrol blotted the ledger and closed it. The cover was worn at the corners, the leather cracked like old skin. "Something about the contract. He'll tell you himself."

"Generous of him."

"He's not generous. That's the problem."

Hark stood. The chair scraped against the floorboards. He left the contract on the desk—Verrol would file it, log it, remember it—and crossed the room toward Graen's door. The floor was littered with walnut shells, crushed into the boards by a hundred boots. Graen ate walnuts constantly, cracked them with his thumbs, left the shells where they fell. The crunch underfoot was a warning. You couldn't approach the guildmaster quietly. He'd designed it that way.

The door was already opening.

"Hark." Graen's voice was oil and gravel. "Get in here."

The guildmaster's office was a monument to clutter. Papers stacked on every surface. A scale on the desk that actually worked. A window that looked out on the harbour but was too dirty to see through. Graen himself was built like a barrel—wide, solid, the kind of body that had been strong once and was now just heavy. His hands were not clean. Ink-stained. Walnut-dusted. The hands of a man who did his own dirty work, once, and still remembered how.

He waved Hark in and didn't wait for him to sit.

"Got something for you. Pest control. Ogres."

Hark said nothing. Waited for the number.

"Three of them. Moving down from the foothills. Settled in a cave system about a day's walk north. Been hitting the trade road. Caravans. Couriers. The last group lost two guards and a mule." Graen cracked a walnut. The shell splintered. "Low risk. Ogres are stupid. You've handled worse."

"What's the pay?"

Graen told him.

The number was wrong. Not wrong by a little. Wrong by half. Three ogres, cave system, a day's walk north—the risk was low but the work was real, and the pay didn't cover the work. Hark stood there and let the number hang in the air between them. He didn't argue. Arguing was what Graen wanted. Arguing meant you cared. Arguing meant you'd settle for something in the middle.

"That's light."

"Budgets are tight. Caravans are down. The pass was closed for three weeks after that rockslide—you were there, you know. Revenue's thin." Another walnut. Another crack. "It's what I can offer."

"It's not what the job's worth."

"Maybe not. But it's what's on the table."

The ritual. Graen offered too little. Hark named the gap. They went back and forth—not negotiating, not really, because Graen had already decided what he'd pay and Hark had already decided whether he'd take it. The back-and-forth was just form. The contract was already signed. The pen had already scratched. The ledger was already updated in Verrol's neat hand, and the clerk was already filing Hark's face alongside the number, waiting to see if he'd walk.

He didn't walk.

"Fine."

"Fine?"

"Fine." Hark picked a walnut shell off the desk. Turned it over in his gloved fingers. The shell was sharp at the edges, brittle in the centre. Like everything else in this building. "But if there's more than three, the price goes up."

"Standard clause. Verrol's already written it in."

"Then we're done."

"We're done." Graen swept the shell fragments off his desk and onto the floor. They joined the others. The crunch was the same. "Be careful out there. Ogres are stupid, but stupid kills as often as clever."

Hark was already at the door. He didn't look back. Graen's voice followed him out, oil and gravel, and then the door closed and the voice was gone and the Factorage was quiet again.

Verrol was still at his desk. Still writing. His hands moved across the ledger in the same rhythm, the same precision, the same clean economy of motion. Hark passed him without a word. The nod this time was Verrol's—a slight tilt of the head, nothing more. Three hundred and one.

The iron sign swung as the door closed behind him. Scales with one pan broken. Morning light cutting the street into stripes. The harbour smell was stronger now—the tide was turning, the fish market opening, the gulls screaming their idiot hunger. Hark walked east, toward the gate, toward the foothills, toward three ogres and a price that was already wrong.

The cold in his fingers had settled into something familiar. The day was underway. The contract was signed. The ledger was still writing itself in white lines under his gloves, and the debt, as always, would come due later.
